Introduction to Oscillations

Oscillations are periodic motions that repeat themselves in equal intervals of time. They are fundamental to understanding waves, sound, light, and many physical systems.

Periodic Motion: Motion that repeats itself at regular time intervals.

Oscillatory Motion: To-and-fro motion about a mean position.

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM): The simplest type of oscillatory motion where restoring force is directly proportional to displacement and directed towards mean position.

Simple Harmonic Motion

F ∝ -x, a ∝ -x. Sinusoidal motion described by amplitude, frequency, phase.

x = A sin(ωt + φ)
a = -ω²x
Simple Pendulum

For small angles: T = 2π√(L/g). Independent of mass, depends on length and g.

T = 2π√(L/g)
ω = √(g/L)
Spring-Mass System

T = 2π√(m/k). Series/parallel combinations, effective spring constant.

T = 2π√(m/k)
ω = √(k/m)

Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

SHM is characterized by acceleration that is directly proportional to displacement and always directed towards the mean position.

Condition for SHM: F = -kx or a = -ω²x, where ω² = k/m

a = -ω²x

The negative sign indicates that acceleration is opposite to displacement (directed towards mean position).

SHM Equations

General Form x = A sin(ωt + φ)
or x = A cos(ωt + φ)		 v = Aω cos(ωt + φ)
or v = -Aω sin(ωt + φ)		 a = -Aω² sin(ωt + φ)

Velocity from Displacement - v = ±ω√(A² - x²) -

Zero Values 0 (at mean position) 0 (at extremes) 0 (at mean position)

Key Characteristics of SHM:

  • Periodic: Repeats after fixed time interval T
  • Oscillatory: To-and-fro motion about mean position
  • Sinusoidal: Displacement varies sinusoidally with time
  • Isochronous: Time period independent of amplitude (for small oscillations)
  • Energy Conservation: Total mechanical energy remains constant
  • Phase: Position and direction of motion at given instant

Phase Relationships

Displacement vs Velocity

Velocity leads displacement by π/2 (90°)

When x = 0 (mean), v = ±Aω (maximum)

When x = ±A (extremes), v = 0

Phase difference: Δφ = π/2

v is derivative of x: v = dx/dt

Velocity vs Acceleration

Acceleration leads velocity by π/2 (90°)

Acceleration in phase with displacement but opposite direction

Phase difference: Δφ = π/2

a is derivative of v: a = dv/dt

When v = 0, a = ±Aω² (maximum)

Displacement vs Acceleration

Acceleration opposite to displacement (180° out of phase)

a = -ω²x (always directed towards mean)

Phase difference: Δφ = π

When x = +A, a = -Aω²

When x = -A, a = +Aω²

SHM as Projection of Circular Motion

SHM can be represented as projection of uniform circular motion:

x = A cos(ωt + φ) (projection on x-axis)
y = A sin(ωt + φ) (projection on y-axis)

Reference circle: Radius = A, angular speed = ω

Phase angle: φ = initial angular position

Phasor diagram: Rotating vector representation

Useful for adding SHMs, analyzing phase differences

Example: SHM Parameters

A particle executes SHM with amplitude 10 cm and time period 2 s. Find maximum velocity and acceleration.

Solution:

A = 10 cm = 0.1 m, T = 2 s

ω = 2π/T = 2π/2 = π rad/s

v_max = Aω = 0.1 × π = 0.314 m/s

a_max = Aω² = 0.1 × π² = 0.986 m/s²

Maximum velocity occurs at mean position, maximum acceleration at extremes.

Simple Pendulum

A simple pendulum consists of a point mass suspended by a massless, inextensible string from a rigid support.

Assumptions for Simple Pendulum:

  • Bob is point mass
  • String is massless and inextensible
  • Motion is in vertical plane
  • Amplitude is small (θ < 10°)
  • No air resistance or friction

Derivation of Time Period

For small angles (θ < 10°): sinθ ≈ θ (in radians)

Restoring force: F = -mg sinθ ≈ -mgθ

But θ = x/L, so F = -(mg/L)x

Comparing with F = -kx: k = mg/L

Time period: T = 2π√(m/k) = 2π√(m/(mg/L))

T = 2π√(L/g)

Angular frequency: ω = √(g/L)

Pendulum Characteristics

Property Simple Pendulum Physical Pendulum Torsional Pendulum

Time Period T = 2π√(L/g) T = 2π√(I/mgd) T = 2π√(I/C)

Important Points:

  • Isochronous: Time period independent of amplitude (for small angles)
  • Independent of mass: T depends only on L and g
  • Second's pendulum: T = 2 s, L = g/π² ≈ 0.994 m ≈ 1 m
  • Effective g: In elevator, satellite, etc. changes T
  • Large amplitudes: T increases with amplitude, correction: T = T₀[1 + θ²/16 + ...]
  • g measurement: g = 4π²L/T² (precise method)

Special Cases & Applications

Conical Pendulum

Bob moves in horizontal circle

String traces cone

T = 2π√(L cosθ/g)

θ = angle with vertical

Period depends on θ

Used in amusement rides

Spring Pendulum

Mass attached to vertical spring

Two restoring forces: spring + gravity

T = 2π√(m/k)

Same as horizontal spring

Gravity changes equilibrium position only

Effective spring constant

Pendulum in Accelerating Frame

Elevator, vehicle, etc.

Effective g' = g ± a

T' = 2π√(L/g')

Upward acceleration: g' = g + a

Downward acceleration: g' = g - a

Free fall: g' = 0, T → ∞ (no oscillation)

NEET IMPORTANT

Graphical Method for Pendulum Problems

L vs T² graph: Straight line through origin

T² = (4π²/g)L

Slope = 4π²/g

From slope, g = 4π²/slope

Length correction: For accurate L, measure from point of suspension to center of mass

Large amplitude correction: T = T₀(1 + θ²/16) where θ in radians

Example: Pendulum in Elevator

A simple pendulum has period 2 s on Earth's surface. Find its period in an elevator accelerating upward at 2 m/s². (g = 10 m/s²)

Solution:

Effective acceleration: g' = g + a = 10 + 2 = 12 m/s²

T = 2π√(L/g) ⇒ L = gT²/(4π²) = 10×4/(4π²) = 10/π² m

New period: T' = 2π√(L/g') = 2π√[(10/π²)/12] = 2π√(10/(12π²))

= 2π × √(10)/(π√12) = 2√(10/12) = 2√(5/6) ≈ 1.83 s

Period decreases when effective g increases.

Spring-Mass Systems

Spring-mass systems are common examples of SHM where the restoring force is provided by Hooke's law.

Hooke's Law: F = -kx, where k is spring constant (stiffness) and x is displacement from equilibrium.

T = 2π√(m/k) and ω = √(k/m)

For vertical spring: equilibrium position shifts but time period unchanged.

Spring Combinations

Combination Effective Spring Constant (k_eff) Time Period Energy Distribution Applications
Series 1/k_eff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂ + ... T = 2π√(m/k_eff) Same force, different extensions Softer suspension, weighing machines
Parallel k_eff = k₁ + k₂ + ... T = 2π√(m/k_eff) Same extension, different forces Stiffer suspension, vehicle springs
Two masses, one spring k_eff = k (same) T = 2π√(μ/k)
μ = m₁m₂/(m₁+m₂)		 Reduced mass system Diatomic molecule vibration
Cut spring k' = nk (n parts) T' = T/√n Stiffer when shorter Spring design

Spring Constant (k):

  • Measure of stiffness: larger k = stiffer spring
  • SI unit: N/m
  • For helical spring: k = (Gd⁴)/(8nD³) where G = modulus of rigidity, d = wire diameter, D = coil diameter, n = number of turns
  • k ∝ 1/L (for same material and cross-section)
  • Series: k_eff < smallest individual k
  • Parallel: k_eff > largest individual k

Special Spring Systems

Vertical Spring

Mass hangs from vertical spring

Equilibrium: mg = kx₀

Extension x₀ = mg/k

Time period same as horizontal: T = 2π√(m/k)

Oscillation about new equilibrium

Energy includes gravitational PE

Two Springs (Same Side)

Mass between two springs

If both connected to same wall: parallel

If connected to different walls: series

Effective k = k₁ + k₂ (parallel)

T = 2π√(m/(k₁+k₂))

Common in vehicle suspensions

Springs in Opposite Directions

Mass connected to springs on both sides

Both springs in tension/compression

Effective k = k₁ + k₂

Equilibrium where forces balance

Used in vibration isolation systems

Center of oscillation shifts

Reduced Mass Concept

For two masses m₁ and m₂ connected by spring:

μ = m₁m₂/(m₁ + m₂) (reduced mass)
T = 2π√(μ/k)

Special cases:

  • m₁ = m₂ = m: μ = m/2, T = 2π√(m/2k)
  • m₂ → ∞ (fixed wall): μ = m₁, T = 2π√(m₁/k)
  • m₁ = m₂ = M: Center of mass remains fixed

Application: Diatomic molecule vibration, coupled oscillators

Example: Spring Combinations

Two springs with k₁ = 100 N/m and k₂ = 200 N/m. Find time period for 1 kg mass when connected: (a) in series, (b) in parallel.

Solution:

(a) Series: 1/k_eff = 1/100 + 1/200 = 3/200 ⇒ k_eff = 200/3 ≈ 66.67 N/m

T = 2π√(m/k_eff) = 2π√(1/66.67) = 2π√(0.015) = 2π × 0.122 = 0.767 s

(b) Parallel: k_eff = 100 + 200 = 300 N/m

T = 2π√(1/300) = 2π√(0.00333) = 2π × 0.0577 = 0.362 s

Parallel combination gives stiffer system → shorter period.

Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

In SHM, energy continuously converts between kinetic and potential forms, but total mechanical energy remains constant.

Total Energy (E): Sum of kinetic energy (KE) and potential energy (PE). For conservative systems, E remains constant.

E = KE + PE = constant

For spring-mass system: PE = (1/2)kx², KE = (1/2)mv²

Energy Expressions in SHM

Position Displacement (x) Velocity (v) Kinetic Energy (KE) Potential Energy (PE) Total Energy (E)
Mean Position (x=0) 0 ±Aω (max) (1/2)mω²A² (max) 0 (min) (1/2)mω²A²
Extreme Position (x=±A) ±A 0 0 (min) (1/2)mω²A² (max) (1/2)mω²A²
Any Position (x) A sin(ωt+φ) Aω cos(ωt+φ) (1/2)mω²(A²-x²) (1/2)mω²x² (1/2)mω²A²

Energy Relations:

E = KE + PE = (1/2)mω²A² = (1/2)kA²
KE = (1/2)mω²(A² - x²) = E - PE
PE = (1/2)mω²x² = (1/2)kx²

Average Values over one period:

〈KE〉 = 〈PE〉 = E/2 = (1/4)mω²A²
〈Total Energy〉 = E = (1/2)mω²A²

Average KE = Average PE = Half of total energy

Energy Distribution Graphs

KE vs Displacement

KE = (1/2)mω²(A² - x²)

Parabolic curve (inverted)

Maximum at x=0 (mean)

Zero at x=±A (extremes)

Symmetrical about mean

KE + PE = constant (straight line)

PE vs Displacement

PE = (1/2)mω²x²

Parabolic curve (upright)

Minimum at x=0 (mean)

Maximum at x=±A (extremes)

Symmetrical about mean

Always positive (or zero)

Energy vs Time

KE = (1/2)mω²A² cos²(ωt+φ)

PE = (1/2)mω²A² sin²(ωt+φ)

Both vary sinusoidally

Frequency = 2ω (twice SHM frequency)

KE and PE out of phase

Sum = constant straight line

NEET IMPORTANT

Energy Method for SHM

Alternative method to derive SHM equation:

  1. Write total energy: E = KE + PE = constant
  2. Differentiate with respect to time: dE/dt = 0
  3. For spring: E = (1/2)mv² + (1/2)kx²
  4. dE/dt = mv(dv/dt) + kx(dx/dt) = 0
  5. Since v = dx/dt and a = dv/dt: mva + kxv = 0
  6. Divide by v: ma + kx = 0 ⇒ a = -(k/m)x
  7. Thus ω² = k/m, SHM established

Useful for complex systems where force analysis is difficult

Example: Energy Calculation

A 0.5 kg mass executes SHM with amplitude 0.1 m and frequency 2 Hz. Find total energy and KE when displacement is 0.05 m.

Solution:

ω = 2πf = 2π×2 = 4π rad/s

Total energy: E = (1/2)mω²A² = (1/2)×0.5×(4π)²×(0.1)²

= 0.5×0.5×16π²×0.01 = 0.5×0.5×1.579 = 0.395 J

KE at x = 0.05 m: KE = (1/2)mω²(A² - x²)

= (1/2)×0.5×(4π)²×(0.1² - 0.05²)

= 0.5×0.5×16π²×(0.01 - 0.0025) = 0.5×0.5×16π²×0.0075

= 0.5×0.5×1.184 = 0.296 J

At x = A/2, KE = 3/4 of total energy.

Damped & Forced Oscillations

Real oscillators experience damping due to resistive forces. When driven by periodic force, resonance can occur.

Damped Oscillations: Oscillations where amplitude decreases with time due to resistive forces (friction, air resistance).

Forced Oscillations: Oscillations driven by external periodic force.

Resonance: Large amplitude response when driving frequency matches natural frequency.

Types of Damping

Type Condition Amplitude Decay Equation of Motion Examples
Underdamped (Light) b < 2√(mk) A(t) = A₀e^(-bt/2m) Oscillatory x = A₀e^(-γt) sin(ω't+φ)
ω' = √(ω₀²-γ²)		 Pendulum in air, spring in water
Critically Damped b = 2√(mk) Fastest return to equilibrium without oscillation x = (A + Bt)e^(-γt) Car suspension, door closers
Overdamped (Heavy) b > 2√(mk) Slow return to equilibrium without oscillation x = A₁e^(r₁t) + A₂e^(r₂t)
r₁,r₂ real & negative		 Pendulum in thick oil
Undamped b = 0 Constant amplitude x = A sin(ω₀t+φ) Ideal SHM (theoretical)

Damping Parameters:

  • Damping constant (b): Force = -bv (proportional to velocity)
  • Damping coefficient (γ): γ = b/2m
  • Natural frequency: ω₀ = √(k/m)
  • Damped frequency: ω' = √(ω₀² - γ²)
  • Quality factor (Q): Q = ω₀/2γ = measure of sharpness of resonance
  • Logarithmic decrement: Λ = ln(A₁/A₂) = γT' (measure of damping)

Resonance Phenomena

Forced Oscillations

Equation: m(d²x/dt²) + b(dx/dt) + kx = F₀ cos(ωt)

Steady state solution: x = A cos(ωt - δ)

A = F₀/√[(k-mω²)² + (bω)²]

Phase lag: tanδ = bω/(k-mω²)

Amplitude depends on ω

Resonance

Maximum amplitude when ω = ω_r

ω_r = √(ω₀² - 2γ²) (damped)

For light damping: ω_r ≈ ω₀

Resonant amplitude: A_max = F₀/(bω₀)

Sharpness: Δω = 2γ (bandwidth)

Q = ω₀/Δω = ω₀/(2γ)

Applications & Examples

Useful: Tuning circuits, MRI, musical instruments

Destructive: Tacoma Narrows bridge, mechanical failures

Everyday: Swing pushing, microwave heating

Biological: Hearing (cochlear resonance)

Atomic: Electron transitions, NMR

JEE ADVANCED

Sharpness of Resonance

Quality factor (Q): Measure of resonance sharpness

Q = ω₀/Δω = ω₀/(2γ) = √(mk)/b

Where Δω = bandwidth at A_max/√2

High Q: Sharp resonance, low damping (crystal oscillators, Q ~ 10⁴-10⁶)

Low Q: Broad resonance, high damping (shock absorbers, Q ~ 1-10)

Power absorption: P(ω) = (F₀²ω²b)/[(k-mω²)² + (bω)²]

Maximum power at ω = ω₀, P_max = F₀²/(2b)

Example: Damped Oscillation

A damped oscillator has mass 0.2 kg, k = 80 N/m, damping constant b = 2 kg/s. Find if it's underdamped, and its damped frequency.

Solution:

Natural frequency: ω₀ = √(k/m) = √(80/0.2) = √400 = 20 rad/s

Critical damping: b_c = 2√(mk) = 2√(0.2×80) = 2√16 = 8 kg/s

Since b = 2 < b_c = 8, system is underdamped

Damping coefficient: γ = b/2m = 2/(2×0.2) = 5 rad/s

Damped frequency: ω' = √(ω₀² - γ²) = √(400 - 25) = √375 = 19.36 rad/s

Frequency slightly reduced from natural frequency due to damping.

Problem Solving Strategies

Systematic approach to solve oscillation problems in JEE and NEET.

Type 1: SHM Parameters

Steps:

  1. Identify given quantities: A, T, ω, x, v, a
  2. Use relations: ω = 2π/T, v_max = Aω, a_max = Aω²
  3. For specific position: v = ω√(A²-x²), a = -ω²x
  4. Check phase relationships
  5. Use appropriate trigonometric form

Tip: v leads x by 90°, a leads v by 90°, a opposite to x

Type 2: Time Period Calculation

Steps:

  1. Find restoring force F or torque τ
  2. Check if F ∝ -x or τ ∝ -θ
  3. Write in form: a = -ω²x or α = -ω²θ
  4. Extract ω, then T = 2π/ω
  5. For pendulum: T = 2π√(L/g)

Tip: For small oscillations, sinθ ≈ θ, cosθ ≈ 1 - θ²/2

Type 3: Energy Problems

Steps:

  1. Total energy: E = (1/2)kA² = (1/2)mω²A²
  2. At any x: KE = E - PE, PE = (1/2)kx²
  3. Average values: 〈KE〉 = 〈PE〉 = E/2
  4. For fraction of energy: use ratios
  5. Energy method to derive ω

Tip: When KE = PE, x = A/√2, v = Aω/√2

JEE/NEET TIP

Common Problem Patterns

1. Time period in different situations: Elevator, liquid, on inclined plane

2. Spring combinations: Series/parallel, cut springs

3. Phase problems: Initial conditions, phase difference

4. Two-body oscillations: Reduced mass concept

5. Energy conservation: Max speed, positions where KE=PE

6. Pendulum variations: Conical, physical, torsional

7. Damping: Amplitude decay, logarithmic decrement

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • SHM: a ∝ -x, motion is sinusoidal with constant amplitude (undamped)
  • Simple pendulum: T = 2π√(L/g), independent of mass, valid for small θ (<10°)
  • Spring-mass: T = 2π√(m/k), ω = √(k/m), independent of gravity direction
  • Energy in SHM: Total energy constant, KE and PE interconvert, average values equal
  • Phase: v leads x by 90°, a leads v by 90°, a opposite to x (180° out of phase)
  • Damping: Amplitude decays exponentially, underdamped systems oscillate with reduced frequency
  • Resonance: Maximum amplitude when driving frequency matches natural frequency
  • Physical pendulum: T = 2π√(I/mgd), reduces to simple pendulum for point mass
  • Spring combinations: Series - softer, parallel - stiffer

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Applying pendulum formula for large amplitudes without correction
  • Confusing angular frequency (ω) with frequency (f): ω = 2πf
  • Forgetting that spring period in vertical orientation same as horizontal
  • Mixing up series and parallel spring combinations
  • Not converting angles to radians in calculations
  • Assuming velocity is maximum at extreme positions (it's zero!)
  • Confusing underdamped, critically damped, and overdamped motions
  • Forgetting phase constant (φ) in displacement equation
  • Applying reduced mass formula incorrectly for two-body systems
