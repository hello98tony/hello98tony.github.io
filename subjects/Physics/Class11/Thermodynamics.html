Introduction to Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is the branch of physics that deals with heat, work, temperature, and their relation to energy, radiation, and physical properties of matter.

System: The part of the universe under study. Can be open, closed, or isolated.

Surroundings: Everything outside the system that can exchange energy with it.

Boundary: The surface separating system from surroundings.

First Law

Energy conservation: ΔU = Q - W. Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed.

ΔU = Q - W
Q = ΔU + W
Second Law

Entropy always increases. Heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold. Efficiency limits.

η ≤ 1 - T₂/T₁
ΔS ≥ Q/T
Processes

Isothermal, adiabatic, isobaric, isochoric. Different PV diagrams, different work calculations.

W = ∫PdV
PV = constant

First Law of Thermodynamics

The first law is essentially the principle of conservation of energy applied to thermodynamic systems.

Mathematical Statement: The change in internal energy of a system equals the heat added to the system minus the work done by the system.

ΔU = Q - W

Where:
ΔU = Change in internal energy
Q = Heat added to system
W = Work done by system

Sign Convention

Quantity Positive Sign Negative Sign
Heat (Q) Added to system Released by system
Work (W) Done by system Done on system
ΔU Increases Decreases

Key Concepts:

  • Internal Energy (U): Sum of all microscopic energies (kinetic + potential) of molecules
  • For ideal gas: U depends only on temperature (U = f(T) only)
  • Heat (Q): Energy transfer due to temperature difference
  • Work (W): Energy transfer by mechanical means, W = ∫PdV
  • Path dependence: Q and W depend on path, ΔU depends only on initial and final states

Applications of First Law

Cyclic Process

System returns to initial state

ΔU = 0 (since U is state function)

Q_net = W_net

Heat absorbed = Work done

PV diagram forms closed loop

Area inside loop = Net work

Free Expansion

Gas expands into vacuum

No external pressure: W = 0

No heat exchange: Q = 0

ΔU = 0

For ideal gas: ΔT = 0 (isothermal)

Irreversible process

Enthalpy (H)

Useful for constant pressure processes

H = U + PV

At constant pressure: ΔH = Q_p

For chemical reactions, phase changes

For ideal gas: H = U + nRT

Work Calculation in Different Processes

General: W = ∫PdV

Constant pressure: W = PΔV

Constant volume: W = 0

Isothermal (ideal gas): W = nRT ln(V₂/V₁) = nRT ln(P₁/P₂)

Adiabatic (ideal gas): W = (P₁V₁ - P₂V₂)/(γ-1) = nR(T₁ - T₂)/(γ-1)

For monatomic gas: γ = 5/3, for diatomic: γ = 7/5

Example: First Law Application

A gas absorbs 200 J of heat and expands, doing 150 J of work. What is change in internal energy?

Solution:

Q = +200 J (heat absorbed, positive)
W = +150 J (work done by gas, positive)

ΔU = Q - W = 200 - 150 = 50 J

Internal energy increases by 50 J

The remaining 150 J of heat energy is converted to work.

Second Law of Thermodynamics

The second law introduces the concept of entropy and establishes the direction of spontaneous processes.

Kelvin-Planck Statement: It is impossible to construct a heat engine that operates in a cycle and converts all heat absorbed from a hot reservoir into work without rejecting some heat to a cold reservoir.

Clausius Statement: Heat cannot spontaneously flow from a colder body to a hotter body without external work being done.

Different Statements - Same Meaning

Statement By Formulation Implication
Kelvin-Planck No 100% efficient heat engine η < 100%, must reject heat
Clausius Heat flows hot → cold naturally Refrigerators need work input
Carnot η ≤ 1 - T₂/T₁ Maximum efficiency depends on temperatures
Entropy ΔS_universe ≥ 0 Total entropy never decreases

Carnot's Theorem:

  • No engine operating between two reservoirs can be more efficient than a Carnot engine
  • All reversible engines operating between same reservoirs have same efficiency
    • η_max = 1 - T₂/T₁
  • Where T₁ and T₂ are absolute temperatures of hot and cold reservoirs
  • Practical implication: Higher T₁, lower T₂ → higher efficiency

Heat Engines vs Refrigerators

Heat Engine

Converts heat to work

η = W/Q₁ = 1 - Q₂/Q₁

Q₁ = Heat from hot reservoir

Q₂ = Heat to cold reservoir

W = Q₁ - Q₂

Efficiency η < 1

Refrigerator

Pumps heat from cold to hot

K = Q₂/W = Q₂/(Q₁ - Q₂)

Q₂ = Heat from cold reservoir

Q₁ = Heat to hot reservoir

W = Q₁ - Q₂ (work input)

Coefficient of performance K > 0

Heat Pump

Heats room by extracting heat from outside

K' = Q₁/W = Q₁/(Q₁ - Q₂)

Similar to refrigerator but different purpose

K' = 1 + K (always > 1)

More efficient than electric heater

NEET IMPORTANT

Practical Efficiency Limits

Steam engine: T₁ ≈ 550K, T₂ ≈ 300K

η_max = 1 - 300/550 = 1 - 0.545 = 0.455 = 45.5%

Actual efficiency: About 30-35% (due to friction, heat losses)

Internal combustion: T₁ ≈ 2500K, T₂ ≈ 300K

η_max = 1 - 300/2500 = 1 - 0.12 = 0.88 = 88%

Actual: About 25-30% (gasoline), 40% (diesel)

Example: Carnot Efficiency

A Carnot engine operates between 500K and 300K. If it absorbs 1000J from hot reservoir, find work output and heat rejected.

Solution:

Efficiency: η = 1 - T₂/T₁ = 1 - 300/500 = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4 = 40%

Work output: W = η × Q₁ = 0.4 × 1000 = 400J

Heat rejected: Q₂ = Q₁ - W = 1000 - 400 = 600J

Or: Q₂/Q₁ = T₂/T₁ = 300/500 = 0.6, so Q₂ = 0.6 × 1000 = 600J

40% efficiency means 400J work from 1000J heat input.

Thermodynamic Processes

A thermodynamic process is the path of transformation between equilibrium states of a system.

Reversible Process: Infinitely slow process where system remains in equilibrium throughout. Can be reversed without leaving any trace on surroundings.

Irreversible Process: Rapid process with finite differences, cannot be reversed without changing surroundings.

Specific Heats for Ideal Gas:

C_v = (f/2)R, C_p = C_v + R, γ = C_p/C_v = 1 + 2/f

Where f = degrees of freedom

  • Monatomic (He, Ne, Ar): f = 3, C_v = (3/2)R, C_p = (5/2)R, γ = 5/3 ≈ 1.67
  • Diatomic (H₂, N₂, O₂): f = 5, C_v = (5/2)R, C_p = (7/2)R, γ = 7/5 = 1.4
  • Polyatomic (CO₂, CH₄): f = 6, C_v = 3R, C_p = 4R, γ = 4/3 ≈ 1.33

Work in PV Diagrams

Work Calculation

Work = Area under PV curve

W = ∫PdV

Expansion: W > 0 (system does work)

Compression: W < 0 (work done on system)

Clockwise cycle: Net work done by system

Counterclockwise: Net work done on system

Slopes in PV Diagram

Isothermal: P ∝ 1/V, hyperbolic curve

Adiabatic: P ∝ 1/V^γ, steeper than isothermal

Isobaric: Horizontal line (P constant)

Isochoric: Vertical line (V constant)

Adiabatic slope = γ × Isothermal slope

Polytropic Process

General process: PV^n = constant

n = 0: Isobaric (P constant)

n = 1: Isothermal (T constant)

n = γ: Adiabatic (Q = 0)

n = ∞: Isochoric (V constant)

Work: W = (P₁V₁ - P₂V₂)/(n-1)

JEE ADVANCED

Adiabatic Relations

For adiabatic process (Q = 0) with ideal gas:

PV^γ = constant
TV^(γ-1) = constant
P^(1-γ)T^γ = constant

Where γ = C_p/C_v

Temperature change: T₂/T₁ = (V₁/V₂)^(γ-1) = (P₂/P₁)^((γ-1)/γ)

During adiabatic expansion: T decreases, during compression: T increases

Example: Adiabatic Compression

One mole of diatomic gas (γ = 1.4) at 300K is compressed adiabatically to half its volume. Find final temperature.

Solution:

Adiabatic relation: TV^(γ-1) = constant

T₁V₁^(γ-1) = T₂V₂^(γ-1)

T₂ = T₁(V₁/V₂)^(γ-1) = 300 × (2)^(0.4)

2^0.4 = e^(0.4 ln2) = e^(0.4×0.693) = e^0.277 = 1.319

T₂ = 300 × 1.319 = 395.7K

Temperature increases from 300K to 396K during adiabatic compression.

Heat Engines & Refrigerators

Devices that convert between heat and work based on thermodynamic principles.

Carnot Engine: Ideal reversible heat engine operating between two reservoirs. Consists of two isothermal and two adiabatic processes. Maximum possible efficiency.

Carnot Cycle (Ideal Gas)

Process Type Heat Exchange Work Done ΔU
1→2: Isothermal expansion Reversible, T₁ constant Q₁ = nRT₁ ln(V₂/V₁) > 0 W₁ = Q₁ > 0 0
2→3: Adiabatic expansion Reversible, Q = 0 0 W₂ = nC_v(T₁ - T₂) > 0 -W₂
3→4: Isothermal compression Reversible, T₂ constant Q₂ = nRT₂ ln(V₄/V₃) < 0 W₃ = Q₂ < 0 0
4→1: Adiabatic compression Reversible, Q = 0 0 W₄ = nC_v(T₂ - T₁) < 0 -W₄
Cycle Reversible Q_net = Q₁ + Q₂ = W W_net = Q₁ - |Q₂| 0

Efficiency Formulas:

η = W/Q₁ = (Q₁ - Q₂)/Q₁ = 1 - Q₂/Q₁
For Carnot: η_max = 1 - T₂/T₁
For any engine: η ≤ 1 - T₂/T₁

Coefficient of Performance:

Refrigerator: K = Q₂/W = Q₂/(Q₁ - Q₂) = T₂/(T₁ - T₂)
Heat pump: K' = Q₁/W = Q₁/(Q₁ - Q₂) = T₁/(T₁ - T₂)

Real World Applications

Internal Combustion

Otto cycle (gasoline):

1-2: Adiabatic compression

2-3: Isochoric heat addition

3-4: Adiabatic expansion

4-1: Isochoric heat rejection

η_otto = 1 - 1/r^(γ-1)
Diesel Engine

Diesel cycle:

1-2: Adiabatic compression

2-3: Isobaric heat addition

3-4: Adiabatic expansion

4-1: Isochoric heat rejection

Higher compression ratio than Otto

Higher efficiency

Steam Engine

Rankine cycle:

Boiler → Turbine → Condenser → Pump

Phase changes: liquid ↔ vapor

Power plants, steam turbines

Lower temperature limit: ~300K

Practical efficiency ~35%

NEET IMPORTANT

Refrigerator Working

Components: Compressor, condenser, expansion valve, evaporator

Working fluid: Refrigerant (Freon, ammonia)

Process:

  1. Compressor: Adiabatic compression (P↑, T↑)
  2. Condenser: Isobaric heat rejection (gas → liquid)
  3. Expansion valve: Adiabatic throttling (P↓, T↓)
  4. Evaporator: Isobaric heat absorption (liquid → gas)

K = Q_cold/W_input = T_cold/(T_hot - T_cold)

Example: Refrigerator Performance

A refrigerator maintains -3°C inside while room is 27°C. If it removes 1000J from cold chamber, find minimum work input.

Solution:

T_cold = -3 + 273 = 270K

T_hot = 27 + 273 = 300K

For ideal (Carnot) refrigerator:

K_max = T_cold/(T_hot - T_cold) = 270/(300-270) = 270/30 = 9

K = Q_cold/W ⇒ W_min = Q_cold/K_max = 1000/9 = 111.1J

Minimum 111J work needed to remove 1000J heat from -3°C to 27°C.

Entropy & Statistical Thermodynamics

Entropy measures disorder or randomness in a system. It gives thermodynamic processes a direction.

Entropy (S): A measure of the number of specific ways a thermodynamic system may be arranged. It increases in irreversible processes and remains constant in reversible ones.

ΔS = ∫(dQ_rev/T)

For finite reversible process: ΔS = Q_rev/T

Entropy Changes in Different Processes

Process Entropy Change (ΔS) Explanation
Reversible adiabatic 0 No heat exchange, reversible
Reversible isothermal Q/T Heat transfer at constant T
Ideal gas (V,T change) nC_v ln(T₂/T₁) + nR ln(V₂/V₁) From definition integration
Ideal gas (P,T change) nC_p ln(T₂/T₁) - nR ln(P₂/P₁) Alternative form
Phase change at constant T,P mL/T L = latent heat
Mixing of ideal gases -nR∑x_i ln(x_i) x_i = mole fraction

Second Law in Terms of Entropy:

  • For any process: ΔS_universe ≥ 0
  • Reversible process: ΔS_universe = 0
  • Irreversible process: ΔS_universe > 0
  • Isolated system: ΔS ≥ 0 (entropy never decreases)
  • Entropy is a state function - depends only on initial and final states
  • Entropy of universe always increases (arrow of time)

Statistical Interpretation

Boltzmann's Formula

Microscopic definition of entropy:

S = k ln W

Where:
k = Boltzmann constant (1.38×10⁻²³ J/K)
W = Number of microstates
ln = natural logarithm

Entropy ∝ logarithm of possible arrangements

Third Law of Thermodynamics

As temperature approaches absolute zero, entropy approaches a minimum constant value.

lim_(T→0) S = 0 (for perfect crystal)

At T = 0K, perfect crystal has only one microstate (W=1)

S = k ln 1 = 0

Provides absolute entropy scale

Implies 0K is unattainable

Maxwell's Demon

Thought experiment: A demon could violate second law

Opens door for fast molecules to one side

Creates temperature difference without work

Resolution: Demon's measurement increases entropy

Information has thermodynamic cost

Landauer's principle: Erasing bit produces heat

JEE ADVANCED

Entropy of Mixing

For mixing of ideal gases at constant T,P:

ΔS_mix = -nR(x_A ln x_A + x_B ln x_B)

Where x_A, x_B are mole fractions

For equal amounts: n_A = n_B = n/2

ΔS_mix = nR ln 2

Entropy increases when gases mix (irreversible process)

Gibbs paradox: Entropy of mixing independent of gas similarity

Example: Entropy Change in Melting

Calculate entropy change when 1kg ice at 0°C melts to water at 0°C. L_f = 3.34×10⁵ J/kg.

Solution:

Melting occurs at constant T = 0°C = 273K

Heat absorbed: Q = mL_f = 1 × 3.34×10⁵ = 3.34×10⁵ J

ΔS = Q/T = 3.34×10⁵ / 273 = 1223 J/K

Positive ΔS: More disorder in liquid than solid

Water molecules have more freedom in liquid state.

Problem Solving Strategies

Systematic approach to solve thermodynamics problems in JEE and NEET.

Type 1: First Law Problems

Steps:

  1. Identify: Q (heat), W (work), ΔU (internal energy)
  2. Apply sign convention correctly
  3. Use: ΔU = Q - W
  4. For ideal gas: ΔU = nC_vΔT
  5. For cyclic process: ΔU = 0, Q_net = W_net

Tip: Remember Q and W are path-dependent, ΔU is state function

Type 2: Process Problems

Steps:

  1. Identify process type (isothermal, adiabatic, etc.)
  2. Use appropriate equation of state
  3. Calculate work using correct formula
  4. Find ΔU using ΔU = nC_vΔT
  5. Use first law to find Q

Tip: Adiabatic: Q=0, Isothermal: ΔT=0, Isobaric: ΔP=0, Isochoric: ΔV=0

Type 3: Engine/Refrigerator

Steps:

  1. Identify Q_hot, Q_cold, W
  2. For engine: η = W/Q_hot = 1 - Q_cold/Q_hot
  3. For refrigerator: K = Q_cold/W
  4. For Carnot: η_max = 1 - T_cold/T_hot
  5. Check if process is reversible

Tip: Temperatures must be in Kelvin for efficiency formulas

JEE/NEET TIP

Common Problem Patterns

1. Cyclic processes: ΔU = 0, net work = area inside PV loop

2. Adiabatic processes: Use PV^γ = constant, TV^(γ-1) = constant

3. Mixing of gases: Final temperature from heat balance, entropy increase

4. Polytropic processes: PV^n = constant, work = (P₁V₁-P₂V₂)/(n-1)

5. Phase changes: Include latent heat, temperature constant during change

6. Free expansion: W=0, Q=0, ΔU=0, for ideal gas: ΔT=0

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • First law: Energy conservation, ΔU = Q - W with proper sign convention
  • Second law: Efficiency limits, entropy always increases in natural processes
  • Carnot engine: Maximum possible efficiency η_max = 1 - T₂/T₁
  • Work in PV diagram: Area under curve, clockwise cycle = work done by system
  • Internal energy of ideal gas depends only on temperature: U = (f/2)nRT
  • Adiabatic process: Q = 0, PV^γ = constant, steeper than isothermal on PV diagram
  • Entropy: Measure of disorder, ΔS_universe ≥ 0 for all processes
  • Heat engine converts heat to work, refrigerator pumps heat from cold to hot

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Wrong sign convention for Q and W in first law
  • Using Celsius instead of Kelvin in efficiency formulas
  • Confusing adiabatic (Q=0) with isothermal (ΔT=0)
  • Forgetting that γ differs for monatomic/diatomic gases
  • Applying ideal gas equation to phase changes
  • Mixing up work formulas for different processes
  • Assuming 100% efficiency is possible for real engines
  • Confusing entropy with energy (entropy measured in J/K, not J)
