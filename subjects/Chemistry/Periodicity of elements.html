Introduction to Periodic Table

The Periodic Table is a systematic arrangement of chemical elements in order of increasing atomic number, revealing periodic trends in their properties.

Periodic Law: The properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers. When elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic number, elements with similar properties recur at regular intervals.

Alkali Metals

Group 1 elements: Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr. Highly reactive metals with one valence electron.

ns¹ configuration
Transition Metals

Groups 3-12: Elements with partially filled d orbitals. Form colored compounds.

(n-1)d¹⁻¹⁰ ns¹⁻²
Halogens

Group 17: F, Cl, Br, I, At. Highly reactive nonmetals needing one electron.

ns² np⁵ configuration

Historical Development

The periodic table evolved through several stages as our understanding of elements and their properties grew.

Historical Periodic Tables Timeline

Year Scientist Contribution Basis Limitations
1817 Döbereiner Law of Triads Atomic weight Only a few triads
1864 Newlands Law of Octaves Atomic weight Failed beyond calcium
1869 Mendeleev First periodic table Atomic weight Position anomalies
1913 Moseley Modern periodic law Atomic number None significant
1945 Seaborg Actinide concept Electronic config None significant

Mendeleev's Periodic Table (1869):

  • Arranged elements in order of increasing atomic weights
  • Left gaps for undiscovered elements (predicted properties)
  • Grouped elements with similar properties in vertical columns
  • Corrected atomic weights of some elements
  • Predicted existence of eka-aluminium (Ga), eka-boron (Sc), eka-silicon (Ge)

Key Achievements and Limitations

Mendeleev's Predictions

Predicted: Gallium (1875), Scandium (1879), Germanium (1886)

Properties: Accurately predicted density, melting point, atomic weight

Significance: Validated periodic law

Anomalies in Mendeleev's Table

Position anomalies: Ar-K, Co-Ni, Te-I

Hydrogen position: Could be placed in group 1 or 17

Isotopes: Same element different positions

Modern Periodic Law

Moseley (1913): Properties are periodic function of atomic number

Basis: X-ray spectra of elements

Result: Resolved position anomalies

Example: Mendeleev's Predictions

Mendeleev predicted properties of eka-silicon (Germanium) in 1871:

Predicted (1871): Atomic weight = 72, Density = 5.5 g/cm³

Actual (1886): Atomic weight = 72.6, Density = 5.36 g/cm³

Predicted: Will form EsO₂ (density 4.7)

Actual: GeO₂ density = 4.70 g/cm³

This remarkable accuracy validated the periodic law.

Modern Periodic Table

The modern periodic table is based on atomic number and electronic configuration, organized into periods, groups, and blocks.

Features of Modern Periodic Table:

  • Periods: 7 horizontal rows (1-7)
  • Groups: 18 vertical columns (1-18)
  • Blocks: s, p, d, f based on valence electron configuration
  • Representative Elements: s and p blocks (Groups 1,2,13-18)
  • Transition Elements: d-block (Groups 3-12)
  • Inner Transition: f-block (Lanthanides & Actinides)

Periodic Table Structure

Blocks of Periodic Table:

  • s-block: Groups 1, 2, Helium - last electron enters s orbital
  • p-block: Groups 13-18 (except He) - last electron enters p orbital
  • d-block: Groups 3-12 - last electron enters d orbital
  • f-block: Lanthanides & Actinides - last electron enters f orbital

General Electronic Configuration:

s-block: ns¹⁻² | p-block: ns² np¹⁻⁶ | d-block: (n-1)d¹⁻¹⁰ ns¹⁻² | f-block: (n-2)f¹⁻¹⁴ (n-1)d⁰⁻¹ ns²

Anomalous Period 2 Elements

Period 2 elements show anomalies due to:

  1. Small atomic size
  2. High electronegativity
  3. Absence of d-orbitals
  4. High ionization energy

Examples: Li (different from other alkali metals), Be (amphoteric), B (non-metal), etc.

Example: Identifying Element from Position

An element is in period 3, group 16. Identify the element and write its electronic configuration.

Solution:

Period 3 ⇒ n = 3 (valence shell)

Group 16 ⇒ 6 valence electrons (ns² np⁴)

Electronic configuration: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁴

Atomic number = 2+2+6+2+4 = 16

Thus, the element is Sulfur (S)

Periodic Trends in Properties

Periodic trends are patterns in properties of elements across periods and down groups, explained by electronic configuration and effective nuclear charge.

Effective Nuclear Charge (Z_eff): Z_eff = Z - σ, where Z is atomic number and σ is shielding constant. It determines most periodic trends.

Key Periodic Trends

Property Trend Across Period Trend Down Group Explanation Exception
Atomic Radius Decreases Increases Increasing Z_eff vs added shells Noble gases larger
Ionic Radius Cations: Decrease
Anions: Decrease		 Increases Electron gain/loss vs added shells Transition metals irregular
Ionization Energy Increases Decreases Increasing Z_eff vs distance Group 13 < 12, Group 16 < 15
Electron Affinity Increases Decreases Increasing Z_eff vs size Group 15 < 14, Noble gases +ve
Electronegativity Increases Decreases Increasing Z_eff vs distance Transition metals irregular
Metallic Character Decreases Increases Ease of electron loss Metalloids show both

Factors Affecting Periodic Trends:

  • Nuclear Charge: Increases across period, increases down group
  • Atomic Size: Decreases across period, increases down group
  • Shielding Effect: Constant across period, increases down group
  • Penetration Effect: s > p > d > f (affects ionization energy)
  • Electronic Configuration: Stability of half-filled/filled orbitals

Trend Exceptions Explained

Ionization Energy Exceptions

Be > B: Be (2s² filled) more stable than B (2s² 2p¹)

N > O: N (2p³ half-filled) more stable than O (2p⁴)

Mg > Al: Mg (3s² filled) more stable than Al (3s² 3p¹)

Electron Affinity Exceptions

N < C: N (2p³ half-filled) stable, less tendency to gain

O < F: O experiences electron-electron repulsion

Be, Mg: Have filled s-subshell, stable

Atomic Size Anomalies

Noble gases: Larger than halogens (van der Waals radius)

Lanthanide contraction: 5d elements similar size to 4d

Transition series: Slow size decrease across period

NEET FOCUS

Lanthanide Contraction

Definition: Gradual decrease in atomic/ionic radii of lanthanides (La to Lu)

Causes: Poor shielding by 4f electrons

Consequences:

  • Similar sizes of 4d and 5d transition elements
  • Similar properties of Zr and Hf, Nb and Ta, etc.
  • Difficulty in separation of lanthanides
Example: Ordering by Atomic Radius

Arrange in increasing atomic radius: Na, Mg, Al, Si, P

Solution:

All are in period 3. Atomic radius decreases across period due to increasing Z_eff.

Order across period: Na > Mg > Al > Si > P

Thus increasing radius: P < Si < Al < Mg < Na

Alternative: Na (186 pm) > Mg (160) > Al (143) > Si (117) > P (115)

Special Group Characteristics

Diagonal Relationship

Li-Mg: Both form normal oxides, nitrides

Be-Al: Both amphoteric, form polymeric hydrides

B-Si: Both form acidic oxides, covalent compounds

Cause: Similar charge/size ratio

Inert Pair Effect

Definition: Reluctance of s-electrons to participate in bonding

Increases down group: Tl(+1) more stable than Tl(+3)

Examples: Pb(+2), Bi(+3) more stable than higher states

Cause: Poor shielding by d/f electrons

Anomalous Behavior

First member anomaly: Small size, high electronegativity

Examples: Li (covalent character), Be (amphoteric)

Period 2 anomalies: N, O, F show hydrogen bonding

Cause: Absence of d-orbitals

Electronic Configuration and Periodicity

Electronic configuration determines an element's position in the periodic table and explains periodic trends in properties.

Periodicity in Electronic Configuration: Elements in same group have same valence shell configuration. Period number = principal quantum number of valence shell.

Electronic Configuration Rules

Rule Description Example Consequence
Aufbau Principle Electrons fill lowest energy orbitals first 4s before 3d Determines configuration
Hund's Rule Maximize parallel spins in degenerate orbitals p³: ↑ ↑ ↑ Affects magnetic properties
Pauli Exclusion No two electrons can have same four quantum numbers Maximum 2 electrons/orbital Determines capacity

Exceptional Configurations:

  • Chromium (24): [Ar] 4s¹ 3d⁵ (not 4s² 3d⁴) - half-filled stability
  • Copper (29): [Ar] 4s¹ 3d¹⁰ (not 4s² 3d⁹) - fully filled stability
  • Silver (47): [Kr] 5s¹ 4d¹⁰ - similar to copper
  • Gold (79): [Xe] 6s¹ 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ - fully filled stability
  • Molybdenum (42): [Kr] 5s¹ 4d⁵ - half-filled stability
  • Palladium (46): [Kr] 4d¹⁰ - 5s empty, fully filled d

Reason: Extra stability of half-filled (d⁵, f⁷) and fully filled (d¹⁰, f¹⁴) subshells.

Magnetic Properties from Configuration

Paramagnetic: Unpaired electrons, attracted to magnetic field

Diamagnetic: All electrons paired, repelled by magnetic field

Ferromagnetic: Strong attraction (Fe, Co, Ni)

Examples:

  • O₂: Paramagnetic (2 unpaired electrons)
  • N₂: Diamagnetic (all electrons paired)
  • Cr³⁺: Paramagnetic (3 unpaired d electrons)
  • Zn²⁺: Diamagnetic (d¹⁰ configuration)
Example: Predicting Properties from Configuration

An element has electronic configuration [Ar] 4s² 3d¹⁰ 4p³. Predict its:

1. Group and period 2. Metallic character 3. Common oxidation states

Solution:

1. Valence configuration: 4s² 4p³ ⇒ Group 15, Period 4

2. Located in p-block, right side ⇒ Non-metal (Arsenic - metalloid)

3. Group 15 elements show -3, +3, +5 oxidation states

Thus: Group 15, Period 4, Metalloid, Oxidation states: -3, +3, +5

Problem Solving Strategies

Effective techniques for solving periodic table problems in JEE and NEET.

Type 1: Position Prediction

Steps:

  1. Write electronic configuration
  2. Identify valence shell (n = period)
  3. Count valence electrons (determines group)
  4. For d-block: Group = valence electrons + (n-1)d electrons
  5. For f-block: Separate lanthanides/actinides

Tip: Remember exceptional configurations (Cr, Cu, etc.)

Type 2: Trend Comparison

Steps:

  1. Identify period and group positions
  2. Remember general trends across and down
  3. Consider exceptions (half-filled stability)
  4. For ions: Consider size change on ion formation
  5. Use data if provided

Tip: For isoelectronic species: size ∝ 1/nuclear charge

Type 3: Property Prediction

Steps:

  1. Identify position in periodic table
  2. Compare with neighboring elements
  3. Consider group characteristics
  4. Remember anomalies (diagonal relationship)
  5. Use periodic law logic

Tip: For unknown properties, interpolate between known elements

JEE ADVANCED TIP

Isoelectronic Species and Size

Definition: Species with same number of electrons

Examples: O²⁻, F⁻, Ne, Na⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺ (all have 10 electrons)

Size order: O²⁻ > F⁻ > Ne > Na⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Al³⁺

Size ∝ 1/Z_eff (for isoelectronic series)

Reason: Increasing nuclear charge pulls electrons closer

MODERN PERIODIC LAW
Properties are periodic functions of atomic numbers (Moseley, 1913). Basis: X-ray spectra.
ATOMIC RADIUS TRENDS
Decreases across period (Z_eff increases), increases down group (shells added). Noble gases larger.
IONIZATION ENERGY
Increases across period, decreases down group. Exceptions: Be>B, N>O, Mg>Al due to stability.
ELECTRON AFFINITY
Increases across period, decreases down group. Exceptions: N
ELECTRONEGATIVITY
Increases across period (F highest: 4.0), decreases down group. Pauling scale most common.
METALLIC CHARACTER
Decreases across period (Cs most metallic), increases down group. Related to ionization energy.
LANTHANIDE CONTRACTION
Poor shielding by 4f causes gradual size decrease. Results: Similar sizes of 4d & 5d elements.
DIAGONAL RELATIONSHIP
Li-Mg, Be-Al, B-Si due to similar charge/size ratio. Explains anomalous properties.

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Modern periodic law is based on atomic number (Moseley)
  • Period number = principal quantum number of valence shell
  • Group number for s and p blocks = number of valence electrons
  • d-block group = (n-1)d + ns electrons (except few)
  • Atomic radius decreases across period due to increasing Z_eff
  • Ionization energy increases across period with exceptions
  • Electron affinity generally increases across period
  • Metallic character decreases across period, increases down group
  • Diagonal relationship: Li-Mg, Be-Al, B-Si
  • Inert pair effect increases down groups 13-16
  • Lanthanide contraction explains similar sizes of 4d and 5d elements
  • Exceptional configurations: Cr, Cu, Ag, Au, Mo, Pd

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Confusing atomic number with atomic weight basis
  • Forgetting ionization energy exceptions (Be>B, N>O, Mg>Al)
  • Mixing up trends across period vs down group
  • Forgetting that noble gases have larger atomic radius
  • Not considering exceptional electronic configurations
  • Confusing group numbers in old and new notations
  • Forgetting diagonal relationships
  • Not considering inert pair effect for heavier elements
