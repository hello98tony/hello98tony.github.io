Introduction to Kinematics

Kinematics is the branch of mechanics that describes the motion of objects without considering the forces that cause the motion. One-dimensional motion occurs along a straight line.

Key Concept: Motion can be described using position, displacement, velocity, and acceleration. These quantities can be positive or negative depending on the chosen coordinate system.

Basic Quantities

Position, Displacement, Distance, Velocity, Acceleration - the fundamental building blocks of kinematics.

v = Δx/Δt
a = Δv/Δt
Graphical Analysis

Position-time, velocity-time, and acceleration-time graphs provide visual understanding of motion.

Slope = Rate of change
Area under curve = Quantity
Equations of Motion

Three key equations describe motion with constant acceleration along a straight line.

v = u + at
s = ut + ½at²

Basic Concepts & Definitions

Fundamental Quantities

Quantity Definition Formula SI Unit Nature
Position (x) Location of object relative to origin - meter (m) Vector
Displacement (Δx) Change in position (final - initial) Δx = x_f - x_i meter (m) Vector
Distance Total path length traveled Path length meter (m) Scalar
Velocity (v) Rate of change of displacement v = Δx/Δt m/s Vector
Speed Rate of change of distance Speed = distance/time m/s Scalar
Acceleration (a) Rate of change of velocity a = Δv/Δt m/s² Vector

Key Differences:

  • Distance vs Displacement: Distance is path length (scalar), displacement is shortest distance between points (vector)
  • Speed vs Velocity: Speed is rate of covering distance (scalar), velocity is rate of change of displacement (vector)
  • Average vs Instantaneous: Average over time interval, instantaneous at specific instant
  • Positive vs Negative: In 1D motion, sign indicates direction relative to reference

Types of Motion

Uniform Motion

Constant velocity (zero acceleration)

a = 0
v = constant

Equal displacements in equal times

Straight line in position-time graph

Uniformly Accelerated Motion

Constant acceleration

a = constant
v = u + at

Velocity changes uniformly with time

Straight line in velocity-time graph

Non-uniform Acceleration

Variable acceleration

a ≠ constant
v = ∫a dt

Complex motion patterns

Requires calculus for analysis

Example: Distance vs Displacement

A car travels 5 km east, then 3 km west, then 2 km east. Calculate total distance and displacement.

Solution:

Distance = 5 + 3 + 2 = 10 km (total path length)

Displacement = 5E + 3W + 2E = (5 - 3 + 2)E = 4 km East

Distance is always positive, displacement can be positive, negative, or zero.

Equations of Motion

For motion with constant acceleration along a straight line, we have three fundamental equations.

Constant Acceleration Assumption: These equations are valid only when acceleration is constant. They form the foundation for solving most kinematics problems in JEE/NEET.

First Equation

v = u + at

Relates velocity with time

Where:
v = final velocity
u = initial velocity
a = acceleration
t = time

Second Equation

s = ut + ½at²

Relates displacement with time

Where:
s = displacement
u = initial velocity
a = acceleration
t = time

Third Equation

v² = u² + 2as

Relates velocity with displacement

Where:
v = final velocity
u = initial velocity
a = acceleration
s = displacement

Sign Convention:

  • Choose a reference direction as positive
  • All quantities in that direction are positive
  • All quantities opposite to that direction are negative
  • Acceleration due to gravity (g) is usually taken as -9.8 m/s² when upward is positive
  • Consistency in sign convention is crucial for correct solutions

Special Cases

Case Condition Simplified Equations
Motion from rest u = 0 v = at, s = ½at², v² = 2as
Motion to rest v = 0 0 = u + at, s = ut + ½at², 0 = u² + 2as
Uniform motion a = 0 v = u, s = ut
Free fall (upward positive) a = -g v = u - gt, s = ut - ½gt², v² = u² - 2gs
Free fall (downward positive) a = +g v = u + gt, s = ut + ½gt², v² = u² + 2gs
JEE ADVANCED

Nth Second Concept

Distance traveled in nth second:

s_n = u + a/2(2n - 1)

Where s_n = distance traveled during nth second (from t = n-1 to t = n)

For free fall from rest: s_n = g/2(2n - 1)

Ratios: s_1 : s_2 : s_3 : ... = 1 : 3 : 5 : ... (odd numbers)

Example: Equations Application

A car accelerates uniformly from rest at 2 m/s² for 10 seconds. Find final velocity and distance covered.

Solution:

Given: u = 0, a = 2 m/s², t = 10 s

Using v = u + at: v = 0 + 2×10 = 20 m/s

Using s = ut + ½at²: s = 0×10 + ½×2×(10)² = 100 m

Using v² = u² + 2as: 20² = 0 + 2×2×s ⇒ s = 400/4 = 100 m (verification)

Graphs provide visual representation of motion and relationships between kinematic quantities.

Straight line (sloping) Uniform motion Constant Constant Zero

Velocity-Time (v-t) Graphs

Graph Shape Interpretation Slope Area under curve Acceleration
Horizontal line Uniform motion Zero Displacement Zero
Straight line (sloping) Constant acceleration Constant Displacement Constant
Curve Variable acceleration Variable Displacement Variable
Below time axis Negative velocity Acceleration Negative displacement From slope

Graphical Relationships:

  • Slope of x-t graph = Instantaneous velocity
  • Slope of v-t graph = Instantaneous acceleration
  • Area under v-t graph = Displacement (with sign)
  • Area under a-t graph = Change in velocity
  • Curvature of x-t graph indicates acceleration
  • Intercept on velocity axis = Initial velocity

Graph Interpretation Rules

For x-t Graph

• Slope = velocity

• Positive slope → positive velocity

• Negative slope → negative velocity

• Zero slope → at rest

• Changing slope → acceleration

• Maximum/minimum point → v = 0

For v-t Graph

• Slope = acceleration

• Area = displacement

• Above axis → positive velocity

• Below axis → negative velocity

• Zero crossing → direction change

• Constant slope → constant acceleration

For a-t Graph

• Value = acceleration

• Area = Δv

• Above axis → acceleration

• Below axis → deceleration

• Zero line → constant velocity

• Constant value → constant acceleration

Example: Graph Analysis

From the v-t graph shown, calculate: (a) Acceleration in first 4s, (b) Displacement in first 8s, (c) Average velocity

Solution:

(a) a = slope = (20-0)/(4-0) = 5 m/s²

(b) Displacement = area under curve

Area = ½×4×20 + 4×20 + ½×4×20 = 40 + 80 + 40 = 160 m

(c) Average velocity = total displacement/total time = 160/12 = 13.33 m/s

Relative Motion

Motion described relative to a reference frame. Crucial for problems involving multiple moving objects.

Relative Velocity: Velocity of object A as observed from object B. v_AB = v_A - v_B (vector subtraction).

Key Concepts

Situation Relative Velocity Magnitude Direction
Same direction v_AB = v_A - v_B Difference of magnitudes Direction of faster object
Opposite directions v_AB = v_A + v_B Sum of magnitudes Direction of v_A
Perpendicular v_AB = √(v_A² + v_B²) Vector sum magnitude tan θ = v_B/v_A
At angle θ |v_AB| = √(v_A²+v_B²-2v_Av_Bcosθ) Law of cosines Depends on components

Relative Motion Formulas:

v_AB = v_A - v_B (velocity of A relative to B)
a_AB = a_A - a_B (acceleration of A relative to B)
r_AB = r_A - r_B (position of A relative to B)

All are vector quantities - direction matters!

Common Relative Motion Problems

Two Bodies on Same Line

Cars/trains moving on same road/track

Relative velocity = v₁ - v₂

Time to meet: t = d/(v₁ - v₂)

Separation change rate: v₁ - v₂

Overtaking problems

River-Boat Problems

Boat crossing river with current

v_boat,ground = v_boat,water + v_water,ground

Shortest time vs shortest path

Drift calculation

Swimmer problems similar

Rain-Man Problems

Rain falling relative to moving person

v_rain,man = v_rain,ground - v_man,ground

Angle at which rain appears to fall

Umbrella angle calculation

Relative velocity vector diagram

NEET IMPORTANT

Minimum Distance Problems

For two objects with constant velocities:

Minimum distance = |(r_AB · v_AB)|/|v_AB|

Where r_AB = initial relative position vector

v_AB = relative velocity vector

Time of closest approach: t = -(r_AB · v_AB)/|v_AB|²

Example: Relative Velocity

Two trains 100 km apart approach each other at 60 km/h and 40 km/h. When will they meet? Where?

Solution:

Relative velocity = 60 + 40 = 100 km/h (opposite directions)

Time to meet = distance/relative velocity = 100/100 = 1 hour

Distance from first train: 60 km/h × 1 h = 60 km

Distance from second train: 40 km/h × 1 h = 40 km

Meeting point is 60 km from first train, 40 km from second train

Projectile Motion

Motion under gravity with initial velocity at an angle to horizontal. Two-dimensional but can be analyzed as two independent 1D motions.

Key Insight: Horizontal and vertical motions are independent. Horizontal: constant velocity. Vertical: constant acceleration (g).

Projectile Motion Parameters

Parameter Formula Description
Time of flight (T) T = 2u sinθ/g Total time in air
Maximum height (H) H = u² sin²θ/(2g) Peak height reached
Horizontal range (R) R = u² sin2θ/g Total horizontal distance
Equation of path y = x tanθ - gx²/(2u² cos²θ) Parabolic trajectory
Velocity at time t v_x = u cosθ, v_y = u sinθ - gt Components at any time
Velocity at height h v = √(u² - 2gh) Speed at given height

Important Results:

  • Maximum range at θ = 45°: R_max = u²/g
  • Same range for complementary angles: θ and (90°-θ)
  • Time of flight ∝ u sinθ
  • Maximum height ∝ u² sin²θ
  • Range ∝ u² sin2θ
  • At maximum height: v_y = 0, v = v_x = u cosθ
  • Horizontal component constant: v_x = u cosθ

Special Cases

Horizontal Projection

θ = 0° from height h

T = √(2h/g)
R = u√(2h/g)
v = √(u² + 2gh)

Used in: bomb dropping, package delivery

Oblique Projection

θ with horizontal

T = 2u sinθ/g
R = u² sin2θ/g
H = u² sin²θ/(2g)

Used in: sports, artillery

Projectile from Height

Launched from height H

T = [u sinθ + √(u² sin²θ + 2gH)]/g
R = u cosθ × T

More complex trajectory

Used in: cliff problems

JEE ADVANCED

Projectile on Inclined Plane

When projectile lands on inclined plane at angle β:

Range along plane: R' = 2u² cosθ sin(θ-β)/(g cos²β)
Time of flight: T' = 2u sin(θ-β)/(g cosβ)

Maximum range when θ = (π/4 + β/2)

For β = 0°, reduces to normal projectile

Example: Projectile Calculation

A ball is thrown at 20 m/s at 37° to horizontal. Find max height, time of flight, and range.

Given: sin37° = 0.6, cos37° = 0.8, g = 10 m/s²

Solution:

Max height: H = u² sin²θ/(2g) = (20² × 0.6²)/(2×10) = 7.2 m

Time of flight: T = 2u sinθ/g = (2×20×0.6)/10 = 2.4 s

Range: R = u² sin2θ/g = (20² × sin74°)/10 = (400×0.96)/10 = 38.4 m

Problem Solving Strategies

Systematic approach to solve kinematics problems in JEE and NEET.

Type 1: Basic Equations

Steps:

  1. Identify knowns and unknowns
  2. Choose appropriate equation
  3. Apply sign convention consistently
  4. Solve algebraically
  5. Check units and reasonableness

Tip: List all given quantities with signs before solving

Type 2: Graph Problems

Steps:

  1. Identify graph type (x-t, v-t, a-t)
  2. Extract information from slopes and areas
  3. Use graphical relationships
  4. Convert to equations if needed
  5. Check for key points (intersections, peaks)

Tip: Area under v-t gives displacement (with sign)

Type 3: Relative Motion

Steps:

  1. Choose reference frame
  2. Write relative velocity equation
  3. Draw vector diagram
  4. Resolve into components if needed
  5. Solve using geometry/trigonometry

Tip: v_AB = v_A - v_B (vector subtraction)

JEE/NEET TIP

Common Problem Patterns

1. Meeting/Overtaking: Set positions equal, solve for time

2. Maximum/Minimum: Use derivative or symmetry

3. Two-stage motion: Solve stage by stage, connect at interface

4. Constrained motion: Use constraint equations

5. Non-constant acceleration: Use calculus (integration)

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Motion in straight line with constant acceleration uses three equations
  • Graphs provide visual understanding - slopes and areas have physical meaning
  • Relative motion: v_AB = v_A - v_B (vector subtraction)
  • Projectile motion: horizontal and vertical motions are independent
  • Maximum range at 45°, same range for complementary angles
  • Free fall acceleration g = 9.8 m/s² (use 10 m/s² for approximation)
  • Sign convention must be consistent throughout problem
  • Distance and displacement are different - displacement can be zero with non-zero distance

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Using equations of motion when acceleration is not constant
  • Confusing distance with displacement, speed with velocity
  • Forgetting sign convention or being inconsistent
  • Not recognizing projectile motion as two independent 1D motions
  • Miscalculating relative velocity (adding when should subtract or vice versa)
  • Forgetting that g is negative when upward is positive
  • Not checking if answer is physically reasonable
  • Confusing average speed with average velocity

Must Remember Values
