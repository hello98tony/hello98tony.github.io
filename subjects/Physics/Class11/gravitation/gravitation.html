Introduction to Gravitation

Gravitation is one of the four fundamental forces of nature. It is the force that attracts any two objects with mass. Newton's Law of Gravitation describes this universal attraction.

Historical Context: Sir Isaac Newton formulated the Law of Universal Gravitation in 1687, explaining both the motion of celestial bodies and objects on Earth with a single law.

Newton's Law

Every particle attracts every other particle with a force proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to square of distance.

F = G(m₁m₂)/r²
Kepler's Laws

Three laws describing planetary motion around the Sun, derived from observations by Johannes Kepler.

T² ∝ r³
Satellite Motion

Artificial satellites orbit Earth following the same gravitational principles as natural celestial bodies.

v = √(GM/r)

Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation

Every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers.

Mathematical Formulation: F = G(m₁m₂)/r², where G is the universal gravitational constant.

F = G × (m₁ × m₂) / r²

Where:
• F = Gravitational force (N)
• G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²
• m₁, m₂ = Masses of the two objects (kg)
• r = Distance between centers of masses (m)

Properties of Gravitational Force

Property Description Consequence
Universal Acts between all objects with mass Exists everywhere in universe
Conservative Work done is path independent Potential energy concept valid
Central Force Acts along line joining centers Conserves angular momentum
Inverse Square Law F ∝ 1/r² Strength decreases with distance²
Always Attractive Never repulsive Only pulls, never pushes
Weakest Force Relative strength = 1 Dominates only at large scales

Gravitational Constant (G):

  • Value: G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²
  • First measured by Henry Cavendish in 1798
  • Dimension: [M⁻¹L³T⁻²]
  • Universal constant - same everywhere in universe
  • Very small value ⇒ Gravitational force is weak for small masses

Gravitational Force Between Various Objects

Earth & Object

F = G × (M_earth × m_object)/R_earth²

Weight = mg = GMm/R²

Thus: g = GM/R²

Two Planets

F = G × (M₁ × M₂)/r²

r = distance between centers

Keeps planets in orbit

Person & Earth

For m = 70 kg person:

F = 70 × 9.8 ≈ 686 N

Earth also pulls with same force on person

Example: Calculating Gravitational Force

Calculate gravitational force between two 1 kg masses separated by 1 m.

Solution:

F = G × (m₁ × m₂)/r²

F = (6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ × 1 × 1)/(1²)

F = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N

This extremely small force shows why we don't feel gravitational attraction to ordinary objects.

Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

Johannes Kepler formulated three laws based on Tycho Brahe's observational data, describing how planets move around the Sun.

Historical Significance: Kepler's laws (1609-1619) provided the foundation for Newton's Law of Gravitation and revolutionized our understanding of celestial mechanics.

The Three Laws

Law Statement Mathematical Form Implication
First Law
(Law of Orbits)		 Planets move in elliptical orbits with Sun at one focus Ellipse equation Orbits are elliptical, not circular
Second Law
(Law of Areas)		 Line joining planet to Sun sweeps equal areas in equal times dA/dt = constant Planet moves faster when closer to Sun
Third Law
(Law of Periods)		 Square of orbital period is proportional to cube of semi-major axis T² ∝ r³ or T²/r³ = constant Planets farther from Sun have longer years

Kepler's Third Law (Detailed):

T² = (4π²/GM) × r³

For solar system: T² ∝ r³, where T is orbital period and r is average distance from Sun

Constant = 4π²/GM_sun for all planets

Elliptical Orbit Parameters

Semi-major Axis (a)

Half the longest diameter of ellipse.

Average distance from Sun

For circular orbit: a = radius

Used in Kepler's Third Law

Eccentricity (e)

Measure of how elliptical an orbit is.

0 ≤ e < 1

e = 0: Perfect circle

e ≈ 0.0167: Earth's orbit (nearly circular)

Perihelion & Aphelion

Closest and farthest points from Sun.

Perihelion: r_min = a(1 - e)
Aphelion: r_max = a(1 + e)

Earth: Perihelion in January, Aphelion in July

Kepler's Laws for Satellites

Kepler's laws apply to any orbital motion:

For Earth satellites: T² = (4π²/GM_earth) × r³

For geostationary satellites: T = 24 hours, r = 42,164 km from Earth's center

For circular orbits: v = √(GM/r), independent of satellite mass

Example: Kepler's Third Law Application

Mars is 1.52 times farther from Sun than Earth. Find Mars' orbital period.

Solution:

Using T² ∝ r³:

T_mars²/T_earth² = (r_mars/r_earth)³

T_mars² = (1.52)³ × (1 year)²

T_mars = √(3.51) × 1 year ≈ 1.87 years

Mars takes about 1.87 Earth years to orbit Sun.

Gravitational Field and Potential

The gravitational field is the region around a mass where its gravitational influence can be felt. Gravitational potential is the work done to bring a unit mass from infinity to that point.

Gravitational Field Intensity (g): Force experienced by unit mass placed in the gravitational field. g = F/m (vector quantity).

Gravitational Field Formulas

Situation Gravitational Field (g) Direction
Point mass M at distance r g = GM/r² Towards mass
On Earth's surface g = GM/R² ≈ 9.8 m/s² Towards Earth's center
At height h above Earth g' = GM/(R+h)² = g[R²/(R+h)²] Towards Earth's center
At depth d below Earth g' = g(1 - d/R) Towards Earth's center
Inside Earth (uniform density) g ∝ r (linear) Towards Earth's center

Gravitational Potential (V)

V = -GM/r (for point mass)

Properties:

  • Scalar quantity (J/kg)
  • Negative (work done against gravity is positive)
  • Zero at infinity
  • g = -dV/dr (field is negative gradient of potential)
Gravitational Potential Energy

Energy due to position in gravitational field.

U = -G(m₁m₂)/r

For Earth: U = -GMm/r

Negative ⇒ Bound system

Zero at infinite separation

Escape Energy

Minimum energy to escape gravitational field.

E_escape = GMm/R

From surface: ½mv_escape² = GMm/R

Thus: v_escape = √(2GM/R)

Orbital Energy

Total energy of orbiting body.

E_total = -GMm/(2r)

Kinetic: K = GMm/(2r)

Potential: U = -GMm/r

Total: E = K + U = -GMm/(2r)

Variation of g with Height and Depth:

At height h: g_h = g[R²/(R+h)²] ≈ g(1 - 2h/R) for h << R
At depth d: g_d = g(1 - d/R) (linear decrease)

At Earth's center: g = 0 (equal pull from all directions)

Example: Gravitational Potential Energy

Calculate gravitational potential energy of 1000 kg satellite at 7000 km from Earth's center. (M_earth = 6×10²⁴ kg, G = 6.67×10⁻¹¹)

Solution:

U = -GMm/r

U = -(6.67×10⁻¹¹ × 6×10²⁴ × 1000)/(7×10⁶)

U = -5.72×10¹⁰ J

Negative sign indicates bound system (satellite is in Earth's gravitational field)

Satellite Motion

Satellites are objects in orbit around celestial bodies, maintained by gravitational attraction. Their motion follows the same principles as planetary motion.

Orbital Velocity: The velocity required for a satellite to maintain a stable circular orbit at a given distance from the planet's center.

Key Parameters for Circular Orbits

Parameter Formula Description
Orbital Velocity (v₀) v₀ = √(GM/r) Speed needed for circular orbit
Time Period (T) T = 2π√(r³/GM) Time for one complete orbit
Angular Velocity (ω) ω = √(GM/r³) Angular speed of satellite
Kinetic Energy (K) K = GMm/(2r) Energy due to motion
Potential Energy (U) U = -GMm/r Energy due to position
Total Energy (E) E = -GMm/(2r) K + U, negative for bound orbit

Types of Satellites

Low Earth Orbit (LEO)

Altitude: 160-2000 km

v ≈ 7.8 km/s
T ≈ 90 minutes

Examples: ISS, Hubble, spy satellites

Applications: Imaging, research, communication

Geostationary Orbit (GEO)

Altitude: 35,786 km

v ≈ 3.07 km/s
T = 24 hours

Fixed above equator, stationary relative to Earth

Applications: Communication, weather, TV broadcast

Polar Orbit

Altitude: 700-800 km

v ≈ 7.5 km/s
T ≈ 100 minutes

Passes over poles, covers entire Earth

Applications: Mapping, reconnaissance, environment monitoring

Geostationary Satellite Conditions:

  1. Orbit must be circular
  2. Orbit must be in equatorial plane (inclination = 0°)
  3. Orbital period = 24 hours (synchronous with Earth's rotation)
  4. Orbital direction same as Earth's rotation (west to east)
Orbital radius: r = [GMT²/(4π²)]^(1/3) ≈ 42,164 km from Earth's center
Height above surface: h = r - R ≈ 35,786 km

Energy Considerations in Orbit Changes

To move satellite from orbit r₁ to r₂ (r₂ > r₁):

ΔE = GMm(1/r₁ - 1/r₂)/2

Energy increases (less negative) for higher orbit

To escape from orbit r: E_escape = GMm/r (additional to orbital energy)

Example: Satellite Orbit Calculation

Find orbital velocity and period of satellite at 500 km above Earth's surface.

Solution:

R = 6400 km, h = 500 km ⇒ r = R + h = 6900 km = 6.9×10⁶ m

v = √(GM/r) = √[(6.67×10⁻¹¹ × 6×10²⁴)/(6.9×10⁶)]

v = √(5.8×10⁷) ≈ 7616 m/s ≈ 7.62 km/s

T = 2π√(r³/GM) = 2π√[(6.9×10⁶)³/(6.67×10⁻¹¹ × 6×10²⁴)]

T ≈ 5670 s ≈ 94.5 minutes

Escape Velocity

Escape velocity is the minimum speed needed for an object to break free from the gravitational attraction of a celestial body without further propulsion.

Physical Meaning: The kinetic energy required to overcome gravitational potential energy and reach infinity with zero speed.

v_escape = √(2GM/R)

Where:
• G = Gravitational constant
• M = Mass of celestial body
• R = Radius of celestial body

Escape Velocity for Different Bodies

Celestial Body Escape Velocity Mass (kg) Radius (km)
Earth 11.2 km/s 5.97×10²⁴ 6371
Moon 2.38 km/s 7.35×10²² 1737
Sun 618 km/s 1.99×10³⁰ 696,000
Mars 5.03 km/s 6.42×10²³ 3390
Jupiter 59.5 km/s 1.90×10²⁷ 69,911

Key Points about Escape Velocity:

  • Independent of mass of escaping object (m cancels out)
  • Depends only on mass and radius of celestial body
  • Direction doesn't matter (scalar minimum speed)
  • Assumes no atmospheric friction or other forces
  • For Earth: v_escape ≈ 11.2 km/s (about 40,000 km/h)
  • Black hole: v_escape ≥ speed of light ⇒ nothing escapes

Orbital Velocity vs Escape Velocity

Orbital Velocity
v_orbital = √(GM/r)

For circular orbit

Keeps object in orbit

For low Earth orbit: ≈ 7.9 km/s

Relation: v_escape = √2 × v_orbital

Escape Velocity
v_escape = √(2GM/r)

To leave gravitational influence

Reach infinity with zero speed

For Earth: ≈ 11.2 km/s

Exactly √2 times orbital velocity

Relation
v_escape = √2 × v_orbital

For same distance r

Proof: √(2GM/r) = √2 × √(GM/r)

Escape velocity is 41.4% greater than orbital velocity

Escape energy = 2 × Orbital kinetic energy

IMPORTANT

Black Holes and Schwarzschild Radius

When escape velocity ≥ speed of light (c):

√(2GM/R) ≥ c ⇒ R ≤ 2GM/c²

Schwarzschild radius: R_s = 2GM/c²

For object compressed within this radius ⇒ Black hole forms

For Earth: R_s ≈ 9 mm (if Earth were compressed to this size!)

Example: Escape Velocity Calculation

Calculate escape velocity from Moon's surface.

Given: M_moon = 7.35×10²² kg, R_moon = 1.74×10⁶ m, G = 6.67×10⁻¹¹

Solution:

v_escape = √(2GM/R)

v = √[(2 × 6.67×10⁻¹¹ × 7.35×10²²)/(1.74×10⁶)]

v = √(5.63×10⁶) ≈ 2373 m/s ≈ 2.37 km/s

Much lower than Earth's 11.2 km/s ⇒ easier to launch from Moon

Problem Solving Strategies

Effective techniques for solving gravitation problems in JEE and NEET.

Type 1: Force & Field Calculations

Steps:

  1. Identify masses and distance between centers
  2. Use F = Gm₁m₂/r² for force
  3. Use g = GM/r² for field intensity
  4. Remember vector nature for multiple masses

Tip: For symmetrical mass distributions, use symmetry to simplify

Type 2: Orbital Motion

Steps:

  1. Use v = √(GM/r) for orbital velocity
  2. Use T = 2π√(r³/GM) for time period
  3. Use Kepler's Third Law: T² ∝ r³
  4. Remember energy formulas: K = GMm/(2r), U = -GMm/r

Tip: For circular orbits, centripetal force = gravitational force

Type 3: Energy & Escape Velocity

Steps:

  1. Use U = -GMm/r for potential energy
  2. Use v_escape = √(2GM/R)
  3. Apply energy conservation: K₁ + U₁ = K₂ + U₂
  4. For orbit changes, calculate energy difference

Tip: Escape velocity is independent of object's mass

JEE ADVANCED TIP

Gravitational Field of Extended Bodies

• For spherical shell: Field inside = 0, outside = GM/r² (as point mass)

• For solid sphere: Field inside ∝ r, outside = GM/r²

• For rod/ring: Use integration or symmetry arguments

• For Earth: Consider variation with height/depth

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Gravitational force is always attractive, never repulsive
  • G is universal constant, same everywhere in universe
  • Kepler's laws apply to any two-body system (planet-sun, satellite-Earth)
  • Orbital velocity is independent of satellite mass
  • Geostationary satellites must be in equatorial plane with period 24 hours
  • Escape velocity is independent of mass and direction of projection
  • Gravitational field inside spherical shell is zero
  • g decreases with height and depth, but differently

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Using r as distance from surface instead of from center
  • Forgetting that orbital velocity is for circular orbits
  • Confusing orbital period with rotational period
  • Thinking escape velocity depends on object's mass
  • Using wrong formula for g at height vs depth
  • Forgetting negative sign in potential and potential energy
  • Confusing geostationary with geosynchronous orbits
  • Not considering vector nature when multiple masses are involved
