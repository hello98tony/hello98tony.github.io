Units and Measurement

Class 11 Physics | NCERT Syllabus

A comprehensive guide to fundamental concepts of physical quantities, their measurement, and applications

Introduction to Physical Quantities

Physics is fundamentally an experimental science that deals with the study of matter, energy, and their interactions. To describe physical phenomena quantitatively, we need to measure various physical quantities.

Physical Quantity: Any measurable property of a material or system that can be quantified by measurement (e.g., length, mass, time, force, energy).

Types of Physical Quantities

Physical quantities are broadly classified into two categories:

  • Fundamental quantities: These are independent physical quantities that cannot be defined in terms of other quantities. They form the basis for deriving other quantities. The seven fundamental quantities in SI system are: length, mass, time, electric current, thermodynamic temperature, amount of substance, and luminous intensity.
  • Derived quantities: These are physical quantities that can be expressed in terms of fundamental quantities through mathematical relationships. Examples include speed (distance/time), force (mass × acceleration), and energy (force × distance).

Note: The distinction between fundamental and derived quantities is somewhat arbitrary and depends on the system of units being used. What is fundamental in one system may be derived in another.

Characteristics of Physical Quantities

Every physical quantity has two essential characteristics:

  • Magnitude: The numerical value representing how much of the quantity there is.
  • Unit: The standard against which the quantity is measured (e.g., meter for length, kilogram for mass).
Example:

When we say "the length of a rod is 5 meters", 5 represents the magnitude and 'meter' is the unit of length.

The International System of Units (SI)

The International System of Units (Système International d'Unités, abbreviated SI) is the modern form of the metric system and is the most widely used system of measurement in science and engineering worldwide.

Historical Development

The SI system was established in 1960 by the 11th General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM). It evolved from the earlier meter-kilogram-second (MKS) system and represents a refinement of the metric system that dates back to the French Revolution.

SI Base Units

The SI system is built upon seven base units which by convention are regarded as dimensionally independent:

SI Base Units with Current Definitions
Physical Quantity SI Unit Symbol Current Definition (Since 2019)
Length meter m Defined by fixing the speed of light in vacuum to be exactly 299,792,458 m/s
Mass kilogram kg Defined by fixing Planck's constant to be exactly 6.62607015×10⁻³⁴ J·s
Time second s Defined by fixing the unperturbed ground-state hyperfine transition frequency of the cesium-133 atom to be exactly 9,192,631,770 Hz
Electric current ampere A Defined by fixing the elementary charge to be exactly 1.602176634×10⁻¹⁹ C
Thermodynamic temperature kelvin K Defined by fixing Boltzmann constant to be exactly 1.380649×10⁻²³ J/K
Amount of substance mole mol Defined by fixing Avogadro constant to be exactly 6.02214076×10²³ mol⁻¹
Luminous intensity candela cd Defined by fixing luminous efficacy of 540 THz radiation to be exactly 683 lm/W

2019 Redefinition: In November 2018, the CGPM voted to redefine the SI base units in terms of fundamental physical constants, which took effect on May 20, 2019. This change means all SI units are now defined by constants of nature rather than physical artifacts.

SI Derived Units

Derived units are formed by combining base units according to the algebraic relations linking the corresponding quantities. Some important derived units include:

Key Facts About Units and Measurement

LENGTH STANDARD
1 meter is defined as the distance traveled by light in vacuum in 1/299,792,458 seconds (exact value since 1983)

Chapter Summary

Key Concepts

  • Physics is a quantitative science based on measurement of physical quantities
  • Physical quantities have magnitude and unit, and can be fundamental or derived
  • The SI system has seven base units defined by fundamental constants
  • Measurement instruments have specific ranges and precisions (least count)
  • All measurements have errors that can be systematic or random
  • Dimensional analysis helps check equations, derive relations, and convert units
  • Significant figures indicate the precision of measurements

Important Formulas

Vernier Calipers: Total Reading = MSR + (VSD × LC) where LC = 1 MSD - 1 VSD
Screw Gauge: Total Reading = PSR + (CSD × LC) where LC = Pitch / No. of CSD
Absolute Error: Δa = |aₘ - aₜ|
Relative Error: Δa/aₜ
Percentage Error: (Δa/aₜ) × 100%
Error Propagation: For Z = A ± B → ΔZ = ΔA + ΔB; For Z = A × B or A/B → ΔZ/Z = ΔA/A + ΔB/B
