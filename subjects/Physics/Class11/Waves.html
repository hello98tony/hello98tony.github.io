Introduction to Waves

A wave is a disturbance that travels through space and time, usually with transfer of energy without permanent displacement of particles.

Wave: A disturbance that propagates through a medium or vacuum, transferring energy from one point to another.

Medium: Material through which wave travels (not required for electromagnetic waves).

Wave Motion: Periodic disturbance that travels through space and time.

Mechanical Waves

Require material medium. Examples: Sound waves, water waves, seismic waves.

v = √(T/μ) (string)
v = √(B/ρ) (fluid)
Sound Waves

Longitudinal waves in air. Speed in air: v = √(γP/ρ) = √(γRT/M)

v = 331 + 0.61t m/s
v ∝ √T (absolute)
Doppler Effect

Change in frequency due to relative motion between source and observer.

f' = f(v ± v₀)/(v ∓ v_s)

Apparent frequency ≠ actual frequency

Progressive Wave Motion

Progressive waves transfer energy from one point to another without transferring matter. The particles oscillate about their mean positions while the wave pattern moves forward.

Wave Function: Mathematical description of wave: y(x,t) = A sin(ωt - kx + φ)

y(x,t) = A sin(ωt - kx + φ)

A = amplitude, ω = angular frequency, k = wave number, φ = phase constant

Parameter Symbol Definition SI Unit Relations
Amplitude A Maximum displacement from mean meter (m) Determines energy: E ∝ A²
Wavelength λ Distance between two consecutive points in same phase meter (m) Distance for one complete cycle
Time Period T Time for one complete wave to pass a point second (s) T = 1/f = 2π/ω
Frequency f or ν Number of waves passing a point per second Hertz (Hz) f = 1/T = ω/2π

Wave Equation (1D): ∂²y/∂t² = v² ∂²y/∂x²

Solution: y(x,t) = f(x - vt) + g(x + vt) (progressive waves)

Sinusoidal Solution: y(x,t) = A sin(ωt ± kx + φ)

+ sign: wave traveling in -x direction, - sign: wave traveling in +x direction

Standing Waves

Standing waves are formed by the superposition of two identical waves traveling in opposite directions. They have fixed nodes (points of zero amplitude) and antinodes (points of maximum amplitude).

Stationary Waves: Wave pattern resulting from interference of two waves of same frequency and amplitude traveling in opposite directions.

y(x,t) = 2A sin(kx) cos(ωt)

Nodes: Points of zero amplitude (destructive interference)

Antinodes: Points of maximum amplitude (constructive interference)

Aspect Progressive Wave Stationary Wave
Energy Transfer Energy transmitted in direction of propagation No net energy transfer (energy localized)
Amplitude Same for all particles Varies from 0 (nodes) to 2A (antinodes)
Phase Different phases at different points Same phase between two nodes, opposite phase between node and antinode

Stationary Wave Equation: y(x,t) = 2A sin(kx) cos(ωt)

Amplitude variation: A(x) = 2A |sin(kx)|

Nodes: sin(kx) = 0 ⇒ kx = nπ ⇒ x = nλ/2, n = 0, 1, 2, ...

Antinodes: |sin(kx)| = 1 ⇒ kx = (n + ½)π ⇒ x = (n + ½)λ/2

Sound Waves

Sound waves are longitudinal waves consisting of compressions (high pressure) and rarefactions (low pressure). They require a medium to propagate and travel at different speeds in different media.

Sound Wave: Longitudinal wave consisting of compressions (high pressure) and rarefactions (low pressure) propagating through a medium.

Audible Range: 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz for humans

Infrasonic: f < 20 Hz, Ultrasonic: f > 20,000 Hz

Characteristic Definition Depends On Unit Formula/Relation
Pitch Subjective perception of frequency Frequency - Higher frequency = higher pitch
Loudness Subjective perception of intensity Amplitude decibel (dB) β = 10 log₁₀(I/I₀), I₀ = 10⁻¹² W/m²
Quality/Timbre Characteristic that distinguishes different sounds of same pitch and loudness Waveform (harmonics) - Depends on number and relative amplitudes of overtones

Key Concept

Speed of sound in air: v = 331 + 0.61t m/s
In gases: v = √(γRT/M)
Audible range: 20 Hz - 20,000 Hz
Ultrasound: >20,000 Hz, Infrasound: <20 Hz

Doppler Effect

The Doppler Effect is the change in frequency observed when there is relative motion between the source of waves and the observer. This causes the familiar change in pitch of a siren as it passes by.

Doppler Effect: Change in frequency (and wavelength) of a wave in relation to an observer moving relative to the source of the wave.

Apparent Frequency: f' = f(v ± v₀)/(v ∓ v_s)

where v = speed of wave, v₀ = observer speed, v_s = source speed

Situation Formula Explanation Effect on Frequency Example
Source moving toward stationary observer f' = f v/(v - v_s) Waves compressed in front Increase Ambulance approaching
Source moving away from stationary observer f' = f v/(v + v_s) Waves stretched behind Decrease Ambulance receding
Observer moving toward stationary source f' = f (v + v₀)/v Observer encounters waves more frequently Increase Running toward siren

Key Concept

Doppler formula: f' = f(v ± v₀)/(v ∓ vₛ)
+ when moving toward, - when moving away
Applies to sound, light (redshift/blueshift), and all waves

Beats Phenomenon

Beats occur when two sound waves of slightly different frequencies interfere with each other. The result is a periodic variation in amplitude perceived as a pulsating sound.

Beats: Periodic rise and fall in intensity resulting from superposition of two waves of nearly equal frequencies.

y(t) = 2A cos(2πΔf t/2) cos(2πf_avg t)

Beat frequency: f_b = |f₁ - f₂|

Number of beats per second = difference in frequencies

Parameter Symbol Definition Formula Unit
Beat Frequency f_b Number of beats per second f_b = |f₁ - f₂| Hz
Beat Period T_b Time between successive beats T_b = 1/f_b s
Average Frequency f_avg Average of two frequencies f_avg = (f₁ + f₂)/2 Hz

Key Concept

Beat frequency: f_b = |f₁ - f₂|
Beat period: T_b = 1/f_b
Used in tuning musical instruments and radar speed guns

Problem Solving Strategies

Systematic approach to solve wave problems in JEE and NEET.

Type 1: Wave Parameters

Steps:

  1. Identify given wave function: y(x,t) = A sin(ωt ± kx + φ)
  2. Extract A, ω, k, φ
  3. Calculate: f = ω/2π, T = 1/f, λ = 2π/k, v = ω/k = fλ
  4. Determine direction: -kx → +x direction, +kx → -x direction
Type 2: Stationary Waves

Steps:

  1. Identify boundary conditions (fixed/free ends)
  2. For string fixed at both ends: λ_n = 2L/n, f_n = n(v/2L)
  3. For closed pipe: λ_n = 4L/(2n-1), f_n = (2n-1)(v/4L)
  4. For open pipe: λ_n = 2L/n, f_n = n(v/2L)
Type 3: Doppler Effect

Steps:

  1. Identify source (S), observer (O), medium speed (v)
  2. Use formula: f' = f(v ± v₀)/(v ∓ v_s)
  3. Sign convention: numerator + when O moves toward S
  4. Denominator - when S moves toward O

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Wave speed: v = √(T/μ) for string, v = √(γRT/M) for sound in gas
  • Sound in air: v = 331 + 0.61t m/s, v ∝ √T (absolute temperature)
  • Doppler effect: f' = f(v ± v₀)/(v ∓ v_s), approaching: higher f, receding: lower f
  • Stationary waves: Nodes at fixed ends, antinodes at free ends
  • String fixed both ends: f_n = n(v/2L), all harmonics present
  • Pipe closed one end: f_n = (2n-1)(v/4L), only odd harmonics
  • Beats: f_b = |f₁ - f₂|, used for tuning instruments
