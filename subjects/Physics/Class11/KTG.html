Kinetic Theory of Gases

Class 11 Physics | JEE & NEET Preparation Guide

Understanding gases at the molecular level - from random motion to macroscopic properties

Introduction to Kinetic Theory

The kinetic theory of gases explains the macroscopic properties of gases (pressure, temperature, volume) in terms of the microscopic motion of their constituent molecules.

Ideal Gas: A theoretical gas that follows the ideal gas equation exactly. Assumes: point molecules, no intermolecular forces, elastic collisions.

PV = nRT

Where P = pressure, V = volume, n = number of moles, R = gas constant, T = absolute temperature

Basic Postulates

Fundamental assumptions about gas molecules: point masses, random motion, elastic collisions, no forces except during collisions.

P = (1/3)ρv²
v_rms = √(3RT/M)
Gas Laws

Boyle's, Charles', Gay-Lussac's, Avogadro's laws derived from kinetic theory. All combined in ideal gas equation.

PV = constant (Boyle)
V/T = constant (Charles)
Molecular Motion

Molecules in constant random motion with different speeds. RMS, average, most probable speeds related differently.

v_rms > v_avg > v_mp
KE_avg = (3/2)kT

Real vs Ideal Gases

Basic Postulates of Kinetic Theory

The kinetic theory is based on several fundamental assumptions about the nature of gases.

Postulate 1: A gas consists of a large number of identical molecules that are in random motion with various speeds.

Postulate 2: The molecules are point masses with negligible volume compared to the container volume.

Postulate 3: The molecules exert no forces on each other except during collisions (which are elastic).

Postulate 4: Collisions with walls are elastic and instantaneous.

Postulate 5: Newton's laws of motion apply to molecular motion.

Derivation of Pressure Formula

Pressure from Molecular Collisions:

  1. Consider a molecule with velocity v hitting wall perpendicularly
  2. Change in momentum per collision = 2mv
  3. Number of collisions per second = v/2L (for cube of side L)
  4. Force on wall = rate of change of momentum
  5. For N molecules with average v²: P = (1/3)(Nm/V)v²
P = (1/3)ρv² = (1/3)(Nm/V)v²

Where ρ = density, m = molecular mass, N = number of molecules, V = volume

Interpretation of Temperature

Average Kinetic Energy

From pressure formula and ideal gas equation:

(1/2)mv²_avg = (3/2)kT

Where k = Boltzmann constant = R/N_A

k = 1.38 × 10⁻²³ J/K

Temperature is measure of average translational KE

For ideal gas: T ∝ average KE

RMS Speed

Root Mean Square speed:

v_rms = √(v²_avg) = √(3kT/m)

For one mole: v_rms = √(3RT/M)

Where M = molar mass

v_rms ∝ √T, v_rms ∝ 1/√M

At same T, lighter gases move faster

Internal Energy

For ideal monatomic gas:

U = (3/2)NkT = (3/2)nRT

Only translational kinetic energy

For diatomic/polyatomic: include rotational, vibrational energy

U depends only on T for ideal gas

ΔU = nC_vΔT

JEE ADVANCED

Van der Waals Equation

Correction to ideal gas equation for real gases:

(P + a/V²)(V - b) = RT

Where:
a = correction for intermolecular forces
b = correction for molecular volume

Critical constants:
V_c = 3b, P_c = a/(27b²), T_c = 8a/(27Rb)

Reduced equation: (P_r + 3/V_r²)(3V_r - 1) = 8T_r

Example: Pressure Calculation

Calculate pressure exerted by 10²³ oxygen molecules (m = 5.3×10⁻²⁶ kg) in 1 m³ container with rms speed 500 m/s.

Solution:

Using pressure formula: P = (1/3)(Nm/V)v_rms²

P = (1/3) × (10²³ × 5.3×10⁻²⁶ / 1) × (500)²

= (1/3) × (5.3×10⁻³) × 2.5×10⁵

= (1/3) × 1325 = 441.7 Pa

Pressure is directly proportional to number of molecules and square of speed.

Gas Laws from Kinetic Theory

All gas laws can be derived from the kinetic theory and the ideal gas equation.

Ideal Gas Equation: The fundamental equation combining all gas laws.

PV = nRT

Where R = 8.314 J/mol·K = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K = 2 cal/mol·K

Fundamental Gas Laws

Law Statement Mathematical Form Kinetic Theory Derivation Graph
Boyle's Law At constant T, P ∝ 1/V PV = constant P = (1/3)ρv², with v² ∝ T constant Hyperbola in PV diagram
Charles' Law At constant P, V ∝ T V/T = constant v² ∝ T, so V must ∝ T to keep P constant Straight line through origin in VT graph
Gay-Lussac's Law At constant V, P ∝ T P/T = constant Direct from P = (1/3)ρv² with v² ∝ T Straight line through origin in PT graph
Avogadro's Law At same T and P, equal volumes contain equal molecules V ∝ n From P = (1/3)(Nm/V)v², with v² ∝ T constant Linear V vs n
Dalton's Law Total pressure = sum of partial pressures P_total = ∑P_i Molecules don't interact, pressures add -

Combined Gas Law:

P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

Standard Conditions:

  • STP (Standard Temperature & Pressure): 0°C = 273K, 1 atm = 1.013×10⁵ Pa
  • Molar volume at STP: 22.4 L = 0.0224 m³
  • NTP (Normal Temperature & Pressure): 20°C = 293K, 1 atm
  • SATP (Standard Ambient): 25°C = 298K, 1 bar = 10⁵ Pa

Example: Combined Gas Law

A gas at 27°C and 2 atm occupies 3 L. What volume at 127°C and 4 atm?

Solution:

T₁ = 27 + 273 = 300K, P₁ = 2 atm, V₁ = 3 L

T₂ = 127 + 273 = 400K, P₂ = 4 atm, V₂ = ?

Using combined gas law: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

V₂ = (P₁V₁T₂)/(P₂T₁) = (2×3×400)/(4×300) = 2400/1200 = 2 L

Volume decreases despite temperature increase due to greater pressure increase.

Pressure, Energy & Molecular Speeds

Understanding how microscopic molecular motion relates to macroscopic pressure and energy.

Pressure Formula (Derived):

P = (1/3)ρv² = (1/3)(Nm/V)v²

Comparing with ideal gas equation PV = NkT:

(1/2)mv² = (3/2)kT

Average translational kinetic energy per molecule = (3/2)kT

Different Molecular Speeds

Speed Type Formula Relation Physical Significance For O₂ at 300K
Most Probable (v_p) √(2kT/m) = √(2RT/M) Speed at distribution peak Maximum molecules have this speed 395 m/s
Average Speed (v_avg) √(8kT/πm) = √(8RT/πM) Arithmetic mean of speeds Used in mean free path calculations 445 m/s
RMS Speed (v_rms) √(3kT/m) = √(3RT/M) Root mean square speed Related to kinetic energy 483 m/s
Relation v_p : v_avg : v_rms = 1 : 1.128 : 1.225 = √2 : √(8/π) : √3

Key Relationships:

  • v_rms ∝ √T (at constant molecular mass)
  • v_rms ∝ 1/√M (at constant temperature)
  • KE_avg = (1/2)mv²_rms = (3/2)kT
  • For mixture: v_rms(mixture) = √(3RT/M_mix) where M_mix = average molar mass
  • At same T: lighter gas molecules move faster
  • To have same v_rms: T ∝ M (higher T needed for heavier gas)

Energy Distribution in Gases

Translational Energy

Energy due to translational motion

KE_trans = (3/2)kT per molecule

For n moles: U_trans = (3/2)nRT

Monatomic gases: only translational

Degrees of freedom: 3 (x, y, z directions)

Contribution: (1/2)kT per degree

Rotational Energy

Energy due to rotational motion

Monatomic: negligible (point masses)

Diatomic: 2 rotational degrees

KE_rot = (2/2)kT = kT per molecule

Linear molecules: 2 rotational degrees

Nonlinear: 3 rotational degrees

Vibrational Energy

Energy due to molecular vibrations

Active at high temperatures

Each vibrational mode: 2 degrees (KE + PE)

Energy = 2 × (1/2)kT = kT per mode

Diatomic: 1 vibrational mode at high T

Polyatomic: multiple vibrational modes

JEE ADVANCED

Law of Equipartition of Energy

In thermal equilibrium, energy is equally distributed among all degrees of freedom.

Energy per degree of freedom = (1/2)kT

For ideal gas:

  • Translational: 3 degrees → (3/2)kT
  • Rotational: depends on molecule shape
  • Vibrational: active at high T, each mode: kT (2 degrees)

Total energy = (f/2)kT per molecule, where f = total degrees of freedom

Example: RMS Speed Comparison

Compare rms speeds of H₂ and O₂ at same temperature. (M_H₂ = 2 g/mol, M_O₂ = 32 g/mol)

Solution:

v_rms = √(3RT/M)

v_H₂/v_O₂ = √(M_O₂/M_H₂) = √(32/2) = √16 = 4

Hydrogen molecules move 4 times faster than oxygen at same temperature

At 300K: v_H₂ ≈ 1920 m/s, v_O₂ ≈ 480 m/s

Lighter gases have higher molecular speeds at same temperature.

Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

The distribution of molecular speeds in a gas at thermal equilibrium follows Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics.

Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution Law: The probability that a molecule has speed between v and v+dv is:

f(v)dv = 4π(m/2πkT)^(3/2) v² e^(-mv²/2kT) dv

Where f(v) is the probability density function

Characteristics of Distribution

Feature Description Mathematical Expression Significance
Asymmetric Not symmetric about peak f(v) ≠ f(-v) More molecules have speeds > v_p than < v_p
Starts at zero f(0) = 0 No molecules at rest All molecules in motion at T > 0
Goes to zero f(∞) = 0 No infinite speed molecules Energy conservation limit
Temperature effect Distribution broadens with T v_p ∝ √T More high-speed molecules at higher T
Mass effect Lighter gases have broader distribution v_p ∝ 1/√m Hydrogen distributes more widely than oxygen

Properties of Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution:

  • Normalization: ∫₀^∞ f(v)dv = 1 (total probability = 1)
  • Most probable speed: v_p = √(2kT/m) where df/dv = 0
  • Average speed: v_avg = ∫₀^∞ v f(v)dv = √(8kT/πm)
  • RMS speed: v_rms = √(∫₀^∞ v² f(v)dv) = √(3kT/m)
  • Width: Δv = v_rms - v_p ≈ 0.225 v_rms
  • Fraction above a speed: ∫_{v₀}^∞ f(v)dv (calculus needed)

NEET IMPORTANT

Distribution at Different Temperatures

Effect of increasing T:

  • Peak shifts right: v_p increases as √T
  • Peak height decreases: distribution broadens
  • Area under curve remains 1 (normalization)
  • More molecules have high speeds
  • Fewer molecules have low speeds

Effect of molecular mass:

  • Heavier gas: narrower distribution
  • Lighter gas: broader distribution
  • At same T: v_p ∝ 1/√m
Example: Fraction of Fast Molecules

Estimate fraction of nitrogen molecules at 300K with speeds exceeding 800 m/s. (M = 28 g/mol, v_rms = 517 m/s)

Approximation: v/v_rms = 800/517 ≈ 1.55

From Maxwell distribution tables: fraction ≈ 0.08

About 8% of molecules have speeds > 800 m/s

Significant fraction have speeds much higher than average.

Exact calculation: Requires integration of Maxwell distribution

Degrees of Freedom & Specific Heats

Degrees of freedom determine how energy is distributed in a molecule and affect specific heats.

Degrees of Freedom: Number of independent ways a molecule can store energy. Includes translational, rotational, and vibrational motions.

Law of Equipartition: Energy per degree = (1/2)kT

Degrees of Freedom for Different Molecules

Molecule Type Example Translational Rotational Vibrational* Total (f) C_v (per mole) γ = C_p/C_v
Monatomic He, Ne, Ar 3 0 0 3 (3/2)R ≈ 12.47 J/mol·K 5/3 ≈ 1.67
Diatomic (rigid) H₂, N₂, O₂ (at room T) 3 2 0 5 (5/2)R ≈ 20.79 J/mol·K 7/5 = 1.4
Diatomic (vibrating) High temperature 3 2 2 (1 mode) 7 (7/2)R ≈ 29.10 J/mol·K 9/7 ≈ 1.29
Linear Polyatomic CO₂ (vibrational inactive) 3 2 0 5 (5/2)R ≈ 20.79 J/mol·K 7/5 = 1.4
Nonlinear Polyatomic H₂O, CH₄ 3 3 0 6 3R ≈ 24.94 J/mol·K 4/3 ≈ 1.33

*Vibrational degrees activated at high temperatures (>1000K for most gases)

Specific Heat Relations:

C_v = (f/2)R (molar specific heat at constant volume)
C_p = C_v + R (Mayer's relation)
γ = C_p/C_v = 1 + 2/f

For ideal gas:

  • Monatomic: f=3, γ=5/3≈1.67
  • Diatomic: f=5, γ=7/5=1.4
  • Polyatomic: f=6, γ=4/3≈1.33

Mean Free Path & Collisions

Mean Free Path (λ)

Average distance between collisions

λ = 1/(√2 π d² n)

Where d = molecular diameter, n = number density

Using ideal gas: λ = kT/(√2 π d² P)

At STP for air: λ ≈ 6×10⁻⁸ m

λ ∝ T, λ ∝ 1/P

Longer mean free path at low pressure, high temperature

Collision Frequency (Z)

Number of collisions per second

Z = v_avg/λ = √2 π d² n v_avg

Using v_avg = √(8kT/πm)

At STP for air: Z ≈ 10⁹ collisions/s

Z ∝ √T, Z ∝ P

More collisions at high pressure

Collision cross-section: σ = πd²

Transport Properties

Viscosity (η): η = (1/3)ρv_avgλ ∝ √T

Gas viscosity increases with T (unlike liquids)

Thermal conductivity (k): k = (1/3)ρv_avgλC_v ∝ √T

Diffusion coefficient (D): D = (1/3)v_avgλ ∝ T^(3/2)/P

All depend on mean free path

JEE ADVANCED

Specific Heat Temperature Dependence

Degrees of freedom activated at different temperatures:

  1. Very low T (< 50K): Only translational (f=3, C_v=3R/2)
  2. Low T (~50-250K): + rotational (f=5, C_v=5R/2 for diatomic)
  3. Room T (250-1000K): Full rotation, no vibration
  4. High T (>1000K): + vibrational modes activated

Quantum mechanics explains step-like increase in C_v with T

Classical equipartition valid only at high enough T

Example: Mean Free Path Calculation

Calculate mean free path of nitrogen at STP. d = 3.7×10⁻¹⁰ m, T = 273K, P = 1.01×10⁵ Pa.

Solution:

Using λ = kT/(√2 π d² P)

k = 1.38×10⁻²³ J/K

λ = (1.38×10⁻²³ × 273)/(√2 × π × (3.7×10⁻¹⁰)² × 1.01×10⁵)

= (3.77×10⁻²¹)/(1.414 × 3.14 × 1.37×10⁻¹⁹ × 1.01×10⁵)

= (3.77×10⁻²¹)/(6.16×10⁻¹⁴) = 6.12×10⁻⁸ m

Nitrogen molecules travel about 60 nm between collisions at STP.

Problem Solving Strategies

Systematic approach to solve kinetic theory problems in JEE and NEET.

Type 1: Molecular Speed Problems

Steps:

  1. Identify which speed (v_rms, v_avg, v_p) is needed
  2. Use appropriate formula
  3. Convert to SI units (kg for m, K for T)
  4. For comparison: use ratios to avoid calculations
  5. Check: v_rms > v_avg > v_p

Tip: v_rms = √(3RT/M), remember molar mass in kg/mol

Type 2: Energy & Temperature

Steps:

  1. For translational KE: use (3/2)kT per molecule
  2. For n moles: U = (f/2)nRT
  3. Identify degrees of freedom (f)
  4. Use equipartition: (1/2)kT per degree
  5. For internal energy change: ΔU = nC_vΔT

Tip: Monatomic: f=3, diatomic: f=5, polyatomic: f=6 (room T)

Type 3: Gas Law Problems

Steps:

  1. Write given quantities with units
  2. Identify constant parameter (P, V, T, n)
  3. Use appropriate gas law
  4. Temperature must be in Kelvin
  5. Check if ideal gas equation applies

Tip: PV = nRT, R = 8.314 J/mol·K = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K

JEE/NEET TIP

Common Problem Patterns

1. Speed comparison: v_rms₁/v_rms₂ = √(T₁M₂/T₂M₁)

2. Energy calculation: KE_total = (f/2)nRT = (f/2)PV

3. Pressure from kinetic theory: P = (1/3)ρv_rms²

4. Mean free path: λ = kT/(√2 π d² P) ∝ T/P

5. Specific heats: C_v = (f/2)R, C_p = C_v + R, γ = 1 + 2/f

6. Gas mixtures: Use average molar mass, Dalton's law

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Kinetic theory explains gas properties in terms of molecular motion
  • Pressure results from molecular collisions with container walls
  • Temperature is proportional to average translational kinetic energy
  • RMS speed: v_rms = √(3RT/M), increases with T, decreases with M
  • Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution describes speed distribution
  • Degrees of freedom: monatomic=3, diatomic=5, polyatomic=6 (at room T)
  • Law of equipartition: Energy = (1/2)kT per degree of freedom
  • Mean free path: average distance between collisions, λ ∝ T/P
  • Specific heats: C_v = (f/2)R, C_p = C_v + R, γ = C_p/C_v = 1 + 2/f

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Using Celsius instead of Kelvin in gas law calculations
  • Confusing v_rms, v_avg, and v_mp (remember: v_rms > v_avg > v_mp)
  • Forgetting to convert molar mass to kg/mol in speed calculations
  • Applying ideal gas equation to real gases without corrections
  • Wrong degrees of freedom assignment for different molecules
  • Mixing up specific heat formulas for different processes
  • Forgetting that mean free path depends on both T and P
  • Assuming all molecules have the same speed (they don't!)
