Introduction to Straight Lines

Straight lines are fundamental concepts in coordinate geometry that describe linear relationships between variables. They form the basis for more complex geometric and algebraic studies.

Straight Line: A line with constant slope, represented algebraically as ax + by + c = 0, where a, b, c are real numbers and a and b are not both zero.

Every straight line can be represented in various forms: slope-intercept, point-slope, two-point, intercept, and normal forms.

Basic Properties of Straight Lines:

  • Uniqueness: Two points uniquely determine a straight line
  • Linearity: Every straight line can be represented by a first-degree equation
  • Slope: Constant rate of change, measures steepness
  • Intercepts: Points where line crosses x-axis (x-intercept) and y-axis (y-intercept)
  • Parallelism: Two lines are parallel if their slopes are equal (m₁ = m₂)
  • Perpendicularity: Two lines are perpendicular if product of slopes is -1 (m₁ × m₂ = -1)
  • Angle with axes: Angle with x-axis determines slope: m = tan θ
  • Collinearity: Three points are collinear if slopes between pairs are equal

Slope (Gradient) of a Line

The slope or gradient of a line measures its steepness and direction. It's defined as the ratio of vertical change (rise) to horizontal change (run) between any two points on the line.

Slope (m): For two points P(x₁, y₁) and Q(x₂, y₂) on a line, slope m = (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁), provided x₁ ≠ x₂.

Angle of inclination (θ): The angle the line makes with positive x-axis in counterclockwise direction. m = tan θ, where 0° ≤ θ < 180°, θ ≠ 90°.

Slope Formula: m = (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁)
Slope from angle: m = tan θ
Condition for parallel lines: m₁ = m₂
Condition for perpendicular lines: m₁ × m₂ = -1
Positive Slope
m > 0

Line rises from left to right

Negative Slope
m < 0

Line falls from left to right
90° < θ < 180°

Zero Slope
m = 0

θ = 0°

m = undefined

θ = 90°

Properties and Applications of Slope:

  • Rate of change: Slope represents rate of change of y with respect to x
  • Parallel lines: Have equal slopes (m₁ = m₂)
  • Perpendicular lines: Have slopes with product -1 (m₁ × m₂ = -1)
  • Collinearity: Three points A, B, C are collinear if slope AB = slope BC = slope AC
  • Angle between lines: tan θ = |(m₁ - m₂)/(1 + m₁m₂)|
  • Horizontal line: Slope = 0, equation: y = constant
  • Vertical line: Slope undefined, equation: x = constant
  • Increasing function: Positive slope (m > 0)
  • Decreasing function: Negative slope (m < 0)
  • Constant function: Zero slope (m = 0)
  • Applications: Physics (velocity), economics (marginal cost), engineering (gradient)

Example: Finding and Interpreting Slope

Find the slope of line passing through points A(2, 3) and B(6, 7). Determine if it's rising or falling and find its angle with x-axis.

Solution:

Step 1: Use slope formula: m = (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁)

Step 2: Substitute values: m = (7 - 3)/(6 - 2) = 4/4 = 1

Step 3: Since m = 1 > 0, line rises from left to right.

Step 4: Angle with x-axis: m = tan θ ⇒ 1 = tan θ ⇒ θ = 45°

Step 5: Interpretation: For every 1 unit increase in x, y increases by 1 unit. Line makes 45° angle with positive x-axis.

Verification: Points C(3, 4) and D(5, 6) should also lie on same line if collinear:
Slope AC = (4-3)/(3-2) = 1/1 = 1 ✓
Slope AD = (6-3)/(5-2) = 3/3 = 1 ✓

Equations of Straight Lines

A straight line can be represented in various algebraic forms depending on given information. Each form has its advantages for specific problems.

General Form: ax + by + c = 0, where a, b, c are real numbers and a and b are not both zero.

Standard Forms: Different forms used based on available information: slope-intercept, point-slope, two-point, intercept, normal.

Slope-Intercept Form

When slope and y-intercept are known

y = mx + c

m = slope, c = y-intercept

Point-Slope Form

When slope and one point are known

y - y₁ = m(x - x₁)

(x₁, y₁) is point on line

Two-Point Form

When two points are known

(y - y₁)/(y₂ - y₁) = (x - x₁)/(x₂ - x₁)

(x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂) are points

Key Points About Line Equations:

  • Every first-degree equation in x and y represents a straight line
  • General form ax + by + c = 0 can be converted to any other form
  • Slope from general form: m = -a/b (if b ≠ 0)
  • x-intercept from general form: -c/a (if a ≠ 0)
  • y-intercept from general form: -c/b (if b ≠ 0)
  • For vertical line: x = constant (a ≠ 0, b = 0)
  • For horizontal line: y = constant (a = 0, b ≠ 0)
  • Line through origin: c = 0 in general form
  • Parallel to x-axis: a = 0 in general form
  • Parallel to y-axis: b = 0 in general form
  • Choosing appropriate form simplifies problem-solving
  • Intercept form useful when x and y intercepts are known or needed
  • Normal form useful for finding perpendicular distance from origin
  • Parametric form useful for locating points at specific distances
Example: Converting Between Forms

Given line in slope-intercept form: y = 2x + 3. Convert to: (a) General form, (b) Intercept form, (c) Normal form.

Solution:

(a) General form: y = 2x + 3 ⇒ 2x - y + 3 = 0

(b) Intercept form: First find intercepts:
x-intercept: Set y=0 ⇒ 0 = 2x + 3 ⇒ x = -3/2
y-intercept: Set x=0 ⇒ y = 3
Intercept form: x/(-3/2) + y/3 = 1 ⇒ -2x/3 + y/3 = 1 ⇒ 2x - y = -3 (same as general form rearranged)

(c) Normal form: From general form 2x - y + 3 = 0:
Compare with x cos α + y sin α = p
Divide by √(a² + b²) = √(2² + (-1)²) = √5
(2/√5)x - (1/√5)y + 3/√5 = 0
Normal form: (2/√5)x - (1/√5)y = -3/√5
Here cos α = 2/√5, sin α = -1/√5, p = 3/√5

Verification: All forms represent same line. Test point (1, 5):
Slope-intercept: 5 = 2(1) + 3 = 5 ✓
General form: 2(1) - 5 + 3 = 2 - 5 + 3 = 0 ✓
Normal form: (2/√5)(1) - (1/√5)(5) = 2/√5 - 5/√5 = -3/√5 ✓

Distance Formulas

Distance formulas are essential in coordinate geometry for finding distances between points, from points to lines, and between parallel lines.

Distance between two points: P(x₁, y₁) and Q(x₂, y₂): d = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]

Distance from point to line: Distance from P(x₁, y₁) to line ax + by + c = 0: d = |ax₁ + by₁ + c|/√(a² + b²)

d = √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²]

Distance Formula Derivation

Derivation of Distance Between Two Points

Given: Points P(x₁, y₁) and Q(x₂, y₂)

Step 1: Draw horizontal and vertical lines from P and Q to form right triangle

Step 2: Horizontal distance (base): |x₂ - x₁|

Step 3: Vertical distance (height): |y₂ - y₁|

Step 4: By Pythagorean theorem: PQ² = (x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²

Step 5: Taking square root: PQ = √[(x₂ - x₁)² + (y₂ - y₁)²]

Step 6: Distance is always positive, so absolute values not needed when squaring

Verification: For P(1, 2) and Q(4, 6):
Horizontal distance: 4 - 1 = 3
Vertical distance: 6 - 2 = 4
Distance: √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5

Distance from Point to Line Derivation

Derivation of Perpendicular Distance Formula

Given: Point P(x₁, y₁) and line L: ax + by + c = 0

Step 1: Find equation of line perpendicular to L passing through P

Step 2: Slope of L: m = -a/b (if b ≠ 0)

Step 3: Slope of perpendicular line: m' = b/a (if a ≠ 0)

Step 4: Equation of perpendicular: y - y₁ = (b/a)(x - x₁)

Step 5: Find intersection Q of L and perpendicular line (foot of perpendicular)

Step 6: Solve simultaneously: ax + by + c = 0 and y - y₁ = (b/a)(x - x₁)

Step 7: Coordinates of Q: x = (b²x₁ - aby₁ - ac)/(a² + b²), y = (a²y₁ - abx₁ - bc)/(a² + b²)

Step 8: Distance PQ = √[(x - x₁)² + (y - y₁)²]

Step 9: Simplify: d = |ax₁ + by₁ + c|/√(a² + b²)

Verification: For P(1, 2) and line 2x + 3y - 12 = 0:
d = |2(1) + 3(2) - 12|/√(2² + 3²) = |2 + 6 - 12|/√13 = |-4|/√13 = 4/√13 ≈ 1.109

Angle Between Two Lines

The angle between two lines is measured as the acute angle (0° to 90°) or obtuse angle (90° to 180°) between them. Formulas relate this angle to the slopes of the lines.

Angle between lines: If m₁ and m₂ are slopes of two lines, then tan θ = |(m₁ - m₂)/(1 + m₁m₂)|, where θ is acute angle between lines.

If lines are perpendicular: 1 + m₁m₂ = 0 ⇒ m₁m₂ = -1

If lines are parallel: m₁ = m₂

Special Cases

Case Condition Angle (θ) tan θ Example Slopes
Parallel Lines m₁ = m₂ 0° 0 m₁ = 2, m₂ = 2
Perpendicular Lines m₁m₂ = -1 90° Undefined m₁ = 2, m₂ = -0.5
Lines at 45° |(m₁-m₂)/(1+m₁m₂)| = 1 45° 1 m₁ = 0, m₂ = 1
Lines at 60° |(m₁-m₂)/(1+m₁m₂)| = √3 60° √3 ≈ 1.732 m₁ = 0, m₂ = √3
Lines at 30° |(m₁-m₂)/(1+m₁m₂)| = 1/√3 30° 1/√3 ≈ 0.577 m₁ = 0, m₂ = 1/√3

Important Points About Angles Between Lines:

  • Formula gives acute angle (0° ≤ θ ≤ 90°) by taking absolute value
  • Obtuse angle = 180° - acute angle
  • For perpendicular lines, denominator 1 + m₁m₂ = 0, so tan θ undefined (θ = 90°)
  • For parallel lines, numerator m₁ - m₂ = 0, so tan θ = 0 (θ = 0°)
  • When one line is vertical (slope undefined), use angle with x-axis: θ = |90° - α| where α is angle of other line
  • Angle between lines given in general form: tan θ = |(a₁b₂ - a₂b₁)/(a₁a₂ + b₁b₂)|
  • Lines ax + by + c = 0 and a'x + b'y + c' = 0 are:
    • Parallel if a/a' = b/b' ≠ c/c'
    • Perpendicular if aa' + bb' = 0
  • Angle bisectors: Lines that divide angle between two lines into two equal angles
  • Equation of angle bisectors: |(a₁x + b₁y + c₁)/√(a₁² + b₁²)| = |(a₂x + b₂y + c₂)/√(a₂² + b₂²)|
Example: Finding Angle Between Lines

Find acute angle between lines with slopes m₁ = 2 and m₂ = -3.

Solution:

Step 1: Use formula: tan θ = |(m₁ - m₂)/(1 + m₁m₂)|

Step 2: Substitute values: tan θ = |(2 - (-3))/(1 + 2×(-3))| = |(2 + 3)/(1 - 6)| = |5/(-5)| = |-1| = 1

Step 3: Since tan θ = 1, θ = 45° (acute angle)

Step 4: Obtuse angle between lines = 180° - 45° = 135°

Verification: Check if lines are perpendicular: m₁m₂ = 2×(-3) = -6 ≠ -1, so not perpendicular

Alternative method: Angle of line 1: θ₁ = tan⁻¹(2) ≈ 63.43°
Angle of line 2: θ₂ = tan⁻¹(-3) ≈ -71.57° (or 180° - 71.57° = 108.43°)
Acute angle difference: |63.43° - 108.43°| = 45° ✓

Find equations of angle bisectors of lines: 3x + 4y - 7 = 0 and 12x - 5y + 8 = 0.

Solution:

Step 1: Formula for angle bisectors: |(a₁x + b₁y + c₁)/√(a₁² + b₁²)| = |(a₂x + b₂y + c₂)/√(a₂² + b₂²)|

Step 2: For lines: L₁: 3x + 4y - 7 = 0 (a₁=3, b₁=4, c₁=-7)
L₂: 12x - 5y + 8 = 0 (a₂=12, b₂=-5, c₂=8)

Step 3: √(a₁² + b₁²) = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5
√(a₂² + b₂²) = √(12² + (-5)²) = √(144 + 25) = √169 = 13

Step 4: Equation: |(3x + 4y - 7)/5| = |(12x - 5y + 8)/13|

Step 5: Removing absolute values gives two equations:
(3x + 4y - 7)/5 = (12x - 5y + 8)/13 ... (1)
(3x + 4y - 7)/5 = -(12x - 5y + 8)/13 ... (2)

Step 6: Simplify (1): 13(3x + 4y - 7) = 5(12x - 5y + 8)
39x + 52y - 91 = 60x - 25y + 40
39x - 60x + 52y + 25y - 91 - 40 = 0
-21x + 77y - 131 = 0 ⇒ 21x - 77y + 131 = 0

Step 7: Simplify (2): 13(3x + 4y - 7) = -5(12x - 5y + 8)
39x + 52y - 91 = -60x + 25y - 40
39x + 60x + 52y - 25y - 91 + 40 = 0
99x + 27y - 51 = 0 ⇒ Divide by 3: 33x + 9y - 17 = 0

Step 8: Angle bisectors are: 21x - 77y + 131 = 0 and 33x + 9y - 17 = 0

Note: One bisector is acute angle bisector, other is obtuse angle bisector (perpendicular to each other)

Concurrency and Collinearity

Concurrency refers to three or more lines meeting at a single point. Collinearity refers to three or more points lying on a single straight line.

Concurrent lines: Three or more lines that intersect at a single point.

Collinear points: Three or more points that lie on the same straight line.

Condition for Concurrency

Three lines a₁x + b₁y + c₁ = 0, a₂x + b₂y + c₂ = 0, a₃x + b₃y + c₃ = 0 are concurrent if:

|a₁ b₁ c₁; a₂ b₂ c₂; a₃ b₃ c₃| = 0
Condition for Collinearity

Three points (x₁, y₁), (x₂, y₂), (x₃, y₃) are collinear if:

|x₁ y₁ 1; x₂ y₂ 1; x₃ y₃ 1| = 0
Area of Triangle

Three points are collinear if area of triangle formed by them is zero:

Area = ½|x₁(y₂-y₃)+x₂(y₃-y₁)+x₃(y₁-y₂)| = 0

Methods to Check Concurrency

To check if three lines are concurrent:

  1. Determinant method:
    |a₁ b₁ c₁|
    |a₂ b₂ c₂| = 0
    |a₃ b₃ c₃|
  2. Intersection method: Find intersection of two lines, substitute in third
  3. Linear combination: One line can be expressed as linear combination of other two
  4. Geometric method: Use properties of triangles (altitudes, medians, etc.)

Methods to Check Collinearity

To check if three points are collinear:

  1. Slope method: Slope AB = Slope BC = Slope AC
  2. Area method: Area of triangle ABC = 0
  3. Distance method: AB + BC = AC or AB + AC = BC or AC + BC = AB
  4. Determinant method:
    |x₁ y₁ 1|
    |x₂ y₂ 1| = 0
    |x₃ y₃ 1|
  5. Equation method: All points satisfy same linear equation

Example: Concurrency of Lines

Check if lines x + 2y - 3 = 0, 2x + 3y - 4 = 0, and 3x + 4y - 5 = 0 are concurrent.

Solution (Determinant method):

Form determinant:
|1 2 -3|
|2 3 -4|
|3 4 -5|

Calculate determinant:
= 1×(3×(-5) - 4×(-4)) - 2×(2×(-5) - 3×(-4)) + (-3)×(2×4 - 3×3)
= 1×(-15 + 16) - 2×(-10 + 12) - 3×(8 - 9)
= 1×1 - 2×2 - 3×(-1)
= 1 - 4 + 3 = 0

Since determinant = 0, lines are concurrent.

Solution (Intersection method):

Solve first two equations:
x + 2y = 3 ...(1)
2x + 3y = 4 ...(2)
From (1): x = 3 - 2y
Substitute in (2): 2(3 - 2y) + 3y = 4 ⇒ 6 - 4y + 3y = 4 ⇒ -y = -2 ⇒ y = 2
Then x = 3 - 2(2) = 3 - 4 = -1
Intersection point is (-1, 2)

Check if (-1, 2) satisfies third equation: 3(-1) + 4(2) - 5 = -3 + 8 - 5 = 0 ✓

Therefore, all three lines pass through (-1, 2), so they are concurrent.

Example: Collinearity of Points

Check if points A(1, 2), B(2, 4), and C(3, 6) are collinear.

Solution (Slope method):

Slope AB = (4 - 2)/(2 - 1) = 2/1 = 2

Slope BC = (6 - 4)/(3 - 2) = 2/1 = 2

Slope AC = (6 - 2)/(3 - 1) = 4/2 = 2

Since all slopes are equal (2), points are collinear.

Solution (Area method):

Area of triangle ABC = ½|1(4-6) + 2(6-2) + 3(2-4)| = ½|1(-2) + 2(4) + 3(-2)| = ½|-2 + 8 - 6| = ½|0| = 0

Since area = 0, points are collinear.

Solution (Equation method):

Equation of line through A(1, 2) and B(2, 4):
Slope m = 2, equation: y - 2 = 2(x - 1) ⇒ y = 2x
Check if C(3, 6) satisfies: 6 = 2×3 = 6 ✓
Therefore, all points lie on line y = 2x, so they are collinear.

Advanced Concepts

Family of lines: Set of lines passing through intersection of two given lines

Equation: (a₁x + b₁y + c₁) + λ(a₂x + b₂y + c₂) = 0

Pair of straight lines: General second-degree equation representing two lines

Condition: ax² + 2hxy + by² + 2gx + 2fy + c = 0 represents pair of lines if abc + 2fgh - af² - bg² - ch² = 0

Homogeneous equation: ax² + 2hxy + by² = 0 represents pair of lines through origin

Angle between lines: tan θ = |2√(h² - ab)/(a + b)|

Applications: Conic sections, transformation geometry, vector algebra

Problem Solving Strategies

Effective techniques for solving straight line problems in JEE and other competitive exams.

Type 1: Finding Line Equations

Steps:

  1. Identify given information (points, slope, intercepts)
  2. Choose appropriate form of line equation
  3. Substitute values and simplify
  4. Convert to required form if needed

Tip: Use point-slope form when slope and one point known

Type 2: Distance Problems

Steps:

  1. Identify what distance needs to be found
  2. Use appropriate distance formula
  3. Substitute values carefully
  4. Simplify and find exact or approximate value

Tip: For point-line distance, use perpendicular distance formula

Type 3: Angle Problems

Steps:

  1. Find slopes of given lines
  2. Use angle formula: tan θ = |(m₁-m₂)/(1+m₁m₂)|
  3. Determine if acute or obtuse angle needed
  4. Find angle using inverse tangent

Tip: Check if lines are parallel or perpendicular first

Common Problem Types:

  • Equation problems: Finding line equations from various given conditions
  • Distance problems: Point-point, point-line, line-line distances
  • Angle problems: Angle between lines, angle bisectors
  • Concurrency problems: Proving lines concurrent, finding point of concurrency
  • Collinearity problems: Proving points collinear, finding line through points
  • Locus problems: Finding equation of path traced by moving point
  • Reflection problems: Finding image of point or line about another line
  • Foot of perpendicular: Finding point on line closest to given point
  • Triangle geometry: Medians, altitudes, perpendicular bisectors
  • Coordinate geometry proofs: Proving geometric properties using coordinates
JEE ADVANCED TIP

Advanced Problem Solving Techniques

• For complex problems: Break into smaller subproblems

• For locus problems: Let moving point be (h, k), find relation, convert to (x, y)

• For symmetric points: Use midpoint and slope conditions

• For triangle centers: Use formulas for centroid, circumcenter, orthocenter

• For transformation problems: Use rotation, translation, reflection formulas

• For optimization: Use calculus or geometric properties

• For parametric problems: Express coordinates in terms of parameter

• For intersection problems: Solve equations simultaneously

• For distance minimization: Use perpendicular distance or calculus

• For angle bisectors: Remember both acute and obtuse angle bisectors

Example: Complex Locus Problem

A point moves such that the sum of its distances from lines 3x - 4y + 7 = 0 and 12x + 5y - 9 = 0 is always 5 units. Find equation of locus.

Solution:

Step 1: Let moving point be P(h, k)

Step 2: Distance from P to line L₁ (3x - 4y + 7 = 0):
d₁ = |3h - 4k + 7|/√(3² + (-4)²) = |3h - 4k + 7|/5

Step 3: Distance from P to line L₂ (12x + 5y - 9 = 0):
d₂ = |12h + 5k - 9|/√(12² + 5²) = |12h + 5k - 9|/13

Step 4: Given: d₁ + d₂ = 5

Step 5: So: |3h - 4k + 7|/5 + |12h + 5k - 9|/13 = 5

Step 6: Multiply by 65 (LCM of 5 and 13): 13|3h - 4k + 7| + 5|12h + 5k - 9| = 325

Step 7: Remove absolute values by considering cases based on signs of expressions:
Case 1: 3h - 4k + 7 ≥ 0 and 12h + 5k - 9 ≥ 0
Case 2: 3h - 4k + 7 ≥ 0 and 12h + 5k - 9 < 0
Case 3: 3h - 4k + 7 < 0 and 12h + 5k - 9 ≥ 0
Case 4: 3h - 4k + 7 < 0 and 12h + 5k - 9 < 0

Step 8: For each case, we get linear equation in h and k. Since h and k can vary, locus consists of four line segments forming a parallelogram.

Step 9: Replace (h, k) with (x, y) to get locus equation:
13|3x - 4y + 7| + 5|12x + 5y - 9| = 325

Conclusion: The locus is not a single line but set of points satisfying above equation, which represents boundary of region between two pairs of parallel lines.

SLOPE OF A LINE
m = (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁) = tan θ. Positive: rising, Negative: falling, Zero: horizontal, Undefined: vertical
EQUATION FORMS
Slope-intercept: y = mx + c, Point-slope: y-y₁=m(x-x₁), Two-point, Intercept: x/a+y/b=1, General: ax+by+c=0
DISTANCE FORMULAS
Two points: √[(x₂-x₁)²+(y₂-y₁)²], Point-line: |ax₁+by₁+c|/√(a²+b²), Parallel lines: |c₁-c₂|/√(a²+b²)
ANGLE BETWEEN LINES
tan θ = |(m₁-m₂)/(1+m₁m₂)|. Parallel: m₁=m₂, Perpendicular: m₁m₂=-1
CONCURRENCY & COLLINEARITY
Concurrency: |a₁ b₁ c₁; a₂ b₂ c₂; a₃ b₃ c₃|=0. Collinearity: Area=0 or slopes equal
SECTION FORMULA
Point dividing AB in ratio m:n: ((mx₂+nx₁)/(m+n), (my₂+ny₁)/(m+n)). Midpoint: ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2)
APPLICATIONS
Physics (trajectories), Engineering (design), Computer graphics, Navigation, Economics (linear models)

Foundation for coordinate geometry, conic sections, 3D geometry, calculus. 2-3 questions every year

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Slope m = tan θ, where θ is angle with positive x-axis
  • Positive slope: line rises left to right (0° < θ < 90°)
  • Negative slope: line falls left to right (90° < θ < 180°)
  • Zero slope: horizontal line (θ = 0°)
  • Undefined slope: vertical line (θ = 90°)
  • Two points uniquely determine a straight line
  • Every first-degree equation in x and y represents a straight line
  • General form: ax + by + c = 0 can be converted to any other form
  • Distance between parallel lines ax+by+c₁=0 and ax+by+c₂=0: |c₁-c₂|/√(a²+b²)
  • Perpendicular distance from point to line is shortest distance
  • Angle between lines: tan θ = |(m₁-m₂)/(1+m₁m₂)| gives acute angle
  • For perpendicular lines: m₁m₂ = -1
  • For parallel lines: m₁ = m₂
  • Three points collinear if area of triangle = 0 or slopes equal
  • Three lines concurrent if determinant = 0 or intersect at same point
  • Section formula: Point dividing line segment in ratio m:n
  • Midpoint formula: Average of coordinates
  • Centroid of triangle: Average of vertices' coordinates
  • Foot of perpendicular: Find by solving line equation and perpendicular through point
  • Image of point about line: Use midpoint and perpendicular conditions
  • Family of lines through intersection: L₁ + λL₂ = 0
