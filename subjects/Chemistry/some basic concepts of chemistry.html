Laws of Chemical Combination

These are the fundamental laws established through careful experimental observations that govern how elements combine to form compounds.

Historical Context: These laws were established in the late 18th century by scientists like Lavoisier, Proust, and Dalton, leading to the development of atomic theory.

The Five Fundamental Laws

Law Statement Scientist Example Significance
Law of Conservation of Mass Mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction Antoine Lavoisier (1789) 2g H₂ + 16g O₂ → 18g H₂O Total mass reactants = Total mass products
Law of Definite Proportions A given compound always contains exactly the same proportion of elements by mass Joseph Proust (1799) Water always has H:O = 1:8 by mass Constant composition of compounds
Law of Multiple Proportions When two elements form more than one compound, masses of one element combined with fixed mass of other are in simple whole number ratio John Dalton (1803) CO & CO₂: Oxygen ratio 1:2 Supports atomic theory
Law of Gaseous Volumes When gases react, they do so in volumes which bear simple ratio to one another and to volumes of products (if gaseous) Gay-Lussac (1808) 2L H₂ + 1L O₂ → 2L H₂O vapor Led to Avogadro's hypothesis
Avogadro's Law Equal volumes of all gases under same conditions of temperature and pressure contain equal number of molecules Amedeo Avogadro (1811) 22.4L of any gas at STP = 1 mole Foundation for mole concept

Key Points about Laws:

  • Law of Conservation of Mass is the basis for balancing chemical equations
  • Law of Definite Proportions explains why compounds have fixed composition
  • Law of Multiple Proportions provided evidence for Dalton's atomic theory
  • Gay-Lussac's Law applies only to gases measured at same T & P
  • Avogadro's Law allows us to relate volumes of gases to number of molecules
Example: Law of Multiple Proportions

Carbon forms two oxides. In first, 12g C combines with 16g O. In second, 12g C combines with 32g O. Show this follows law of multiple proportions.

Solution:

First oxide: Mass of O combining with 12g C = 16g

Second oxide: Mass of O combining with 12g C = 32g

Ratio of oxygen masses = 16:32 = 1:2 (simple whole number ratio)

Thus follows Law of Multiple Proportions

Dalton's Atomic Theory (1808)

John Dalton proposed his atomic theory to explain the laws of chemical combination. This theory marked the beginning of modern chemistry.

Postulates of Dalton's Atomic Theory:

Postulate 1

Matter consists of indivisible particles called atoms.

Modern modification: Atoms are divisible (contain subatomic particles)

Postulate 2

Atoms of a given element are identical in mass and chemical properties.

Modern modification: Isotopes exist (same element, different mass)

Postulate 3

Atoms of different elements have different masses and properties.

This part remains valid in modern atomic theory

Postulate 4

Atoms combine in small whole number ratios to form compounds.

Explains Law of Multiple Proportions

Postulate 5

Atoms can neither be created nor destroyed in chemical reactions.

Explains Law of Conservation of Mass

Drawbacks of Dalton's Atomic Theory

  1. Atoms are divisible: Discovery of subatomic particles (electrons, protons, neutrons)
  2. Isotopes exist: Atoms of same element can have different masses
  3. Isobars exist: Atoms of different elements can have same mass number
  4. Allotropes: Same element can exist in different forms (e.g., diamond & graphite)
  5. Failed to explain: Why atoms combine, nature of bonding
Atomic Mass Unit (amu) = 1/12th mass of one carbon-12 atom
1 amu = 1.66054 × 10⁻²⁷ kg
Example: Atomic Theory Application

How does Dalton's atomic theory explain:

a) Law of Conservation of Mass?

b) Law of Definite Proportions?

Explanation:

a) Since atoms are neither created nor destroyed (Postulate 5), total mass remains constant

b) Since atoms combine in fixed ratios (Postulate 4), compounds have definite composition

Atomic & Molecular Mass

Understanding mass at atomic and molecular levels is crucial for quantitative chemical calculations.

Atomic Mass: Mass of an atom expressed in atomic mass units (amu). Relative atomic mass is the ratio of average mass of an atom to 1/12th mass of carbon-12 atom.

Key Concepts

Term Definition Formula/Example Unit
Atomic Mass Mass of single atom H = 1.008 amu, O = 16.00 amu amu
Gram Atomic Mass Atomic mass expressed in grams 1 g-atom H = 1.008 g g
Molecular Mass Sum of atomic masses in molecule H₂O = 2×1 + 16 = 18 amu amu
Gram Molecular Mass Molecular mass in grams 1 g-mole H₂O = 18 g g
Formula Mass For ionic compounds NaCl = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5 amu amu

Average Atomic Mass Calculation

Average Atomic Mass = Σ (Isotopic mass × Fractional abundance)
Example: Average Atomic Mass

Chlorine has two isotopes: Cl-35 (75%) and Cl-37 (25%). Calculate average atomic mass.

Solution:

Average mass = (35 × 0.75) + (37 × 0.25)

= 26.25 + 9.25 = 35.5 amu

This explains why atomic mass of chlorine is 35.5 amu (not whole number)

Molecular Mass Calculation

H₂SO₄:

2 × H = 2 × 1 = 2

1 × S = 1 × 32 = 32

4 × O = 4 × 16 = 64

Total = 98 amu

Common Molar Masses

H₂O = 18 g/mol

CO₂ = 44 g/mol

NaCl = 58.5 g/mol

H₂SO₄ = 98 g/mol

NaOH = 40 g/mol

Isotopes & Average Atomic Mass

For element with isotopes A, B, C:

Avg. mass = (m_A × %_A + m_B × %_B + m_C × %_C) / 100

Where % = percentage abundance

Fractional abundance = %/100

The Mole Concept

The mole is the SI unit for amount of substance. It provides a bridge between the microscopic world of atoms/molecules and macroscopic measurements.

Mole: Amount of substance containing as many elementary entities as there are atoms in exactly 12 g of carbon-12.

Key Relationships

Relationship Formula When to Use
Mass ↔ Moles n = m / M Given mass, find moles or vice versa
Moles ↔ Particles N = n × N_A Given moles, find number of particles
Volume ↔ Moles (Gas at STP) n = V / 22.4 Gas volume at STP (0°C, 1 atm)
Molarity M = n / V (in L) Solution concentration

Important Values to Remember:

  • Avogadro's number (N_A) = 6.022 × 10²³ mol⁻¹
  • Molar volume at STP = 22.4 L/mol
  • STP conditions: 0°C (273 K) and 1 atm pressure
  • NTP conditions: 20°C (293 K) and 1 atm pressure (molar volume ≈ 24 L/mol)
  • 1 mole electrons = 6.022 × 10²³ electrons = 96500 C (1 Faraday)

Percentage Composition

% of element = (Mass of element in compound / Molar mass of compound) × 100
Example: Mole Calculation

Calculate:

a) Number of moles in 180g of water

b) Number of molecules in 180g of water

c) Number of atoms in 180g of water

Solution:

a) Molar mass H₂O = 18 g/mol

Moles = 180g / 18 g/mol = 10 mol

b) Molecules = 10 mol × 6.022×10²³ mol⁻¹ = 6.022×10²⁴ molecules

c) Each H₂O molecule has 3 atoms (2H + 1O)

Total atoms = 6.022×10²⁴ × 3 = 1.807×10²⁵ atoms

Stoichiometry & Stoichiometric Calculations

Stoichiometry involves calculations based on balanced chemical equations, relating masses, moles, and volumes of reactants and products.

Stoichiometry: Greek: "stoicheion" (element) + "metron" (measure). Quantitative relationship between reactants and products in chemical reactions.

Steps in Stoichiometric Calculations

  1. Write balanced chemical equation
  2. Convert given quantities to moles (using appropriate conversions)
  3. Use mole ratios from balanced equation
  4. Convert moles to desired units
  5. Check answer (units, significant figures, reasonableness)

Types of Stoichiometric Problems

Mass-Mass

Given: Mass of reactant

Find: Mass of product

Use: n = m/M, then mole ratios

Example: Given CaCO₃ mass, find CO₂ mass produced

Mass-Volume

Given: Mass of solid

Find: Volume of gas

Use: n = m/M, then V = n × 22.4 (STP)

Example: Given CaCO₃ mass, find CO₂ volume at STP

Volume-Volume

Given: Volume of gas

Find: Volume of gas

Use: Gay-Lussac's Law

Example: Given O₂ volume, find CO₂ volume produced

Limiting Reagent Concept:

  • Limiting reagent: Reactant that gets completely consumed first
  • Excess reagent: Reactant left over after reaction
  • To identify: Calculate (moles/coefficient) for each reactant; smallest is limiting
  • Product amount is determined by limiting reagent
IMPORTANT

Reaction Yield Calculations

Theoretical yield = Calculated maximum product
Actual yield = Experimentally obtained amount
Percentage yield = (Actual yield / Theoretical yield) × 100%

Percentage yield is always ≤ 100%

Example: Stoichiometry with Limiting Reagent

2g H₂ reacts with 32g O₂ to form water. Find:

a) Limiting reagent

b) Mass of water formed

Solution:

Reaction: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

Moles H₂ = 2g / 2 g/mol = 1 mol

Moles O₂ = 32g / 32 g/mol = 1 mol

For H₂: moles/coefficient = 1/2 = 0.5

For O₂: moles/coefficient = 1/1 = 1

H₂ has smaller ratio ⇒ Limiting reagent

From equation: 2 mol H₂ produces 2 mol H₂O

So 1 mol H₂ produces 1 mol H₂O = 18 g

Problem Solving Strategies

Effective techniques for solving problems in "Some Basic Concepts of Chemistry" for JEE and NEET.

Type 1: Mole Concept Problems

Steps:

  1. Identify what's given and what's asked
  2. Convert given to moles using appropriate formula
  3. Perform required calculation in moles
  4. Convert back to asked units
  5. Check significant figures

Tip: Always work in moles first, it simplifies calculations

Type 2: Stoichiometry with Limiting Reagent

Steps:

  1. Write balanced chemical equation
  2. Convert all reactants to moles
  3. Calculate (moles/coefficient) for each
  4. Identify limiting reagent (smallest ratio)
  5. Calculate product based on limiting reagent

Tip: Product amount is always determined by limiting reagent

Type 3: Empirical & Molecular Formulas

Steps:

  1. Assume 100g sample → Convert % to grams
  2. Convert grams to moles for each element
  3. Divide by smallest number of moles
  4. Multiply to get simplest whole numbers
  5. Molecular formula = (Empirical formula)ₙ

Tip: For hydrate formulas, treat water as separate element

JEE ADVANCED TIP

Advanced Problem Solving

• For mixture problems: Set up simultaneous equations

• For consecutive reactions: Consider all steps or use overall reaction

• For redox stoichiometry: Use concept of equivalents

• For gas reactions not at STP: Use ideal gas equation PV = nRT

• Remember: 1 mole of gas at STP = 22.4 L (memorize this!)

LAW OF CONSERVATION OF MASS
Mass neither created nor destroyed. Total mass reactants = Total mass products. Basis for balancing equations.
LAW OF DEFINITE PROPORTIONS
Compound always has same element proportions by mass. Water always H:O = 1:8 by mass.
LAW OF MULTIPLE PROPORTIONS
When two elements form multiple compounds, mass ratios are simple whole numbers. Evidence for atomic theory.
AVOGADRO'S NUMBER
N_A = 6.022×10²³ mol⁻¹. Number of particles in 1 mole. Links microscopic to macroscopic world.
MOLE CONCEPT
1 mole = 6.022×10²³ particles = Molar mass in grams = 22.4 L at STP (for gases). Central to all chemical calculations.
STOICHIOMETRY
Calculation of reactants/products using balanced equations. Based on mole ratios from balanced equations.
LIMITING REAGENT
Reactant completely consumed first. Determines maximum product amount. Identify by (moles/coefficient) ratio.
PERCENTAGE YIELD
% yield = (Actual yield/Theoretical yield)×100. Always ≤ 100%. Measures efficiency of reaction.

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • All stoichiometric calculations require balanced chemical equations
  • Mole concept is the bridge between microscopic particles and macroscopic measurements
  • Limiting reagent determines the maximum amount of product possible
  • Percentage yield is always ≤ 100% (actual yield ≤ theoretical yield)
  • At STP (0°C, 1 atm), 1 mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 L
  • Empirical formula gives simplest whole number ratio; molecular formula gives actual numbers
  • Atomic mass is relative; based on carbon-12 scale (exactly 12 amu)
  • Laws of chemical combination are experimental facts; Dalton's theory tried to explain them
  • Significant figures must be maintained in calculations
  • Dimensional analysis helps check correctness of equations

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Forgetting to balance chemical equations before stoichiometry
  • Using atomic mass instead of molar mass in calculations
  • Confusing molarity (mol/L solution) with molality (mol/kg solvent)
  • Not converting units properly (mg → g, mL → L, etc.)
  • Assuming 22.4 L/mol applies to all conditions (only at STP!)
  • Forgetting that limiting reagent determines product amount
  • Confusing empirical formula with molecular formula
  • Not considering significant figures in final answers
  • Mixing up Gay-Lussac's law (volumes) with Avogadro's law
  • Forgetting that mole applies to atoms, molecules, ions, electrons
