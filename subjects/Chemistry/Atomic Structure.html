Introduction to Atomic Structure

The atom is the basic unit of matter that constitutes a chemical element. Understanding atomic structure is fundamental to all branches of chemistry and physics.

Historical Evolution: The concept of the atom has evolved from Democritus' indivisible particles to the complex quantum mechanical model we use today.

Bohr's Atomic Model

Electrons move in specific circular orbits around the nucleus with quantized energy levels.

E = -13.6/n² eV
Quantum Mechanical Model

Electrons exist in probability clouds called orbitals, described by wave functions.

Ĥψ = Eψ

Evolution of Atomic Models

Our understanding of atomic structure has evolved through several key models, each building upon the previous one and explaining more experimental observations.

Historical Atomic Models Timeline

Model Year Scientist Key Features Limitations
Dalton's Model 1808 John Dalton Atoms as indivisible solid spheres No internal structure
Thomson's Model 1904 J.J. Thomson Plum pudding model, electrons embedded in positive sphere Couldn't explain scattering experiments
Rutherford Model 1911 Ernest Rutherford Nuclear model, dense nucleus with electrons orbiting Electrons should spiral into nucleus
Bohr's Model 1913 Niels Bohr Quantized orbits, fixed energy levels Only worked for hydrogen
Quantum Model 1926 Schrödinger Wave functions, electron clouds, orbitals Complex mathematics

Key Experiments:

  • Cathode Ray Tube (1897): J.J. Thomson discovered electrons
  • Gold Foil Experiment (1911): Rutherford discovered nucleus
  • Hydrogen Spectrum (1885): Balmer series led to Bohr's model
  • Photoelectric Effect (1905): Einstein explained particle nature of light
  • Davisson-Germer (1927): Confirmed wave nature of electrons

Example: Calculating Number of Subatomic Particles

For Carbon-14 atom (¹⁴₆C), calculate protons, neutrons, and electrons.

Solution:

Atomic number (Z) = 6 ⇒ Protons = 6

Mass number (A) = 14 ⇒ Neutrons = A - Z = 14 - 6 = 8

For neutral atom: Electrons = Protons = 6

Thus: Protons = 6, Neutrons = 8, Electrons = 6

Bohr's Atomic Model

Niels Bohr proposed his model in 1913 to explain the hydrogen spectrum and overcome limitations of Rutherford's model. It introduced quantization of electron orbits.

Postulates of Bohr's Model:

  1. Electrons move in circular orbits without radiating energy
  2. Only certain orbits are permitted where angular momentum is quantized
  3. Electrons can jump between orbits by absorbing or emitting photons
Angular Momentum: mvr = nħ, where ħ = h/2π
Radius of nth orbit: rₙ = n²a₀, where a₀ = 0.529 Å
Energy of nth orbit: Eₙ = -13.6/n² eV (for hydrogen)

R = 1.097 × 10⁷ m⁻¹

Where n₁ and n₂ are principal quantum numbers (n₂ > n₁)

Bohr-Sommerfeld Model

Extended Bohr's model with elliptical orbits:

∮pᵢdqᵢ = nᵢh (Quantization condition)

For circular orbits: mvr = nh/2π

For elliptical orbits: Two quantum numbers - n (principal) and k (azimuthal)

Example: Energy of Photon Emitted

Calculate energy of photon emitted when electron in hydrogen atom jumps from n=4 to n=2.

Solution:

E₄ = -13.6/4² = -0.85 eV

E₂ = -13.6/2² = -3.4 eV

ΔE = E₄ - E₂ = (-0.85) - (-3.4) = 2.55 eV

Convert to Joules: 2.55 × 1.602×10⁻¹⁹ = 4.08×10⁻¹⁹ J

Alternatively: ΔE = 13.6(1/2² - 1/4²) = 13.6(1/4 - 1/16) = 13.6 × 3/16 = 2.55 eV

Quantum Mechanical Model

The quantum mechanical model describes electrons not as particles in orbits but as wave functions that give probability distributions for finding electrons in particular regions.

Schrödinger Equation: Ĥψ = Eψ, where Ĥ is the Hamiltonian operator, ψ is the wave function, and E is energy. Solutions give atomic orbitals.

Quantum Numbers

Principal (n)

Energy level, shell

n = 1, 2, 3, ...

Determines energy and size

Azimuthal (l)

Subshell shape

l = 0 to n-1

s, p, d, f orbitals

Magnetic (mₗ)

Orbital orientation

mₗ = -l to +l

For p-orbitals: -1, 0, +1

Spin (mₛ)

Electron spin

mₛ = +½ or -½

Pauli exclusion principle

Atomic Orbitals

Orbital l value Number of Orbitals Max Electrons Shape

Quantum Mechanical Principles:

  • Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle: Δx·Δp ≥ h/4π
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons can have all four quantum numbers same
  • Aufbau Principle: Electrons fill lowest energy orbitals first
  • Hund's Rule: Electrons occupy degenerate orbitals singly first
  • Wave-Particle Duality: Electrons exhibit both wave and particle properties

Orbital Shapes and Nodes

Cloverleaf shapes

Example: Quantum Numbers

Which quantum numbers are possible for an electron in 3d orbital?

Solution:

For 3d: n = 3

d orbital ⇒ l = 2

mₗ = -2, -1, 0, +1, +2 (five possible values)

mₛ = +½ or -½

Possible sets: (3,2,-2,±½), (3,2,-1,±½), (3,2,0,±½), (3,2,1,±½), (3,2,2,±½)

Electron Configuration

Electron configuration describes the distribution of electrons in atomic orbitals. It follows specific rules and determines chemical properties.

Aufbau Principle: Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p, 7s, 5f, 6d, 7p...

Electron Filling Rules

Rule Description Example
Pauli Exclusion Maximum 2 electrons per orbital with opposite spins 1s² (not 1s³)
Aufbau Principle Fill lowest energy orbitals first 4s fills before 3d
Hund's Rule Fill degenerate orbitals singly first p³: ↑ ↑ ↑ (not ↑↓ ↑)

Notable Electron Configurations

Hydrogen (1)
1s¹

Simplest atom

One electron

Basis for Bohr model

Carbon (6)
1s² 2s² 2p²

Four valence electrons

Forms covalent bonds

Basis of organic chemistry

Copper (29)
[Ar] 4s¹ 3d¹⁰

Exception to Aufbau

Half-filled d subshell

More stable configuration

Exceptions to Aufbau Principle:

  • Chromium (24): [Ar] 4s¹ 3d⁵ (not 4s² 3d⁴) - half-filled d subshell
  • Copper (29): [Ar] 4s¹ 3d¹⁰ (not 4s² 3d⁹) - filled d subshell
  • Molybdenum (42): [Kr] 5s¹ 4d⁵ - similar to Cr
  • Silver (47): [Kr] 5s¹ 4d¹⁰ - similar to Cu
  • Gold (79): [Xe] 6s¹ 4f¹⁴ 5d¹⁰ - similar to Cu

These exceptions occur because half-filled or fully filled d or f subshells are more stable.

Isoelectronic Species

Species with same electron configuration:

N³⁻, O²⁻, F⁻, Ne, Na⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺ all have 1s² 2s² 2p⁶

Size decreases with increasing nuclear charge: N³⁻ > O²⁻ > F⁻ > Ne > Na⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Al³⁺

Example: Electron Configuration

Write electron configuration for Iron (Fe, Z=26).

Solution:

Following Aufbau order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d

1s² (2) + 2s² (4) + 2p⁶ (10) + 3s² (12) + 3p⁶ (18) + 4s² (20) + 3d⁶ (26)

Thus: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d⁶ or [Ar] 4s² 3d⁶

For Fe³⁺ ion: Remove 4s² and one 3d electron ⇒ [Ar] 3d⁵

Periodic Table and Atomic Structure

The periodic table organizes elements based on atomic structure. Elements in same group have similar electron configurations in their valence shells.

Periodic Trends Based on Atomic Structure

Property Trend Across Period Trend Down Group Explanation
Atomic Radius Decreases Increases Increasing nuclear charge vs electron shielding
Ionization Energy Increases Decreases Nuclear charge and distance from nucleus
Electron Affinity Increases Decreases Stability of added electron
Electronegativity Increases Decreases Ability to attract electrons
Metallic Character Decreases Increases Ease of losing electrons

Blocks of Periodic Table:

  • s-block: Groups 1, 2 (alkali and alkaline earth metals) - valence electrons in s orbital
  • p-block: Groups 13-18 (including metalloids and nonmetals) - valence electrons in p orbital
  • d-block: Groups 3-12 (transition metals) - filling d orbitals
  • f-block: Lanthanides and actinides - filling f orbitals

NEET FOCUS

Effective Nuclear Charge

Z_eff = Z - σ

Where Z = atomic number, σ = shielding constant

Slater's rules for calculating σ:

1. Electrons in same group: 0.35 (except 1s: 0.30)

2. Electrons in n-1 shell: 0.85

3. Electrons in n-2 or lower: 1.00

Example: Periodic Trends

Arrange in increasing atomic radius: Mg, Si, S, Na, Al

Solution:

All are in period 3. Atomic radius decreases across period.

Order: Na (group 1) > Mg (group 2) > Al (group 13) > Si (group 14) > S (group 16)

Thus: S < Si < Al < Mg < Na (increasing radius)

Problem Solving Strategies

Effective techniques for solving atomic structure problems in JEE and NEET.

Type 1: Quantum Numbers

Steps:

  1. Identify orbital from principal and azimuthal quantum numbers
  2. Check possible mₗ values: -l to +l
  3. Apply Pauli principle: max 2 electrons per orbital
  4. Remember mₛ = ±½ for electrons

Tip: For n=3, l can be 0,1,2 ⇒ s, p, d orbitals

Type 2: Electronic Configuration

Steps:

  1. Use Aufbau order: 1s 2s 2p 3s 3p 4s 3d 4p...
  2. Follow Hund's rule for degenerate orbitals
  3. Check for exceptions (Cr, Cu, etc.)
  4. For ions: remove from highest n first

Tip: 4s fills before 3d but loses electrons first

Type 3: Spectrum Calculations

Steps:

  1. Use Rydberg formula: 1/λ = R(1/n₁² - 1/n₂²)
  2. For hydrogen: Eₙ = -13.6/n² eV
  3. ΔE = hc/λ = hν
  4. Identify series from transition

Tip: Lyman: UV, Balmer: Visible, Paschen+: IR

JEE ADVANCED TIP

de Broglie Wavelength and Uncertainty Principle

λ = h/p = h/mv (de Broglie wavelength)
Δx·Δp ≥ h/4π (Heisenberg uncertainty)

For electron in Bohr orbit: 2πr = nλ (standing wave condition)

Minimum uncertainty in position for confined particle

BOHR'S MODEL
Quantized orbits: mvr = nħ. Energy: Eₙ = -13.6/n² eV. Explains hydrogen spectrum
QUANTUM NUMBERS
n=1,2,3... (shell). l=0...n-1 (subshell). mₗ=-l...+l (orbital). mₛ=±½ (spin)
AUFBAU PRINCIPLE
Electrons fill lowest energy orbitals first: 1s,2s,2p,3s,3p,4s,3d,4p... Exceptions: Cr,Cu
RYDBERG FORMULA
1/λ = R(1/n₁² - 1/n₂²). R=1.097×10⁷ m⁻¹. Lyman:n→1, Balmer:n→2, Paschen:n→3
HEISENBERG UNCERTAINTY
Δx·Δp ≥ h/4π. Cannot know both position and momentum exactly. Fundamental limit
PAULI EXCLUSION
No two electrons can have same four quantum numbers. Maximum 2 electrons per orbital
DE BROGLIE WAVELENGTH
λ = h/mv. Wave-particle duality. For Bohr orbit: 2πr = nλ (standing wave)
PERIODIC TRENDS
Atomic radius decreases across period, increases down group. Ionization energy opposite trend

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Bohr's model only works for hydrogen and hydrogen-like ions
  • Quantum numbers define orbitals uniquely
  • Electron configuration determines chemical properties
  • Exceptions to Aufbau: Cr, Cu, Mo, Ag, Au, etc.
  • 4s orbital fills before 3d but loses electrons first
  • Heisenberg uncertainty is fundamental, not experimental limitation
  • de Broglie wavelength explains quantization in Bohr model
  • Noble gases have stable electron configurations

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Confusing n and l quantum numbers
  • Wrong Aufbau order (3d before 4s)
  • Forgetting exceptions (Cr, Cu configurations)
  • Incorrect electron removal order for ions
  • Mixing up Lyman, Balmer, Paschen series
  • Wrong Rydberg formula application
  • Confusing atomic radius trends
  • Forgetting that 4s loses electrons before 3d
