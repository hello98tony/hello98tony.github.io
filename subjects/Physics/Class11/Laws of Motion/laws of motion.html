Introduction to Newton's Laws

Newton's Laws of Motion form the foundation of classical mechanics. They describe the relationship between a body and the forces acting upon it, and its motion in response to those forces.

Sir Isaac Newton (1642-1727): Published "Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica" in 1687, where he formulated the three laws of motion that revolutionized physics.

First Law (Inertia)

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force.

If ΣF = 0, then a = 0
Second Law (Force)

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

F = ma
Third Law (Action-Reaction)

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces always occur in pairs.

F_AB = -F_BA

Newton's First Law: Law of Inertia

The First Law defines the concept of inertia - the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion.

Inertia: The property of matter that causes it to resist changes in velocity (both magnitude and direction).

Key Concepts of First Law

Concept Definition Example
Inertia of Rest Tendency to remain at rest Passenger thrown backward when bus starts
Inertia of Motion Tendency to remain in motion Passenger thrown forward when bus stops suddenly
Inertia of Direction Tendency to maintain direction Passenger thrown sideways when bus turns
No Net Force

ΣF = 0

Constant velocity or at rest

No acceleration

Net Force ≠ 0

ΣF ≠ 0

Acceleration occurs

Velocity changes

Mass & Inertia

More mass = More inertia

Inertia ∝ Mass

Measure of resistance to acceleration

Frame of Reference: First law holds only in inertial frames (non-accelerating frames). In non-inertial frames (accelerating frames), pseudo-forces appear.

Example: First Law Application

Why does a passenger tend to fall forward when a moving bus stops suddenly?

Explanation:

The passenger's body has inertia of motion. When the bus stops, the lower part of the body in contact with the seat stops, but the upper part continues moving forward due to inertia.

Newton's Second Law: Force & Acceleration

The Second Law quantifies the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration.

Mathematical Form: The net force acting on an object equals the rate of change of its momentum.

F = ma (for constant mass)
F = dp/dt = d(mv)/dt (general form)

Key Aspects of Second Law

Aspect Description Implication
Vector Nature F and a are vectors in same direction Acceleration direction = Net force direction
Instantaneous F = ma at each instant Force causes instantaneous acceleration
Principle of Superposition Forces add as vectors ΣF = F₁ + F₂ + F₃ + ...
Mass Measurement m = F/a (inertial mass) Mass resists acceleration

Applications of Second Law

Constant Force

Constant force produces constant acceleration.

a = F/m = constant

Example: Object falling under gravity

Variable Force

Variable force produces variable acceleration.

a(t) = F(t)/m

Example: Spring force, rocket propulsion

Impulse-Momentum

Force applied over time changes momentum.

J = ∫F dt = Δp

Impulse = Change in momentum

JEE ADVANCED

Variable Mass Systems

For systems with changing mass (like rockets):

F_ext + v_rel(dm/dt) = m(dv/dt)

Where v_rel is relative velocity of ejected mass

For rocket: Thrust = v_rel(dm/dt)

Example: Second Law Calculation

A force of 10 N acts on a mass of 2 kg. Find acceleration. If force is doubled, find new acceleration.

Solution:

a = F/m = 10/2 = 5 m/s²

When force doubled: F' = 20 N

a' = F'/m = 20/2 = 10 m/s²

Acceleration doubles when force doubles (mass constant)

Newton's Third Law: Action & Reaction

Forces always occur in pairs. If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B simultaneously exerts an equal and opposite force on object A.

Action-Reaction Pair: Two forces that are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different bodies.

F_AB = -F_BA

Characteristics of Action-Reaction Pairs

Characteristic Explanation Example
Equal Magnitude Both forces have same magnitude Foot pushes ground, ground pushes foot
Opposite Direction Forces act in opposite directions Rocket exhaust down, rocket goes up
Different Bodies Forces act on different objects Book on table: Book pushes table, table pushes book
Same Type Both forces are of same type Both gravitational, both contact, etc.
Simultaneous Both forces exist at same time No delay between action and reaction

Common Misconceptions:

  • Action and reaction forces do NOT cancel each other (they act on different bodies)
  • Third law pairs are always of the same type (both contact or both non-contact)
  • The stronger object does NOT exert a greater force
  • Action and reaction forces exist simultaneously

Real-World Examples

Walking/Running

Foot pushes ground backward

Ground pushes foot forward

Forward push causes motion

Rocket Propulsion

Rocket pushes exhaust down

Exhaust pushes rocket up

Conservation of momentum

Swimming

Swimmer pushes water backward

Water pushes swimmer forward

Forward motion results

Example: Third Law Application

A horse pulls a cart. The horse exerts a force on the cart. What is the reaction force?

Explanation:

Action: Horse pulls cart forward

Reaction: Cart pulls horse backward

These forces are equal and opposite, but act on different objects.

Types of Forces in Nature

Forces can be classified based on their nature and how they act on objects.

Force: A push or pull that can cause an object to accelerate. Measured in Newtons (N). 1 N = 1 kg·m/s²

Fundamental Forces

Force Relative Strength Range Mediator Examples
Gravitational 1 Infinite Graviton (hypothetical) Planetary motion, weight
Electromagnetic 10³⁶ Infinite Photon Friction, tension, normal
Strong Nuclear 10³⁸ 10⁻¹⁵ m Gluons Holds nucleus together
Weak Nuclear 10²⁵ 10⁻¹⁸ m W/Z bosons Radioactive decay

Common Forces in Mechanics

Weight (Gravity)

Force due to gravity.

W = mg

Always acts downward toward Earth's center

g = 9.8 m/s² (approx)

Normal Force

Contact force perpendicular to surface.

N = mg cosθ (on incline)

Prevents objects from passing through surfaces

Perpendicular to contact surface

Tension

Force transmitted through string/rope.

T = Force along string

Always pulls, never pushes

Massless, inextensible string idealization

Spring Force

Force exerted by stretched/compressed spring.

F = -kx (Hooke's Law)

k = spring constant

x = displacement from equilibrium

Applied Force

Direct push or pull by external agent.

F_applied

Example: Pushing a box, pulling a cart

Direction depends on application

Buoyant Force

Upward force in fluids.

F_b = ρVg

ρ = fluid density

V = volume displaced

Example: Free Body Diagram (FBD)

Draw FBD for a book resting on a table.

Solution:

1. Weight (W) downward: W = mg

2. Normal force (N) upward: N = mg (since book is at rest)

These two forces are equal and opposite, but NOT action-reaction pair (both act on same body - the book).

Friction

Friction is the force that opposes relative motion or tendency of relative motion between surfaces in contact.

Causes of Friction: Microscopic irregularities on surfaces, intermolecular forces, and adhesion between surfaces.

Types of Friction

Type Condition Formula Characteristics
Static Friction No relative motion f_s ≤ μ_sN Self-adjusting, prevents motion
Kinetic Friction Relative motion exists f_k = μ_kN Constant, opposes motion
Rolling Friction Rolling without slipping f_r = μ_rN Least among three types

Key Properties:

  • Friction is independent of area of contact (for given normal force)
  • Static friction coefficient (μ_s) > Kinetic friction coefficient (μ_k)
  • Friction depends on nature of surfaces (roughness, materials)
  • Friction is parallel to surface, opposite to intended motion
  • Rolling friction < Sliding friction < Static friction

Angle of Friction & Repose

Angle of Friction (λ)

Angle between resultant contact force and normal.

tan λ = μ

For limiting friction: tan λ = μ_s

For kinetic friction: tan λ = μ_k

Angle of Repose (θ)

Maximum angle before object slides down incline.

μ_s = tan θ

On verge of sliding: mg sinθ = μ_s mg cosθ

Thus: μ_s = tan θ

JEE ADVANCED

Friction in Inclined Plane Problems

For object on incline with friction:

Upward motion: ma = mg sinθ + μ_k mg cosθ
Downward motion: ma = mg sinθ - μ_k mg cosθ

For no motion: mg sinθ ≤ μ_s mg cosθ

Example: Friction Calculation

A 5 kg block rests on horizontal surface with μ_s = 0.4. What minimum horizontal force is needed to start motion?

Solution:

Normal force N = mg = 5 × 9.8 = 49 N

Maximum static friction f_s(max) = μ_sN = 0.4 × 49 = 19.6 N

Minimum force to start motion = 19.6 N

Once moving, kinetic friction f_k = μ_kN (μ_k < μ_s)

Equilibrium of Forces

A body is in equilibrium when the net force and net torque acting on it are zero.

Conditions for Equilibrium:

  1. Translational equilibrium: Net force = 0 (ΣF = 0)
  2. Rotational equilibrium: Net torque = 0 (Στ = 0)

Types of Equilibrium

Type Condition Stability Example
Static At rest, no motion Stable Book on table
Dynamic Moving with constant velocity Stable Car on highway at constant speed
Unstable Small displacement causes motion Unstable Pencil standing on tip
Neutral Displacement doesn't change equilibrium Neutral Sphere on flat surface

Solving Equilibrium Problems

Free Body Diagram (FBD)

Essential first step in equilibrium problems.

Steps:

  1. Isolate object
  2. Show all forces acting
  3. Choose coordinate system
  4. Resolve forces into components
Lami's Theorem

For three concurrent forces in equilibrium.

F₁/sinα = F₂/sinβ = F₃/sinγ

α, β, γ are angles opposite to forces F₁, F₂, F₃

Useful for three-force equilibrium problems

Method of Components

Most general method for equilibrium.

ΣF_x = 0, ΣF_y = 0

For 3D: ΣF_x = 0, ΣF_y = 0, ΣF_z = 0

Also Στ = 0 for rotational equilibrium

IMPORTANT

Concurrent vs Non-concurrent Forces

Concurrent forces: Lines of action meet at a point. Only need ΣF = 0 for equilibrium.

Non-concurrent forces: Lines of action don't meet. Need both ΣF = 0 and Στ = 0.

Example: Equilibrium Problem

A 10 kg mass hangs from two strings making angles 30° and 60° with horizontal. Find tensions in strings.

Solution:

Weight W = mg = 10 × 9.8 = 98 N downward

Let T₁ and T₂ be tensions

Horizontal equilibrium: T₁ cos30° = T₂ cos60°

Vertical equilibrium: T₁ sin30° + T₂ sin60° = 98

Solving: T₁ = 49 N, T₂ = 84.87 N

Problem Solving Strategies

Effective techniques for solving Laws of Motion problems in JEE and NEET.

Type 1: Single Body Problems

Steps:

  1. Draw Free Body Diagram (FBD)
  2. Identify all forces acting
  3. Apply ΣF = ma in relevant directions
  4. Solve for unknowns

Tip: Choose coordinate system wisely (along and perpendicular to motion)

Type 2: Connected Bodies

Steps:

  1. Draw separate FBD for each body
  2. Apply ΣF = ma to each body
  3. Consider constraints (same acceleration for connected bodies)
  4. Solve simultaneous equations

Tip: Tension is same throughout massless string

Type 3: Inclined Plane Problems

Steps:

  1. Choose axes: x along incline, y perpendicular
  2. Resolve weight into components
  3. Account for friction if present
  4. Apply ΣF_x = ma_x, ΣF_y = 0

Tip: Normal force N = mg cosθ on frictionless incline

JEE ADVANCED TIP

Constraint Relations

For connected bodies (pulleys, wedges):

• Length of string is constant ⇒ Relation between accelerations

• Bodies in contact move together ⇒ Same acceleration

• Use geometry to relate displacements, velocities, accelerations

• Differentiate constraint equations to get acceleration relations

NEWTON'S FIRST LAW
Law of Inertia: Object maintains state of motion unless net force acts. ΣF = 0 ⇒ a = 0
NEWTON'S SECOND LAW
F = ma. Acceleration ∝ Force, ∝ 1/mass. Force causes change in momentum: F = dp/dt
NEWTON'S THIRD LAW
Action-Reaction: F_AB = -F_BA. Forces occur in pairs on different bodies. Never cancel each other.
INERTIA
Resistance to change in motion. Inertia ∝ mass. More mass = more inertia = harder to accelerate.
FRICTION
Opposes relative motion. Static (f_s ≤ μ_sN) and Kinetic (f_k = μ_kN). μ_s > μ_k > μ_r
EQUILIBRIUM
ΣF = 0 and Στ = 0. Static (at rest) or Dynamic (constant velocity). Solve using FBD.
IMPULSE
J = ∫F dt = Δp. Force applied over time changes momentum. Area under F-t graph.
FREE BODY DIAGRAM
Essential tool. Isolate object, show all forces, choose coordinates, resolve components.

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Newton's First Law defines inertial frames of reference
  • Second Law is valid only in inertial frames (non-accelerating)
  • Third Law forces always act on different bodies and never cancel
  • Mass is measure of inertia, weight is force due to gravity
  • Static friction is self-adjusting up to maximum value μ_sN
  • Normal force is perpendicular to surface, adjusts to prevent penetration
  • Tension in massless string is same throughout
  • Free Body Diagram is the most important problem-solving tool

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Confusing mass (scalar) with weight (vector force)
  • Thinking action-reaction forces cancel (they act on different bodies)
  • Forgetting that friction opposes intended motion, not necessarily actual motion
  • Not drawing proper Free Body Diagrams
  • Confusing coefficient of static friction (μ_s) with kinetic friction (μ_k)
  • Assuming normal force always equals weight (only on horizontal surfaces)
  • Forgetting to consider all forces in FBD (especially tension in multiple strings)
  • Applying F = ma in non-inertial frames without pseudo-forces
SUPER TIP

Problem Solving Framework

STEP 1: Read problem carefully, identify given quantities and what's asked

STEP 2: Draw clear Free Body Diagram for each object

STEP 3: Choose coordinate system (usually along and perpendicular to motion)

STEP 4: Apply ΣF = ma in each direction for each object

STEP 5: Solve equations simultaneously

STEP 6: Check units, magnitude, and direction of answers
