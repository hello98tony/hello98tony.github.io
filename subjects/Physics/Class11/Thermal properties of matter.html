Introduction to Thermal Physics

Thermal physics is the study of heat, temperature, and their relation to energy and work. It encompasses thermodynamics, kinetic theory, and statistical mechanics.

Temperature: Measure of average kinetic energy of molecules in a substance. Determines direction of heat flow (hot → cold).

Heat: Energy in transit due to temperature difference. Measured in joules (J).

Heat Transfer

Conduction, convection, radiation - three modes of heat transfer with different mechanisms.

Q = kAΔT/ℓ
Q = σeAT⁴
Thermal Expansion

Increase in dimensions with temperature. Different coefficients for solids, liquids, gases.

ΔL = αLΔT
ΔV = γVΔT
Calorimetry

Measurement of heat transfer. Based on principle of conservation of energy.

Q = mcΔT
Q = mL

Temperature & Heat

Temperature and heat are fundamental but distinct concepts in thermal physics.

Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics: If two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This establishes temperature as a fundamental property.

Temperature Scales

0°C / 32°F / 273K
25°C / 77°F / 298K
100°C / 212°F / 373K
Scale Fixed Points Conversion Formulas Absolute Zero
Celsius (°C) 0°C (ice point), 100°C (steam point) °C = K - 273.15
°C = 5/9(°F - 32)		 -273.15°C
Fahrenheit (°F) 32°F (ice point), 212°F (steam point) °F = 9/5°C + 32
°F = 9/5(K - 273) + 32		 -459.67°F
Kelvin (K) 273.15K (ice point), 373.15K (steam point) K = °C + 273.15
K = 5/9(°F - 32) + 273		 0K

Key Differences:

  • Temperature: Intensive property (independent of mass), measures molecular KE
  • Heat: Extensive property (depends on mass), energy in transit
  • Internal Energy: Total energy (KE + PE) of all molecules
  • Thermal Equilibrium: No net heat flow between objects at same temperature

Thermometers & Measurement

Mercury Thermometer

Based on thermal expansion of mercury

Range: -39°C to 357°C

Advantages: Linear expansion, visible

Disadvantages: Toxic, breakable

Formula: ΔL = αLΔT

Resistance Thermometer

Based on temperature dependence of resistance

Rt = R₀[1 + α(T - T₀)]

Platinum commonly used

High accuracy, wide range

Used in laboratories

Thermocouple

Based on Seebeck effect

Two dissimilar metals produce voltage with ΔT

E = α(T₁ - T₂)

Wide range: -200°C to 2000°C

Fast response, rugged

Ideal Gas Thermometer

Based on ideal gas equation: PV = nRT

Constant volume gas thermometer:

T = (273.16K) × (P/Pₜᵣ)

Where Pₜᵣ is pressure at triple point of water

Most accurate temperature measurement

Independent of gas type at low pressure

Example: Temperature Conversion

Convert 37°C (human body temperature) to Fahrenheit and Kelvin.

Solution:

To Fahrenheit: °F = (9/5 × °C) + 32 = (9/5 × 37) + 32 = 66.6 + 32 = 98.6°F

To Kelvin: K = °C + 273.15 = 37 + 273.15 = 310.15K

Normal body temperature: 37°C = 98.6°F = 310.15K

Thermal Expansion

Most materials expand when heated and contract when cooled, due to increased molecular vibrations.

Coefficient of Linear Expansion (α): Fractional change in length per degree temperature change.

α = ΔL/(LΔT)

Where ΔL = change in length, L = original length, ΔT = temperature change

Types of Thermal Expansion

Type Formula Coefficient Applications
Linear ΔL = αLΔT
L' = L(1 + αΔT)		 α (per °C or K) Railway tracks, bridges
Areal/Superficial ΔA = βAΔT
β = 2α		 β = 2α Metal plates, bimetallic strips
Volume (Solids/Liquids) ΔV = γVΔT
γ = 3α		 γ = 3α Thermometers, expansion joints
Volume (Gases) V/T = constant (Charles' Law) 1/273 per °C Hot air balloons, gas laws

Anomalous Expansion of Water:

  • Water contracts from 0°C to 4°C (density increases)
  • Water expands from 4°C to 100°C (normal behavior)
  • Maximum density at 4°C: 1000 kg/m³
  • Ice less dense than water (floats)
  • Important for aquatic life in winter

Practical Applications

Bimetallic Strip

Two metals with different α bonded together

Heating causes bending

Radius of curvature: R = t/[3(α₂ - α₁)ΔT]

Applications: Thermostats, thermometers, circuit breakers

Expansion Gaps

Gaps left in railway tracks, bridges

Allow for expansion in summer

Gap calculation: ΔL = αLΔT

Example: Steel rail, L = 12m, ΔT = 40°C, α = 1.2×10⁻⁵/°C

ΔL = 1.2×10⁻⁵ × 12 × 40 = 0.00576m ≈ 5.8mm

Riveting & Shrinking

Hot rivets inserted, contract on cooling

Tight joint created

Metal rims on wooden wheels: Heat rim, place on wheel, cool

Rim contracts for tight fit

Industrial joining technique

NEET IMPORTANT

Time Period of Pendulum

For a simple pendulum: T = 2π√(L/g)

With temperature change: L' = L(1 + αΔT)

New time period: T' = T√(1 + αΔT) ≈ T(1 + αΔT/2)

Fractional change: ΔT/T = (1/2)αΔT

Clock loses time in summer, gains in winter

Example: Linear Expansion

A steel rod is 2m long at 20°C. Find its length at 120°C. α = 1.2×10⁻⁵/°C.

Solution:

ΔT = 120 - 20 = 100°C

ΔL = αLΔT = 1.2×10⁻⁵ × 2 × 100 = 0.0024m = 2.4mm

New length L' = L + ΔL = 2 + 0.0024 = 2.0024m

Alternatively: L' = L(1 + αΔT) = 2(1 + 1.2×10⁻⁵×100) = 2(1.0012) = 2.0024m

Calorimetry & Specific Heat

Calorimetry is the science of measuring heat changes in physical and chemical processes.

Principle of Calorimetry: Heat lost by hot body = Heat gained by cold body (assuming no heat loss to surroundings).

m₁c₁(T₁ - T_f) = m₂c₂(T_f - T₂) + ...

Where T_f = final equilibrium temperature

Heat Capacity & Specific Heat

Quantity Definition Formula Units Significance
Heat Capacity (C) Heat required to raise temperature by 1°C C = Q/ΔT J/°C Extensive property
Specific Heat (c) Heat per unit mass per °C c = Q/(mΔT) J/kg·K Intensive property
Molar Specific Heat Heat per mole per °C C_m = Q/(nΔT) J/mol·K For gases: C_v, C_p
Water Equivalent Mass of water with same heat capacity W = mc kg Simplifies calculations

Specific Heat Values (Approximate):

  • Water: 4186 J/kg·K (highest common substance)
  • Ice: 2100 J/kg·K
  • Aluminum: 900 J/kg·K
  • Iron: 450 J/kg·K
  • Copper: 385 J/kg·K
  • Mercury: 140 J/kg·K
  • Lead: 130 J/kg·K (lowest common solid)

Water's high specific heat makes it excellent for cooling systems and climate regulation.

Latent Heat & Phase Changes

Latent Heat of Fusion

Heat for solid ↔ liquid phase change

Q = mL_f

For water: L_f = 3.34×10⁵ J/kg

Temperature constant during phase change

Melting ice at 0°C requires heat

Latent Heat of Vaporization

Heat for liquid ↔ vapor phase change

Q = mL_v

For water: L_v = 22.6×10⁵ J/kg

Much larger than L_f (≈ 7 times)

Boiling water at 100°C requires heat

Sublimation

Direct solid ↔ vapor phase change

Examples: Dry ice (CO₂), naphthalene

Latent heat of sublimation: L_s = L_f + L_v

Cooling effect used in refrigeration

Freeze drying of food

JEE ADVANCED

Dulong-Petit Law

For solid elements at room temperature:

Molar heat capacity ≈ 3R ≈ 25 J/mol·K

Where R = 8.314 J/mol·K (gas constant)

Based on classical theory: Each atom has 6 degrees of freedom (3 KE + 3 PE)

Energy per mole = 3RT, C_v = dU/dT = 3R

Valid at high temperatures, fails at low temperatures

Example: Calorimetry Problem

50g ice at 0°C mixed with 100g water at 40°C. Find final temperature. (L_f = 80 cal/g, c_water = 1 cal/g°C)

Solution:

Heat required to melt ice: Q₁ = mL_f = 50×80 = 4000 cal

Heat available from water cooling to 0°C: Q₂ = mcΔT = 100×1×40 = 4000 cal

Since Q₁ = Q₂, all ice melts, final temperature = 0°C

Mixture has 150g water at 0°C

Exactly enough heat to melt ice, none left to raise temperature.

Heat Transfer Mechanisms

Heat transfer occurs through three mechanisms: conduction, convection, and radiation.

Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction: Rate of heat flow through a material is proportional to the temperature gradient and cross-sectional area.

dQ/dt = -kA(dT/dx)

Where k = thermal conductivity, A = area, dT/dx = temperature gradient

Modes of Heat Transfer

Mode Mechanism Formula Examples
Conduction Molecular collisions (solids) or electron movement (metals) Q/t = kAΔT/ℓ Metal spoon in hot soup, building insulation
Convection Bulk movement of fluid (liquid/gas) Q/t = hAΔT Sea breeze, room heaters, boiling water
Radiation Electromagnetic waves, no medium required Q/t = σeAT⁴ Sunlight, infrared heaters, thermal imaging

Thermal Conductivity Values (W/m·K):

  • Silver: 429 (best conductor)
  • Copper: 401
  • Aluminum: 237
  • Iron: 80
  • Glass: 0.8
  • Water: 0.6
  • Wood: 0.1
  • Air: 0.026 (good insulator)
  • Styrofoam: 0.033 (excellent insulator)

Conduction Through Composite Slabs

Series Combination

Slabs in series (different materials end-to-end)

Total thermal resistance: R = R₁ + R₂ + ...
R = ℓ/(kA) for each slab

Heat current same through all slabs

Temperature drops across each slab

ΔT_total = (Q/t)(R₁ + R₂ + ...)
Parallel Combination

Slabs in parallel (side-by-side)

Total thermal resistance: 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + ...

Temperature difference same across all slabs

Heat currents add: Q/t = (Q/t)₁ + (Q/t)₂ + ...

Equivalent conductivity: k_eq = (k₁A₁ + k₂A₂ + ...)/(A₁ + A₂ + ...)

Radial Conduction

Through cylindrical or spherical shells

Cylinder: Q/t = 2πkℓ(T₁ - T₂)/ln(r₂/r₁)

Sphere: Q/t = 4πkr₁r₂(T₁ - T₂)/(r₂ - r₁)

Applications: insulated pipes, spherical containers

Critical thickness of insulation for cylinders

NEET IMPORTANT

Newton's Law of Cooling

Rate of cooling ∝ temperature difference with surroundings:

dT/dt = -k(T - T_s)

Where T = object temperature, T_s = surroundings temperature

Integrated form: (T - T_s) = (T₀ - T_s)e^(-kt)

Applications: Determining time of death, cooling of hot objects

Valid for moderate temperature differences (≈ 30°C)

Example: Conduction Through Wall

A brick wall (k = 0.8 W/m·K) is 20cm thick with area 10m². Inside temperature 25°C, outside 5°C. Find heat loss per hour.

Solution:

ΔT = 25 - 5 = 20°C, ℓ = 0.2m, A = 10m²

Q/t = kAΔT/ℓ = 0.8×10×20/0.2 = 800 W

Heat loss in 1 hour = 800 × 3600 = 2.88×10⁶ J

In kilowatt-hours: 800 W × 1 h = 0.8 kWh

Significant heat loss through walls highlights need for insulation.

Kinetic Theory of Gases

The kinetic theory explains macroscopic properties of gases (pressure, temperature) in terms of microscopic molecular motion.

Basic Postulates:

  • Gas consists of large number of molecules in random motion
  • Molecules are point masses with negligible volume
  • No intermolecular forces except during collisions
  • Collisions are elastic and instantaneous
  • Newton's laws apply to molecular motion

Kinetic Theory Formulas

Relation Formula Explanation
Pressure P = (1/3)ρv² = (1/3)(Nm/V)v² Pressure due to molecular collisions
RMS Speed v_rms = √(3kT/m) = √(3RT/M) Root mean square speed of molecules
Average KE (1/2)mv²_avg = (3/2)kT Average kinetic energy per molecule
Internal Energy (monatomic) U = (3/2)nRT = (3/2)NkT Total kinetic energy of all molecules
Mean Free Path λ = 1/(√2πd²n) Average distance between collisions

Gas Laws from Kinetic Theory:

  • Boyle's Law: P ∝ 1/V (at constant T) - From P = (1/3)ρv², with v constant
  • Charles' Law: V ∝ T (at constant P) - From v² ∝ T, so V ∝ T to keep P constant
  • Gay-Lussac's Law: P ∝ T (at constant V) - Direct from P = (1/3)ρv² with v² ∝ T
  • Avogadro's Law: Equal volumes at same T,P contain equal molecules
  • Dalton's Law: Total pressure = sum of partial pressures

Molecular Speed Distributions

Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

Distribution of molecular speeds at given T

Most probable speed: v_p = √(2kT/m)

Average speed: v_avg = √(8kT/πm)

RMS speed: v_rms = √(3kT/m)

Relation: v_p : v_avg : v_rms = 1 : 1.128 : 1.225

Degrees of Freedom

Number of independent ways energy can be stored

Monatomic gas: f = 3 (translation only)

Diatomic gas: f = 5 (3 translation + 2 rotation)

Polyatomic gas: f = 6 (3 translation + 3 rotation)

Law of equipartition: Energy = (1/2)kT per degree

Specific Heats of Gases

Molar specific heat at constant volume: C_v = (f/2)R

Molar specific heat at constant pressure: C_p = C_v + R

Ratio γ = C_p/C_v = 1 + 2/f

Monatomic: γ = 5/3 ≈ 1.67

Diatomic: γ = 7/5 = 1.4

Polyatomic: γ = 4/3 ≈ 1.33

JEE ADVANCED

Mean Free Path & Collision Frequency

Mean free path: λ = kT/(√2πd²P)

Where d = molecular diameter, P = pressure

At STP: λ ≈ 6×10⁻⁸ m for air molecules

Collision frequency: Z = v_avg/λ

At STP: Z ≈ 10⁹ collisions per second

Collision cross-section: σ = πd²

Important for reaction rates, diffusion

Example: RMS Speed Calculation

Calculate RMS speed of oxygen molecules at 27°C. (M = 32g/mol = 0.032kg/mol, R = 8.314 J/mol·K)

Solution:

T = 27 + 273 = 300K

v_rms = √(3RT/M) = √(3×8.314×300/0.032)

= √(7482.6/0.032) = √(233831.25) = 483.6 m/s

Oxygen molecules move at about 1700 km/h at room temperature!

Problem Solving Strategies

Systematic approach to solve thermal physics problems in JEE and NEET.

Type 1: Calorimetry Problems

Steps:

  1. Identify all substances and their masses
  2. Determine initial temperatures and phases
  3. Apply: Heat lost = Heat gained
  4. Include phase changes if applicable
  5. Solve for unknown temperature/mass

Tip: Check if final temperature causes phase changes

Type 2: Thermal Expansion

Steps:

  1. Identify type of expansion (linear, area, volume)
  2. Choose correct formula and coefficient
  3. Apply: ΔL = αLΔT, ΔA = βAΔT, ΔV = γVΔT
  4. Consider composite systems (bimetallic strips)
  5. Check units (temperature in Kelvin or Celsius)

Tip: For area: β = 2α, for volume: γ = 3α

Type 3: Heat Transfer

Steps:

  1. Identify mode (conduction, convection, radiation)
  2. Use appropriate formula
  3. For conduction: Q/t = kAΔT/ℓ
  4. For composite walls: use thermal resistances
  5. For radiation: Stefan-Boltzmann law

Tip: Series: R_total = R₁+R₂, Parallel: 1/R_total = 1/R₁+1/R₂

JEE/NEET TIP

Common Problem Patterns

1. Mixing problems: Heat lost by hot = Heat gained by cold

2. Phase change problems: Include latent heat Q = mL

3. Linear expansion: ΔL = αLΔT, pendulum clocks

4. Composite conductors: Series/parallel thermal resistances

5. Gas laws: PV = nRT, combined gas law

6. Kinetic theory: v_rms = √(3RT/M), KE = (3/2)kT

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Heat is energy in transit, temperature measures average molecular KE
  • Specific heat of water is highest (4186 J/kg·K) - regulates climate
  • Water has anomalous expansion: maximum density at 4°C, ice less dense than water
  • During phase change, temperature remains constant despite heat input
  • Heat transfer: conduction (solids), convection (fluids), radiation (EM waves)
  • Thermal conductivity: metals good conductors, gases good insulators
  • Kinetic theory: v_rms ∝ √T, pressure due to molecular collisions
  • Gas laws: PV = nRT, with R = 8.314 J/mol·K

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Confusing heat (energy) with temperature (intensity)
  • Forgetting latent heat in calorimetry problems with phase changes
  • Using wrong expansion coefficient (α, β, γ for linear, area, volume)
  • Not converting temperature to Kelvin in gas law calculations
  • Applying ideal gas equation to real gases without correction
  • Confusing conduction, convection, and radiation mechanisms
  • Forgetting that radiation doesn't require a medium
  • Mixing up specific heat values for different materials
