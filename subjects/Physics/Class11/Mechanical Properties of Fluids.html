Introduction to Fluid Mechanics

Fluid mechanics is the branch of physics that studies fluids (liquids and gases) and the forces on them. It has two main branches: fluid statics (fluids at rest) and fluid dynamics (fluids in motion).

Fluid: A substance that deforms continuously under the application of shear stress, no matter how small. Both liquids and gases are fluids.

Fluid Statics

Study of fluids at rest. Deals with pressure, buoyancy, and hydrostatic equilibrium.

P = F/A
P = ρgh
Viscosity

Internal friction of a fluid. Resistance to flow. Depends on temperature and nature of fluid.

F = ηA(dv/dx)
η = (πPr⁴)/(8Vl)
Surface Tension

Tendency of liquid surfaces to shrink into minimum surface area. Caused by cohesive forces.

T = F/l
P = 2T/R

Basic Properties of Fluids

Property Definition SI Unit Important Points
Density (ρ) Mass per unit volume kg/m³ ρ = m/V; Water: 1000 kg/m³
Pressure (P) Force per unit area Pascal (Pa) P = F/A; 1 Pa = 1 N/m²
Viscosity (η) Internal friction/resistance to flow Pa·s or Poise η decreases with temperature for liquids
Surface Tension (T) Force per unit length N/m Causes capillary action, droplets
Compressibility Volume change with pressure Pa⁻¹ Liquids: nearly incompressible

Pressure & Hydrostatics

Fluid statics deals with fluids at rest. The key concept is pressure, which increases with depth.

Pressure: Force per unit area. In fluids, pressure acts equally in all directions at a point.

P = F/A

Hydrostatic Pressure

P = P₀ + ρgh

Where:
P = Pressure at depth h
P₀ = Atmospheric pressure (1.01 × 10⁵ Pa)
ρ = Density of fluid
g = Acceleration due to gravity (9.8 m/s²)
h = Depth below surface

Pascal's Law: Pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every portion of the fluid and to the walls of the containing vessel.

F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂

Applications: Hydraulic lift, hydraulic brakes, hydraulic press.

Archimedes' Principle & Buoyancy

Buoyant Force
F_B = ρ_f V_f g

Where:
ρ_f = Density of fluid
V_f = Volume of fluid displaced
g = Acceleration due to gravity

Buoyant force = Weight of fluid displaced

Floatation Conditions

Sinking: Weight > Buoyant force
Floating: Weight = Buoyant force
Rising: Weight < Buoyant force

Fraction submerged = ρ_object/ρ_fluid

For floating body

Center of Buoyancy

Center of mass of displaced fluid

For stable equilibrium:
Center of buoyancy above center of gravity

Metacentric height determines stability

Important for ship design

Atmospheric Pressure & Barometers

Standard atmospheric pressure: 1 atm = 1.013 × 10⁵ Pa = 760 mm of Hg

Mercury barometer: P = ρgh where h is height of mercury column

Manometers: U-tube manometer measures pressure difference

P₁ - P₂ = ρg(h₂ - h₁)
Example: Hydrostatic Pressure

Calculate pressure at depth of 10 m in water. Density of water = 1000 kg/m³, atmospheric pressure = 1.01 × 10⁵ Pa.

Solution:

P = P₀ + ρgh

P = 1.01 × 10⁵ + (1000 × 9.8 × 10)

P = 1.01 × 10⁵ + 0.98 × 10⁵ = 1.99 × 10⁵ Pa

Pressure increases by approximately 1 atm for every 10 m depth in water.

Viscosity & Fluid Flow

Viscosity is the internal friction of a fluid that opposes relative motion between different layers.

Newton's Law of Viscosity: The viscous force between two layers of fluid is proportional to the velocity gradient and area of contact.

F = -ηA(dv/dx)

Where η is coefficient of viscosity, A is area, dv/dx is velocity gradient.

Types of Fluid Flow

Type Description Reynolds Number Examples
Streamline/Laminar Smooth, orderly flow with parallel layers Re < 2000 Honey flow, slow river
Turbulent Chaotic, irregular flow with eddies Re > 4000 Fast river, blood in large arteries
Transitional Between laminar and turbulent 2000 < Re < 4000 Moderate pipe flow

Reynolds Number (Re): Dimensionless number predicting flow pattern

Re = ρvD/η

Where:
ρ = Density of fluid
v = Velocity of flow
D = Characteristic dimension (diameter for pipe)
η = Coefficient of viscosity

Re < 2000: Laminar flow
Re > 4000: Turbulent flow

Poiseuille's Equation

For laminar flow through a cylindrical pipe:

V = (πPr⁴)/(8ηl)

Where:
V = Volume flow rate
P = Pressure difference
r = Radius of pipe
η = Coefficient of viscosity
l = Length of pipe

Stoke's Law

Viscous force on a sphere moving in fluid:

F = 6πηrv

Where:
η = Coefficient of viscosity
r = Radius of sphere
v = Velocity of sphere

Terminal velocity: v_t = 2r²(ρ_s - ρ_f)g/(9η)

Temperature Dependence

For liquids: η decreases with temperature
Viscosity ∝ 1/T (approximately)

For gases: η increases with temperature
Viscosity ∝ √T (approximately)

Reason: In liquids, intermolecular forces dominate. In gases, molecular collisions dominate.

Practical Applications

High viscosity: Lubricants, syrups, paints

Low viscosity: Water, gasoline, air

Blood viscosity: ~4× water, important for circulation

Engine oil: Multi-grade (10W-40) for temperature variations

Example: Terminal Velocity

A steel ball of radius 2 mm and density 7800 kg/m³ falls through water (η = 1×10⁻³ Pa·s, ρ = 1000 kg/m³). Find terminal velocity.

Solution:

Terminal velocity formula: v_t = 2r²(ρ_s - ρ_f)g/(9η)

v_t = 2×(0.002)²×(7800-1000)×9.8/(9×0.001)

v_t = 2×4×10⁻⁶×6800×9.8/(9×10⁻³) = 0.059 m/s

The ball falls slowly due to water viscosity.

Surface Tension & Capillarity

Surface tension is the property of liquid surfaces to behave like stretched elastic membranes.

Surface Tension (T): Force per unit length acting perpendicular to an imaginary line drawn on the liquid surface.

T = F/l

SI Unit: N/m (also J/m² as surface energy)

Surface Tension Phenomena

Excess Pressure

Inside droplet: P_in - P_out = 2T/R

Inside bubble: P_in - P_out = 4T/R

Inside soap bubble: 4T/R (two surfaces)

Where R is radius of curvature

Angle of Contact

Angle between tangent to liquid surface and solid surface

θ < 90°: Liquid wets surface (water-glass)

θ > 90°: Liquid doesn't wet (mercury-glass)

θ = 0°: Complete wetting

θ = 180°: No wetting (theoretical)

Capillary Action

Rise or fall of liquid in narrow tube

h = 2T cosθ/(ρgr)

Where:
h = Capillary rise/fall
T = Surface tension
θ = Angle of contact
ρ = Density
r = Radius of tube

Factors Affecting Surface Tension:

  • Temperature: T decreases with temperature, becomes zero at critical temperature
  • Impurities: Highly soluble impurities increase T, sparingly soluble decrease T
  • Nature of liquid: Water: 0.072 N/m, Mercury: 0.465 N/m
  • Surfactants: Detergents, soaps reduce surface tension dramatically

Applications of Surface Tension

Application Principle Example
Spherical droplets Minimum surface area for given volume Raindrops, soap bubbles
Capillary action Adhesion > Cohesion causes rise Plants drawing water, ink in blotting paper
Detergents Reduce surface tension Cleaning, emulsification
Floating needle Surface tension supports weight Steel needle on water
Medical applications Surfactants in lungs Respiratory distress syndrome
NEET IMPORTANT

Surface Energy

Work done in increasing surface area by unit amount:

Surface energy = T × ΔA

Where T = Surface tension, ΔA = Increase in area

Relation: Surface tension = Surface energy per unit area

Dimensions: [MT⁻²], Units: J/m²

Example: Capillary Rise

Water rises to height of 5 cm in a capillary tube. If tube radius is halved, find new height. (Assume θ = 0°)

Solution:

Capillary rise formula: h = 2T cosθ/(ρgr)

h ∝ 1/r (other factors constant)

If radius halved: r' = r/2, then h' = 2h

New height = 2 × 5 cm = 10 cm

Capillary rise inversely proportional to tube radius.

Bernoulli's Theorem & Fluid Dynamics

Bernoulli's theorem relates pressure, velocity, and elevation for an ideal fluid in steady flow.

Bernoulli's Equation: For steady, incompressible, non-viscous flow along a streamline:

P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant

Where:
P = Pressure energy per unit volume
½ρv² = Kinetic energy per unit volume
ρgh = Potential energy per unit volume

Assumptions of Bernoulli's Theorem

  • Fluid is ideal (non-viscous)
  • Flow is steady (velocity at a point constant)
  • Flow is incompressible (density constant)
  • Flow is irrotational (no eddies)
  • No energy loss due to friction

Applications of Bernoulli's Theorem

Venturimeter

Measures flow rate in pipes

v₁ = A₂√[2(P₁-P₂)/(ρ(A₁²-A₂²))]

Volume flow rate: Q = A₁v₁

Constriction increases velocity, decreases pressure

Aerofoil/Lift

Airplane wing design

Curved upper surface: longer path, faster air, lower pressure

Flat lower surface: slower air, higher pressure

Pressure difference creates lift

Lift ∝ ½ρv²A
Atomizer/Spray

Perfume bottle, carburetor

Air blown over tube creates low pressure

Liquid rises due to pressure difference

Breaks into fine droplets

Same principle: Bunsen burner, paint gun

Torricelli's Theorem: Velocity of efflux from an orifice at depth h:

v = √(2gh)

Same as velocity acquired by free fall from height h

Range of liquid jet: R = 2√(h(H-h))

Maximum range when orifice at middle: h = H/2, R_max = H

Equation of Continuity

For incompressible fluid in steady flow through a pipe:

A₁v₁ = A₂v₂ = constant

Where A is cross-sectional area, v is fluid velocity

Product Av = volume flow rate (Q)

Where cross-section decreases, velocity increases

JEE ADVANCED

Modified Bernoulli Equation

For real (viscous) fluids with energy loss:

P₁/ρg + v₁²/2g + h₁ = P₂/ρg + v₂²/2g + h₂ + h_L

Where h_L = head loss due to friction

Darcy-Weisbach equation: h_L = f(L/D)(v²/2g)

Where f = friction factor, L = pipe length, D = diameter

Example: Bernoulli's Application

Water flows through a horizontal pipe with cross-section 0.1 m² at 2 m/s. If pipe narrows to 0.05 m², find pressure difference. (ρ = 1000 kg/m³)

Solution:

From continuity: A₁v₁ = A₂v₂

v₂ = (A₁v₁)/A₂ = (0.1×2)/0.05 = 4 m/s

From Bernoulli (horizontal, so h constant):

P₁ + ½ρv₁² = P₂ + ½ρv₂²

P₁ - P₂ = ½ρ(v₂² - v₁²) = ½×1000×(16-4) = 6000 Pa

Pressure decreases where velocity increases.

Real-World Applications

Fluid mechanics principles have numerous practical applications in daily life and technology.

Medical Applications

Blood Flow & Pressure

Poiseuille's law: Flow ∝ r⁴ (radius to fourth power)

Small change in artery radius greatly affects flow

Atherosclerosis reduces radius, increases blood pressure

Sphygmomanometer measures blood pressure

Respiratory System

Surfactants reduce surface tension in alveoli

Prevents collapse during exhalation

Respiratory distress syndrome in premature babies

Bernoulli effect in cough, sneeze

Intravenous Therapy

Height of IV bag controls pressure

P = ρgh provides pressure for flow

Flow rate controlled by clamp (adjusts radius)

Viscosity affects flow rate

Engineering Applications

Application Principle Used Description
Hydraulic Machines Pascal's Law Press, lift, brakes, jacks - force multiplication
Aerodynamics Bernoulli's Theorem Airplane wings, race cars, spoilers
Chemical Industry Viscosity & Flow Pipeline design, mixing, pumping
Meteorology Fluid Dynamics Weather patterns, wind flow, storms
Civil Engineering Hydrostatics Dam design, water supply, drainage

Everyday Examples:

  • Shower curtain: Bernoulli effect pulls curtain inward
  • Baseball curveball: Magnus effect due to spin
  • Teapot effect: Liquid adheres to spout surface
  • Walking on mud: Pressure = weight/area, wide shoes reduce pressure
  • Syringe: Pascal's law - small force creates high pressure
  • Hot air balloon: Buoyancy - hot air less dense than cold air
  • Submarine: Ballast tanks control buoyancy

Problem Solving Strategies

Systematic approach to solve fluid mechanics problems in JEE and NEET.

Type 1: Hydrostatic Problems

Steps:

  1. Identify fluid and its density
  2. Determine depth/height differences
  3. Use P = P₀ + ρgh
  4. Consider Pascal's law if force multiplication
  5. Check units (Pa, atm, mm Hg conversions)

Tip: Pressure depends only on depth, not container shape

Type 2: Buoyancy Problems

Steps:

  1. Calculate weight of object
  2. Calculate buoyant force = ρ_f V_f g
  3. Apply equilibrium condition
  4. For floating: weight = buoyant force
  5. Find fraction submerged = ρ_object/ρ_fluid

Tip: Buoyant force acts through center of buoyancy

Type 3: Bernoulli Problems

Steps:

  1. Identify two points along streamline
  2. Write Bernoulli equation: P₁ + ½ρv₁² + ρgh₁ = P₂ + ½ρv₂² + ρgh₂
  3. Use continuity: A₁v₁ = A₂v₂
  4. Solve for unknown
  5. Check assumptions (ideal fluid)

Tip: Height measured from common reference level

JEE/NEET TIP

Common Problem Patterns

1. U-tube problems: Equal pressure at same horizontal level

2. Communicating vessels: Liquid finds same level

3. Manometer problems: P_A + ρ₁gh₁ = P_B + ρ₂gh₂

4. Venturimeter: Use Bernoulli + continuity

5. Capillary tubes: h = 2T cosθ/(ρgr)

6. Terminal velocity: Weight = Buoyant force + Viscous force

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Pressure in fluid acts equally in all directions at a point
  • Buoyant force depends on volume displaced, not weight of object
  • Streamline flow: Re < 2000, Turbulent: Re > 4000
  • Surface tension decreases with temperature, becomes zero at critical temperature
  • Angle of contact: θ < 90° for wetting, θ > 90° for non-wetting
  • Bernoulli's theorem assumes ideal, incompressible, steady, irrotational flow
  • Viscosity of liquids decreases with temperature, gases increases
  • Excess pressure in bubble: 4T/R (soap bubble has two surfaces)

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Confusing gauge pressure (P - P₀) with absolute pressure
  • Forgetting that buoyant force acts through center of buoyancy
  • Applying Bernoulli's theorem to viscous fluids without modification
  • Using wrong sign for capillary rise/fall (depends on θ)
  • Confusing surface tension units (N/m = J/m²)
  • Forgetting temperature dependence of viscosity and surface tension
  • Not checking Reynolds number before using Poiseuille's equation
  • Confusing excess pressure formulas for droplet, bubble, soap bubble
