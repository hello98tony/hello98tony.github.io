Introduction to Complex Numbers

Complex numbers extend the real number system to include solutions to equations like x² + 1 = 0. They form the foundation for advanced mathematics, physics, and engineering.

Complex Number: A number of the form z = a + ib, where a and b are real numbers, and i is the imaginary unit satisfying i² = -1.

• a is called the real part: Re(z) = a
• b is called the imaginary part: Im(z) = b

Complex Algebra

Operations with complex numbers: addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and conjugation.

(a+ib) ± (c+id) = (a±c) + i(b±d)
Argand Plane

Geometric representation of complex numbers as points in a plane with real and imaginary axes.

z = x + iy ↔ Point (x, y)
De Moivre's Theorem

For any real number θ and integer n: (cos θ + i sin θ)ⁿ = cos nθ + i sin nθ

(cos θ + i sin θ)ⁿ = cos nθ + i sin nθ

Complex Algebra Operations

Basic algebraic operations with complex numbers follow similar rules to real numbers, with the additional rule i² = -1.

Complex Conjugate: For z = a + ib, the complex conjugate is denoted as z̄ = a - ib.

Modulus: |z| = √(a² + b²) = distance from origin in Argand plane.

Use distributive property and i² = -1

(a+ib)/(c+id) = [(ac+bd)+i(bc-ad)]/(c²+d²)

Key Algebraic Formulas:

  • i² = -1, i³ = -i, i⁴ = 1, iⁿ = i^(n mod 4)
  • Conjugate: z̄ = a - ib for z = a + ib
  • Modulus: |z| = √(a² + b²) = √(z z̄)
  • Reciprocal: 1/z = z̄/|z|² for z ≠ 0
  • Division: Multiply numerator and denominator by conjugate of denominator
  • Real and Imaginary parts: Re(z) = (z + z̄)/2, Im(z) = (z - z̄)/(2i)
  • Square root: √(a+ib) = ±[√((√(a²+b²)+a)/2) + i sign(b)√((√(a²+b²)-a)/2)]
Example: Complex Division

Simplify: (3 + 4i) / (1 - 2i)

Solution:

Multiply numerator and denominator by conjugate of denominator:

= (3+4i)(1+2i) / [(1-2i)(1+2i)]

= [3 + 6i + 4i + 8i²] / [1² - (2i)²]

= [3 + 10i - 8] / [1 - (-4)] (since i² = -1)

= (-5 + 10i) / 5

= -1 + 2i

Thus, (3+4i)/(1-2i) = -1 + 2i

JEE TIP

Complex Number Manipulation

• For division: Always multiply by conjugate of denominator

• For modulus: |z|² = z z̄ (useful for simplification)

• For powers of i: i¹ = i, i² = -1, i³ = -i, i⁴ = 1 (cycle repeats every 4)

• For finding real/imaginary parts: Use z + z̄ and z - z̄ formulas

• For square roots: Use formula or convert to polar form

Argand Plane & Geometric Interpretation

The Argand plane (complex plane) provides a geometric representation of complex numbers, where each complex number corresponds to a point with coordinates (Re(z), Im(z)).

Argand Plane: A coordinate plane where the x-axis represents the real part and the y-axis represents the imaginary part of complex numbers.

• Point representation: z = x + iy ↔ (x, y)
• Modulus: |z| = √(x² + y²) = distance from origin
• Argument: arg(z) = θ = tan⁻¹(y/x) = angle with positive real axis

Geometric Properties and Loci:

  • Circle: |z - z₀| = r represents circle with center z₀ and radius r
  • Perpendicular bisector: |z - z₁| = |z - z₂| represents perpendicular bisector of segment joining z₁ and z₂
  • Line: arg(z - z₁) = θ represents ray starting at z₁ making angle θ with positive real axis
  • Half-line: arg(z - z₁) = constant represents a ray from z₁
  • Region inside circle: |z - z₀| < r represents interior of circle
  • Region outside circle: |z - z₀| > r represents exterior of circle
  • Annulus: r₁ < |z - z₀| < r₂ represents circular ring
  • Ellipse: |z - z₁| + |z - z₂| = constant represents ellipse with foci at z₁ and z₂
Example: Locus Problem

Find the locus of z if |z - 3| = 2|z + 3|

Solution:

Let z = x + iy

|z - 3| = |(x-3) + iy| = √[(x-3)² + y²]

|z + 3| = |(x+3) + iy| = √[(x+3)² + y²]

Given: √[(x-3)² + y²] = 2√[(x+3)² + y²]

Squaring both sides: (x-3)² + y² = 4[(x+3)² + y²]

x² - 6x + 9 + y² = 4[x² + 6x + 9 + y²]

x² - 6x + 9 + y² = 4x² + 24x + 36 + 4y²

0 = 3x² + 30x + 27 + 3y²

Divide by 3: x² + 10x + 9 + y² = 0

Complete square: (x² + 10x + 25) + y² = 25 - 9 = 16

(x+5)² + y² = 4²

This is a circle with center at (-5, 0) and radius 4.

Thus, the locus is a circle centered at -5 + 0i with radius 4.

Complex Numbers as Transformations

Translation: z → z + a (shift by complex number a)

Rotation: z → e^(iθ)z (rotate by angle θ about origin)

Scaling: z → kz (scale by factor k, k real)

Rotation and Scaling: z → (re^(iθ))z (rotate by θ and scale by r)

Inversion: z → 1/z (circle inversion transformation)

Linear Transformation: z → az + b (combination of rotation, scaling, translation)

Möbius Transformation: z → (az+b)/(cz+d) (most general conformal mapping of complex plane)

Polar Form & Euler's Formula

The polar form provides a powerful way to represent complex numbers using modulus and argument, leading to Euler's famous formula connecting complex numbers with trigonometry.

Polar Form: z = r(cos θ + i sin θ), where r = |z| ≥ 0 and θ = arg(z).

Euler's Formula: e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ, for any real θ.

Euler's Formula - "The Most Beautiful Equation"

e^(iπ) + 1 = 0

Connects five fundamental mathematical constants: e, i, π, 1, and 0

e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ

The bridge between exponential and trigonometric functions

• y = Im(z)

x = 3, y = 4

z = r(cos θ + i sin θ)

Example: 5(cos 53.13° + i sin 53.13°)
r = 5, θ ≈ 53.13°

• r = |z|
• θ = arg(z)

Example: 5e^(i·0.927)

Properties of Polar Form:

  • Multiplication: z₁z₂ = r₁r₂[cos(θ₁+θ₂) + i sin(θ₁+θ₂)] = r₁r₂ e^(i(θ₁+θ₂))
  • Division: z₁/z₂ = (r₁/r₂)[cos(θ₁-θ₂) + i sin(θ₁-θ₂)] = (r₁/r₂) e^(i(θ₁-θ₂))
  • Power: zⁿ = rⁿ[cos(nθ) + i sin(nθ)] = rⁿ e^(inθ) (De Moivre's theorem)
  • Root: z^(1/n) = r^(1/n)[cos((θ+2kπ)/n) + i sin((θ+2kπ)/n)], k = 0,1,...,n-1
  • Argument properties: arg(z₁z₂) = arg(z₁) + arg(z₂), arg(z₁/z₂) = arg(z₁) - arg(z₂)
  • Modulus properties: |z₁z₂| = |z₁||z₂|, |z₁/z₂| = |z₁|/|z₂|, |zⁿ| = |z|ⁿ
  • Complex conjugate: If z = re^(iθ), then z̄ = re^(-iθ)
  • Reciprocal: 1/z = (1/r)e^(-iθ) for z ≠ 0
Example: Multiplication in Polar Form

Multiply z₁ = 1 + i and z₂ = √3 + i using polar form.

Solution:

Convert to polar form:

z₁ = 1 + i: r₁ = √(1²+1²) = √2, θ₁ = tan⁻¹(1/1) = π/4

z₂ = √3 + i: r₂ = √((√3)²+1²) = √(3+1) = 2, θ₂ = tan⁻¹(1/√3) = π/6

In polar form: z₁ = √2(cos π/4 + i sin π/4), z₂ = 2(cos π/6 + i sin π/6)

Multiplication: z₁z₂ = (√2 × 2)[cos(π/4+π/6) + i sin(π/4+π/6)]

= 2√2[cos(5π/12) + i sin(5π/12)]

Convert back to rectangular: cos(5π/12) = (√6 - √2)/4, sin(5π/12) = (√6 + √2)/4

z₁z₂ = 2√2[(√6-√2)/4 + i(√6+√2)/4] = (√12-√4)/2 + i(√12+√4)/2

= (2√3-2)/2 + i(2√3+2)/2 = (√3-1) + i(√3+1)

Check: (1+i)(√3+i) = √3 + i + i√3 + i² = (√3-1) + i(1+√3) ✓

De Moivre's Theorem & Roots of Unity

De Moivre's theorem provides a powerful tool for computing powers and roots of complex numbers, with important applications in solving equations and analyzing periodic phenomena.

De Moivre's Theorem: For any integer n and real number θ, (cos θ + i sin θ)ⁿ = cos nθ + i sin nθ.

Corollary: For any complex number z = r(cos θ + i sin θ), zⁿ = rⁿ(cos nθ + i sin nθ).

(cos θ + i sin θ)ⁿ = cos nθ + i sin nθ

Also written as: [r(cos θ + i sin θ)]ⁿ = rⁿ(cos nθ + i sin nθ)
Or in exponential form: (re^(iθ))ⁿ = rⁿ e^(inθ)

Example: (1+i)¹⁰

1+i = √2(cos π/4 + i sin π/4)

(1+i)¹⁰ = (√2)¹⁰(cos 10π/4 + i sin 10π/4)

nth Roots

Find all n solutions to zⁿ = w

z = r^(1/n)[cos((θ+2kπ)/n) + i sin((θ+2kπ)/n)]

k = 0, 1, 2, ..., n-1

Roots of Unity

Solutions of zⁿ = 1

ωₖ = cos(2kπ/n) + i sin(2kπ/n)

Example: Cube roots of unity: 1, (-1±i√3)/2

They satisfy ω³ = 1 and 1+ω+ω²=0

Properties of Roots of Unity

Property Description Formula Example (n=3)
Geometric progression Roots are in GP with common ratio ω 1, ω, ω², ..., ωⁿ⁻¹ 1, ω, ω² where ω = e^(2πi/3)
Sum of roots Sum of all nth roots of unity is 0 1 + ω + ω² + ... + ωⁿ⁻¹ = 0 1 + ω + ω² = 0
Product of roots Product of all nth roots of unity is (-1)ⁿ⁻¹ 1·ω·ω²·...·ωⁿ⁻¹ = (-1)ⁿ⁻¹ 1·ω·ω² = 1 (since n=3, (-1)²=1)
Conjugate pairs Non-real roots occur in conjugate pairs If ω is a root, then ω̄ is also a root ω and ω̄ = ω² are conjugates

Key Results from De Moivre's Theorem:

  • For any integer n: (cos θ + i sin θ)ⁿ = cos nθ + i sin nθ
  • zⁿ = rⁿ(cos nθ + i sin nθ) for z = r(cos θ + i sin θ)
  • nth roots of z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) are: z^(1/n) = r^(1/n)[cos((θ+2kπ)/n) + i sin((θ+2kπ)/n)], k = 0,1,...,n-1
  • nth roots of unity: Solutions of zⁿ = 1 are ωₖ = cos(2kπ/n) + i sin(2kπ/n), k = 0,1,...,n-1
  • Sum of nth roots of unity: 1 + ω + ω² + ... + ωⁿ⁻¹ = 0 (for n > 1)
  • Cube roots of unity: 1, ω, ω² where ω = (-1 + i√3)/2 and ω² = (-1 - i√3)/2, with 1+ω+ω² = 0 and ω³ = 1
  • Fourth roots of unity: 1, i, -1, -i (powers of i)
  • For solving equations: Express in polar form and use De Moivre's theorem
Example: Finding Cube Roots

Find all cube roots of -8.

Solution:

Let z³ = -8

Write -8 in polar form: -8 = 8(cos π + i sin π)

Cube roots: z = 8^(1/3)[cos((π+2kπ)/3) + i sin((π+2kπ)/3)], k = 0,1,2

= 2[cos((π+2kπ)/3) + i sin((π+2kπ)/3)]

For k=0: z₀ = 2(cos π/3 + i sin π/3) = 2(1/2 + i√3/2) = 1 + i√3

For k=1: z₁ = 2(cos π + i sin π) = 2(-1 + i·0) = -2

For k=2: z₂ = 2(cos 5π/3 + i sin 5π/3) = 2(1/2 - i√3/2) = 1 - i√3

Thus, cube roots of -8 are: -2, 1 + i√3, 1 - i√3

Check: (-2)³ = -8, (1±i√3)³ = 1 ± 3i√3 + 3(i√3)² + (i√3)³ = 1 ± 3i√3 - 9 ∓ i3√3 = -8 ✓

Fractals: Mandelbrot Set

The Mandelbrot set is generated by iterating the function f(z) = z² + c for complex numbers c.

zₙ₊₁ = zₙ² + c, starting with z₀ = 0

Points c for which the sequence remains bounded are in the Mandelbrot set.

The boundary of the Mandelbrot set is infinitely complex and self-similar at all scales.

Example: AC Circuit Application

An AC circuit has R = 10Ω, L = 0.1H, and C = 100μF. The source voltage is V = 100∠0° volts at frequency f = 50Hz. Find the current.

Solution:

Angular frequency: ω = 2πf = 2π×50 = 100π rad/s

Inductive reactance: X_L = ωL = 100π×0.1 = 10π Ω ≈ 31.42 Ω

Capacitive reactance: X_C = 1/(ωC) = 1/(100π×100×10⁻⁶) = 100/(π) Ω ≈ 31.83 Ω

Net reactance: X = X_L - X_C = 31.42 - 31.83 = -0.41 Ω (capacitive)

Impedance: Z = R + iX = 10 - 0.41i Ω

|Z| = √(10² + 0.41²) ≈ √(100 + 0.168) ≈ √100.168 ≈ 10.008 Ω

Phase angle: φ = tan⁻¹(X/R) = tan⁻¹(-0.41/10) ≈ tan⁻¹(-0.041) ≈ -2.35°

Current: I = V/Z = 100∠0° / 10.008∠-2.35° = (100/10.008)∠(0° - (-2.35°)) ≈ 9.99∠2.35° A

Thus, current magnitude ≈ 9.99 A, leading voltage by 2.35°.

Problem Solving Strategies

Effective techniques for solving complex number problems in JEE and other competitive exams.

Type 1: Algebraic Manipulation

Steps:

  1. Separate real and imaginary parts
  2. Use i² = -1 to simplify
  3. For division, multiply by conjugate
  4. Equate real and imaginary parts if equation = 0

Tip: Use z + z̄ = 2Re(z) and z - z̄ = 2i Im(z)

Type 2: Geometric Problems

Steps:

  1. Plot points on Argand plane
  2. Use geometry of triangles, circles, etc.
  3. Apply distance formula |z₁ - z₂|
  4. Use argument for angles

Tip: |z - z₀| = r represents circle with center z₀, radius r

Type 3: De Moivre's Theorem

Steps:

  1. Convert to polar form: z = r(cos θ + i sin θ)
  2. Apply theorem: zⁿ = rⁿ(cos nθ + i sin nθ)
  3. For roots, use formula with k = 0,1,...,n-1
  4. Convert back to rectangular form if needed

Tip: For roots of unity, remember 1+ω+ω²+...+ωⁿ⁻¹=0

Example: Challenging Geometry Problem

If z₁, z₂, z₃ are vertices of an equilateral triangle in the complex plane, prove that z₁² + z₂² + z₃² = z₁z₂ + z₂z₃ + z₃z₁.

Solution:

For an equilateral triangle, the vertices satisfy:

z₂ - z₁ = (z₃ - z₁)e^(±iπ/3)

Let ω = e^(iπ/3) = cos π/3 + i sin π/3 = 1/2 + i√3/2

Then z₂ - z₁ = (z₃ - z₁)ω ...(1)

Also, ω satisfies ω² - ω + 1 = 0 (since ω = e^(iπ/3) is a cube root of -1)

From (1): z₂ = z₁ + (z₃ - z₁)ω

Squaring: z₂² = z₁² + 2z₁(z₃ - z₁)ω + (z₃ - z₁)²ω²

Similarly, from z₃ - z₂ = (z₁ - z₂)ω (rotating other way):

z₃ = z₂ + (z₁ - z₂)ω = z₂(1-ω) + z₁ω

We need to prove: z₁² + z₂² + z₃² = z₁z₂ + z₂z₃ + z₃z₁

Or equivalently: z₁² + z₂² + z₃² - z₁z₂ - z₂z₃ - z₃z₁ = 0

This expression is symmetric and equals (z₁ + ωz₂ + ω²z₃)(z₁ + ω²z₂ + ωz₃) = 0

For equilateral triangle, z₁ + ωz₂ + ω²z₃ = 0 (known property)

Thus the product is 0, proving the required identity.

Alternatively, note that for equilateral triangle centered at origin, the vertices are r, rω, rω², which clearly satisfy the identity.

z = a + ib, where a,b ∈ ℝ and i² = -1. Extends real number system
ARGAND PLANE
Geometric representation: x-axis = real, y-axis = imaginary. z = (x,y)
MODULUS & ARGUMENT
|z| = √(a²+b²), arg(z) = tan⁻¹(b/a). Polar form: z = r(cos θ + i sin θ)
EULER'S FORMULA
e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ. Most beautiful equation: e^(iπ) + 1 = 0

(cos θ + i sin θ)ⁿ = cos nθ + i sin nθ. Key for powers and roots
ROOTS OF UNITY
Solutions of zⁿ = 1: ωₖ = e^(2πik/n), k=0,...,n-1. Sum = 0 for n>1

z̄ = a - ib. Properties: z z̄ = |z|², z₁+z₂̄ = z̄₁+z̄₂, z₁z₂̄ = z̄₁z̄₂

AC circuits, signal processing, quantum mechanics, fractals, control systems

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Complex number: z = a + ib where a,b ∈ ℝ and i² = -1
  • Real part: Re(z) = a, Imaginary part: Im(z) = b
  • Complex conjugate: z̄ = a - ib
  • Modulus: |z| = √(a² + b²) = √(z z̄)
  • Argument: arg(z) = tan⁻¹(b/a), with quadrant adjustment
  • Polar form: z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) where r = |z|, θ = arg(z)
  • Euler's formula: e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ
  • Exponential form: z = re^(iθ)
  • De Moivre's theorem: (cos θ + i sin θ)ⁿ = cos nθ + i sin nθ
  • nth roots: z^(1/n) = r^(1/n)[cos((θ+2kπ)/n) + i sin((θ+2kπ)/n)], k = 0,1,...,n-1
  • Cube roots of unity: 1, ω, ω² where ω = (-1+i√3)/2, satisfying 1+ω+ω²=0 and ω³=1
  • Triangle inequality: |z₁ + z₂| ≤ |z₁| + |z₂|
  • Geometric interpretation: |z - z₀| = r represents circle with center z₀ and radius r
  • Rotation: Multiplication by e^(iθ) rotates complex number by angle θ
  • Applications: AC circuits (impedance), signal processing, quantum mechanics

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Forgetting that i² = -1 (not i² = 1 or i² = i)
  • Incorrectly calculating modulus: |a+ib| = √(a²+b²), not a+b or √(a²-b²)
  • Wrong quadrant for argument: arg(z) depends on signs of both a and b
  • Not multiplying by conjugate when dividing complex numbers
  • Forgetting that |z| ≥ 0 always (modulus is non-negative real number)
  • Incorrect application of De Moivre's theorem: only for integer n in (cos θ + i sin θ)ⁿ
  • Missing some roots when finding nth roots: there are exactly n distinct roots
  • Confusing real and imaginary parts: Re(a+ib) = a, Im(a+ib) = b (not ib)
  • Not simplifying powers of i: i¹ = i, i² = -1, i³ = -i, i⁴ = 1 (cycle of 4)
  • Incorrect geometric interpretation in Argand plane
  • Forgetting that complex roots of polynomials with real coefficients occur in conjugate pairs
  • Not checking conditions for equality in triangle inequality
