Introduction to Sequences & Series

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers following a specific pattern, while a series is the sum of terms of a sequence. They form the foundation for calculus, financial mathematics, and various real-world applications.

Sequence: An ordered list of numbers a₁, a₂, a₃, ... where each term follows a specific rule or pattern.

Series: The sum of terms of a sequence: S = a₁ + a₂ + a₃ + ... + aₙ

Arithmetic Progression

A sequence where the difference between consecutive terms is constant. Formula: aₙ = a + (n-1)d

a, a+d, a+2d, a+3d, ...
Geometric Progression

A sequence where the ratio between consecutive terms is constant. Formula: aₙ = arⁿ⁻¹

a, ar, ar², ar³, ...
Harmonic Progression

A sequence where the reciprocals of terms form an AP. Formula: 1/a, 1/(a+d), 1/(a+2d), ...

1/a₁, 1/a₂, 1/a₃, ... where aᵢ are in AP

Key Terminology:

  • Term: Each number in a sequence (a₁, a₂, a₃, ...)
  • Common Difference (d): Constant difference in AP
  • Common Ratio (r): Constant ratio in GP
  • nth Term: General term of sequence (aₙ)
  • Sum of n terms (Sₙ): Sum of first n terms
  • Finite Series: Series with finite number of terms
  • Infinite Series: Series with infinite terms (convergent/divergent)

Arithmetic Progression (AP)

An Arithmetic Progression is a sequence where each term after the first is obtained by adding a constant value to the preceding term.

Arithmetic Progression: A sequence a₁, a₂, a₃, ... is called an AP if aₙ₊₁ - aₙ = constant (d) for all n ≥ 1.

aₙ = a + (n-1)d

Where:
• a = first term
• d = common difference
• n = term number
• aₙ = nth term

nth Term
aₙ = a + (n-1)d

General term of AP

Sum of n Terms
Sₙ = n/2 [2a + (n-1)d]
Sₙ = n/2 (a + l)

Important Results for AP:

  • If a, b, c are in AP, then 2b = a + c (b is arithmetic mean of a and c)
  • The sum of first n natural numbers: Sₙ = n(n+1)/2
  • The sum of first n odd numbers: 1 + 3 + 5 + ... + (2n-1) = n²
  • The sum of first n even numbers: 2 + 4 + 6 + ... + 2n = n(n+1)
  • If Sₙ = An² + Bn, then the sequence is AP with d = 2A
  • The arithmetic mean of n numbers = (Sum of numbers)/n
  • For any AP, Sₙ - Sₙ₋₁ = aₙ (nth term)
  • If the pth term is q and qth term is p, then (p+q)th term is 0
Example: AP Problem

Find the sum of first 20 terms of an AP whose nth term is given by aₙ = 3n + 2.

Solution:

Given: aₙ = 3n + 2

First term: a₁ = 3(1) + 2 = 5

Second term: a₂ = 3(2) + 2 = 8

Common difference: d = a₂ - a₁ = 8 - 5 = 3

Sum of first 20 terms: S₂₀ = 20/2 [2×5 + (20-1)×3]

S₂₀ = 10 [10 + 19×3] = 10 [10 + 57] = 10 × 67 = 670

JEE TIP

AP Selection of Terms

For 3 terms: a-d, a, a+d

For 4 terms: a-3d, a-d, a+d, a+3d

For 5 terms: a-2d, a-d, a, a+d, a+2d

For even number of terms: Use symmetric pairs around center

For product of terms: Choose terms symmetrically to cancel d

Geometric Progression (GP)

A Geometric Progression is a sequence where each term after the first is obtained by multiplying the preceding term by a constant value.

Geometric Progression: A sequence a₁, a₂, a₃, ... is called a GP if aₙ₊₁/aₙ = constant (r) for all n ≥ 1, r ≠ 0.

aₙ = arⁿ⁻¹

Where:
• a = first term
• r = common ratio
• n = term number
• aₙ = nth term

nth Term
aₙ = arⁿ⁻¹

General term of GP

Sum of n Terms
Sₙ = a(1-rⁿ)/(1-r), r≠1
Sₙ = na, r=1

Finite GP sum

Infinite GP Sum
S∞ = a/(1-r)

For |r| < 1 only

Important Results for GP:

  • If a, b, c are in GP, then b² = ac (b is geometric mean of a and c)
  • The product of first n terms: Pₙ = aⁿ × r^{n(n-1)/2}
  • Sum of infinite GP: S∞ = a/(1-r) for |r| < 1
  • If each term of a GP is raised to power k, the new sequence is also GP
  • The geometric mean of n numbers = (Product of numbers)^{1/n}
  • For any GP, Sₙ - Sₙ₋₁ = aₙ (nth term)
  • If Sₙ = A(rⁿ - 1), then the sequence is GP
  • The sum of infinite geometric series 1 + x + x² + ... = 1/(1-x) for |x| < 1
Example: Infinite GP Problem

Find the sum to infinity of the series: 1 + 1/3 + 1/9 + 1/27 + ...

Solution:

This is an infinite GP with:

First term: a = 1

Common ratio: r = (1/3) / 1 = 1/3

Since |r| = 1/3 < 1, the infinite sum exists:

S∞ = a/(1-r) = 1/(1 - 1/3) = 1/(2/3) = 3/2 = 1.5

The sum converges to 1.5 as more terms are added.

Special Cases of GP

Increasing GP

When r > 1, terms increase exponentially

2, 4, 8, 16, 32, ...

Example: Population growth, compound interest

Decreasing GP

When 0 < r < 1, terms decrease to zero

16, 8, 4, 2, 1, 0.5, ...

Example: Radioactive decay, depreciation

Alternating GP

When r < 0, terms alternate signs

3, -6, 12, -24, 48, ...

Example: Oscillating systems

Harmonic Progression (HP)

A Harmonic Progression is a sequence whose reciprocals form an Arithmetic Progression. It appears in physics, music, and various mathematical contexts.

Harmonic Progression: A sequence a₁, a₂, a₃, ... is called an HP if the sequence of reciprocals 1/a₁, 1/a₂, 1/a₃, ... forms an AP.

For HP: a, b, c are in HP ⇔ 1/a, 1/b, 1/c are in AP

Where:
• The reciprocals form an AP
• No simple formula for nth term like AP or GP
• Harmonic Mean: HM = 2ab/(a+b) for two numbers

nth Term of HP
aₙ = 1/[A + (n-1)D]

Where 1/a₁, 1/a₂, ... form AP with first term A and common difference D

Harmonic Mean
HM = n/[1/a₁ + 1/a₂ + ... + 1/aₙ]
HM = 2ab/(a+b)

For two numbers a and b

Relation AM ≥ GM ≥ HM
AM ≥ GM ≥ HM

Equality holds when all numbers are equal

Important Results for HP:

  • If a, b, c are in HP, then b = 2ac/(a+c) (b is harmonic mean of a and c)
  • For two positive numbers, AM ≥ GM ≥ HM
  • If x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ are in HP, then 1/x₁, 1/x₂, ..., 1/xₙ are in AP
  • There is no general formula for sum of n terms of HP
  • For any three numbers in HP, the middle term is the harmonic mean of the other two
  • If a, b, c are in HP, then a/(b+c), b/(c+a), c/(a+b) are also in HP
  • The harmonic mean is always less than or equal to geometric mean
Example: HP Problem

If the pth term of an HP is qr and the qth term is pr, find the rth term.

Solution:

Let the HP be 1/a₁, 1/a₂, 1/a₃, ... where a₁, a₂, a₃, ... form AP

Given: pth term of HP = qr ⇒ 1/[A + (p-1)D] = qr ...(1)

qth term of HP = pr ⇒ 1/[A + (q-1)D] = pr ...(2)

From (1): A + (p-1)D = 1/(qr)

From (2): A + (q-1)D = 1/(pr)

Subtracting: (p-q)D = 1/(qr) - 1/(pr) = (p-q)/(pqr)

⇒ D = 1/(pqr)

Substituting: A = 1/(qr) - (p-1)/(pqr) = (p - p + 1)/(pqr) = 1/(pqr)

rth term of HP = 1/[A + (r-1)D] = 1/[1/(pqr) + (r-1)/(pqr)]

= 1/[r/(pqr)] = 1/[1/(pq)] = pq

Thus, the rth term is pq.

Relation Between AM, GM, HM

Arithmetic Mean (AM) 10
Geometric Mean (GM) 8
Harmonic Mean (HM) 6.4
For positive numbers a and b:
AM = (a+b)/2 ≥ GM = √(ab) ≥ HM = 2ab/(a+b)

Then sum telescopes: Sₙ = u₁ - uₙ₊₁

Vₙ Method

For series where tₙ = polynomial in n

Important Summation Formulas:

  • ∑k = n(n+1)/2 (sum of first n natural numbers)
  • ∑k² = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6 (sum of squares)
  • ∑k³ = [n(n+1)/2]² (sum of cubes)
  • ∑(2k-1) = n² (sum of first n odd numbers)
  • ∑(2k) = n(n+1) (sum of first n even numbers)
  • ∑k(k+1) = n(n+1)(n+2)/3
  • ∑k(k+1)(k+2) = n(n+1)(n+2)(n+3)/4
  • ∑1/[k(k+1)] = 1 - 1/(n+1) (telescoping series)
  • ∑r⋅xʳ⁻¹ = 1/(1-x)² for |x| < 1 (differentiation of geometric series)
Example: Sum of Series

Find the sum of series: 1² + 3² + 5² + ... to n terms.

Solution:

We need sum of squares of first n odd numbers.

kth odd number = 2k-1

Sum = ∑(2k-1)² from k=1 to n = ∑(4k² - 4k + 1)

= 4∑k² - 4∑k + ∑1

= 4[n(n+1)(2n+1)/6] - 4[n(n+1)/2] + n

= [2n(n+1)(2n+1)/3] - 2n(n+1) + n

= n[2(n+1)(2n+1)/3 - 2(n+1) + 1]

= n[(4n²+6n+2)/3 - 2n - 2 + 1]

= n[(4n²+6n+2 - 6n - 6 + 3)/3]

= n(4n² - 1)/3

Thus, 1² + 3² + 5² + ... to n terms = n(4n² - 1)/3

Arithmetico-Geometric Series (AGS)

A series where each term is product of corresponding terms of AP and GP.

Standard AGS: a + (a+d)r + (a+2d)r² + ... + [a+(n-1)d]rⁿ⁻¹

Sum formula: Sₙ = a/(1-r) + dr(1 - rⁿ⁻¹)/(1-r)² - [a+(n-1)d]rⁿ/(1-r), for r ≠ 1

Example: AGS Sum

Find sum: 1 + 2x + 3x² + ... + nxⁿ⁻¹

Solution:

This is AGS with a=1, d=1, r=x

Sₙ = 1/(1-x) + 1⋅x(1 - xⁿ⁻¹)/(1-x)² - [1+(n-1)⋅1]xⁿ/(1-x)

= 1/(1-x) + x(1-xⁿ⁻¹)/(1-x)² - nxⁿ/(1-x)

= [1 - xⁿ]/[1-x]² - nxⁿ/(1-x)

For |x| < 1, as n→∞, S∞ = 1/(1-x)²

Example: Real-World Application

A ball is dropped from a height of 80 meters. Each time it hits the ground, it bounces back to 3/4 of the height from which it fell. Find the total distance traveled by the ball before coming to rest.

Solution:

Initial drop: 80 m

First bounce: up = 80 × 3/4 = 60 m, down = 60 m

Second bounce: up = 60 × 3/4 = 45 m, down = 45 m

This forms two infinite GPs:

Downward distances: 80, 60, 45, ... (GP with a=80, r=3/4)

Upward distances: 60, 45, 33.75, ... (GP with a=60, r=3/4)

Total downward distance = 80/(1 - 3/4) = 80/(1/4) = 320 m

Total upward distance = 60/(1 - 3/4) = 60/(1/4) = 240 m

Total distance = Downward + Upward = 320 + 240 = 560 meters

Problem Solving Strategies

Effective techniques for solving sequence and series problems in JEE and other competitive exams.

Type 1: Finding Terms

Steps:

  1. Identify type of progression (AP/GP/HP)
  2. Use appropriate formula for nth term
  3. Set up equations from given information
  4. Solve system of equations

Tip: For three terms, use symmetric representations

Type 2: Sum Problems

Steps:

  1. Identify pattern in terms
  2. Use appropriate sum formula
  3. For special series, derive general term first
  4. Apply method of differences if applicable

Tip: For polynomial series, use ∑k, ∑k², ∑k³ formulas

Type 3: Mean Problems

Steps:

  1. Identify which mean is involved (AM/GM/HM)
  2. Use appropriate relationship
  3. Apply inequality AM ≥ GM ≥ HM if needed
  4. Check conditions for equality

Tip: For two numbers, use AM×HM = GM²

Common Problem Types:

  • Finding terms: Specific term, missing terms, three/four terms in progression
  • Sum problems: Sum of n terms, sum to infinity, partial sums
  • Mean problems: AM, GM, HM, inequalities between means
  • Relation problems: Terms in multiple progressions, conversion between AP/GP/HP
  • Application problems: Compound interest, population growth, physical applications
  • Special series: Sum of squares, cubes, telescoping series, arithmetico-geometric series
  • Inequality problems: Proving inequalities using AM-GM-HM
  • Inserting means: Insert arithmetic/geometric/harmonic means between two numbers
JEE ADVANCED TIP

Advanced Problem Solving Techniques

• For AGS: Multiply by common ratio and subtract

• For telescoping series: Express term as difference of two consecutive terms

• For polynomial series: Use ∑k, ∑k², ∑k³ formulas or method of differences

• For inequality proofs: Use AM ≥ GM ≥ HM or Cauchy-Schwarz

• For insertion problems: Remember number of means = n, total terms = n+2

• For relation between AP, GP, HP: Convert to common format (usually AP)

Example: Challenging Problem

If a, b, c are in AP; b, c, d are in GP; and c, d, e are in HP, prove that a, c, e are in GP.

Solution:

Given: a, b, c are in AP ⇒ 2b = a + c ...(1)

b, c, d are in GP ⇒ c² = bd ...(2)

c, d, e are in HP ⇒ d = 2ce/(c+e) ...(3)

From (1): b = (a+c)/2

From (2): d = c²/b = c²/[(a+c)/2] = 2c²/(a+c)

From (3): 2c²/(a+c) = 2ce/(c+e)

⇒ c/(a+c) = e/(c+e)

⇒ c(c+e) = e(a+c)

⇒ c² + ce = ae + ce

⇒ c² = ae

Thus, a, c, e are in GP (since c² = ae).

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • AP: aₙ = a + (n-1)d, Sₙ = n/2[2a+(n-1)d], three terms: a-d, a, a+d
  • GP: aₙ = arⁿ⁻¹, Sₙ = a(1-rⁿ)/(1-r) for r≠1, S∞ = a/(1-r) for |r|<1, three terms: a/r, a, ar

  • Telescoping series: Express term as difference of consecutive terms
  • Method of differences: For series where general term is polynomial in n
  • Applications: Compound interest (GP), simple interest (AP), installment payments, population growth
  • Insertion of means: n AMs between a and b form AP with d = (b-a)/(n+1)

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Using AP formulas for GP or vice versa
  • Forgetting that HP has no simple nth term formula (work with reciprocals)
  • Applying S∞ = a/(1-r) when |r| ≥ 1 (series diverges)
  • Miscalculating number of terms: n = (l-a)/d + 1 for AP
  • Using wrong formula for sum of squares/cubes
  • Forgetting that AM ≥ GM ≥ HM applies only to positive numbers
  • Not checking conditions for existence of infinite GP sum
  • Miscounting terms when inserting means between two numbers
  • Using simple interest formula for compound interest problems
  • Not converting all quantities to same units in application problems
