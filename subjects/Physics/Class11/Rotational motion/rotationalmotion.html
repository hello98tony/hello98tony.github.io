System of Rigid Body & Rotational Motion

Class 11 Physics | JEE & NEET Preparation Guide

Master rotational dynamics, torque, moment of inertia, and angular momentum - essential concepts for competitive exams

Introduction to Rotational Motion

Rotational motion describes the motion of objects that rotate about an axis. While translational motion deals with movement from one point to another, rotational motion involves spinning about a fixed point or axis.

Key Concept: Every point in a rotating rigid body moves in a circle whose center lies on the axis of rotation, and every point moves through the same angle during a particular time interval.

Rigid Body

A body is said to be rigid if the distance between any two particles of the body remains constant, regardless of external forces applied.

Δr = 0 (for any two points)
Rotational Motion

Motion of a body about a fixed axis where all particles move in circular paths centered on the axis.

θ = s/r
Torque

The rotational equivalent of force. It measures how much a force acting on an object causes that object to rotate.

τ = r × F

System of Rigid Body

A rigid body is an idealized body in which deformation is zero or so small it can be neglected. The distance between any two given points on a rigid body remains constant in time regardless of external forces exerted on it.

Characteristics of Rigid Body:

  • All points maintain fixed relative positions
  • No deformation under applied forces
  • Can have both translational and rotational motion
  • Mass is continuously distributed

Degrees of Freedom

Type of Motion Degrees of Freedom Description
Translational 3 (x, y, z) Motion along three perpendicular axes
Rotational 3 (θ_x, θ_y, θ_z) Rotation about three perpendicular axes
Total for Rigid Body 6 3 translational + 3 rotational

Center of Mass (COM): The point where the entire mass of the body can be assumed to be concentrated for translational motion.

r_cm = (Σ m_i r_i) / (Σ m_i)
Example: Center of Mass of Two Particles

Two particles of masses 2 kg and 3 kg are located at positions (1, 2) and (4, 6) respectively. Find their center of mass.

Solution:

x_cm = (2×1 + 3×4)/(2+3) = (2+12)/5 = 14/5 = 2.8

y_cm = (2×2 + 3×6)/(2+3) = (4+18)/5 = 22/5 = 4.4

Center of Mass = (2.8, 4.4)

Rotational Motion Parameters

Rotational motion is described using angular quantities analogous to linear quantities in translational motion.

Linear vs Rotational Motion Comparison

Linear Motion Rotational Motion Relation
Displacement (s) Angular Displacement (θ) s = rθ
Velocity (v) Angular Velocity (ω) v = rω
Acceleration (a) Angular Acceleration (α) a_t = rα
Mass (m) Moment of Inertia (I) I = Σmr²
Force (F) Torque (τ) τ = rF sinθ
Momentum (p) Angular Momentum (L) L = rp sinθ

Equations of Rotational Motion

Angular Displacement

The angle through which an object rotates. Measured in radians.

θ = s/r

1 revolution = 2π radians = 360°

Angular Velocity

Rate of change of angular displacement. Measured in rad/s.

ω = dθ/dt

Average ω = Δθ/Δt

Angular Acceleration

Rate of change of angular velocity. Measured in rad/s².

α = dω/dt = d²θ/dt²

Average α = Δω/Δt

Kinematic Equations for Rotational Motion

ω = ω₀ + αt
θ = ω₀t + (1/2)αt²
ω² = ω₀² + 2αθ
θ = (ω₀ + ω)t/2
JEE ADVANCED

Relation between Linear and Angular Acceleration

Total acceleration in circular motion has two components:

a_t = rα (Tangential acceleration)
a_c = rω² = v²/r (Centripetal acceleration)
a_total = √(a_t² + a_c²)

Torque and Rotational Dynamics

Torque is the rotational equivalent of force. It causes angular acceleration in rigid bodies.

Torque (τ): The turning effect of a force about an axis. It is given by the cross product of position vector and force vector.

τ = r × F = rF sinθ n̂

Where:
• τ = Torque (Nm)
• r = Position vector from axis to point of force application
• F = Force applied (N)
• θ = Angle between r and F
• n̂ = Unit vector perpendicular to plane of r and F

Conditions for Rotational Equilibrium

For a body to be in rotational equilibrium, the net torque about any axis must be zero.

Στ = 0

Right Hand Rule: Point fingers in direction of r, curl toward F; thumb points in direction of torque vector.

Types of Torque

Type Description Example
Static Torque No angular acceleration (τ = 0) Balanced see-saw
Dynamic Torque Causes angular acceleration (τ = Iα) Opening a heavy door
Couple Two equal, opposite, parallel forces Turning a steering wheel
Example: Calculating Torque

A force of 20 N is applied at the edge of a wheel of radius 0.5 m. The force makes an angle of 30° with the radius vector. Calculate the torque.

Solution:

τ = rF sinθ = 0.5 × 20 × sin30° = 0.5 × 20 × 0.5 = 5 Nm

Moment of Inertia

Moment of inertia is the rotational analogue of mass. It measures how difficult it is to change the rotational motion of a body about a particular axis.

Moment of Inertia (I): For a system of particles, I = Σ m_i r_i². For continuous bodies, I = ∫ r² dm.

I = Σ m_i r_i² (Discrete system)
I = ∫ r² dm (Continuous body)

Moments of Inertia for Common Shapes

Object Axis Moment of Inertia
Thin rod (length L) Through center, perpendicular ML²/12
Thin rod (length L) Through end, perpendicular ML²/3
Ring (radius R) Through center, perpendicular MR²
Disc (radius R) Through center, perpendicular MR²/2
Solid sphere (radius R) Through center 2MR²/5
Hollow sphere (radius R) Through center 2MR²/3

Theorems on Moment of Inertia

IMPORTANT

Parallel Axis Theorem

The moment of inertia about any axis parallel to an axis through the center of mass is:

I = I_cm + Md²

Where d is the perpendicular distance between the two axes.

IMPORTANT

Perpendicular Axis Theorem

For a planar body lying in the x-y plane:

I_z = I_x + I_y

Where I_z is the moment of inertia about an axis perpendicular to the plane.

Example: Using Parallel Axis Theorem

Find the moment of inertia of a rod of mass M and length L about an axis perpendicular to the rod through one end.

Solution:

I_cm (through center) = ML²/12

Distance from center to end, d = L/2

Using parallel axis theorem: I = I_cm + Md² = ML²/12 + M(L/2)² = ML²/12 + ML²/4 = ML²/3

Angular Momentum and Conservation

Angular momentum is the rotational analogue of linear momentum. It is conserved in the absence of external torque.

Angular Momentum (L): For a particle, L = r × p. For a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis, L = Iω.

L = r × p = r × (mv)
L = Iω (For rigid body)

Conservation of Angular Momentum

If the net external torque acting on a system is zero, the total angular momentum of the system remains constant.

If τ_ext = 0, then L = constant
I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂

Applications of Angular Momentum Conservation:

  • Ice skater spinning faster when pulling arms in
  • Diving from a springboard
  • Planetary motion
  • Gyroscopes

Rotational Kinetic Energy

The kinetic energy due to rotational motion.

K_rot = (1/2)Iω²
JEE ADVANCED

Rolling Motion

For a body rolling without slipping:

v_cm = Rω
K_total = (1/2)Mv_cm² + (1/2)I_cmω²
For pure rolling: K_total = (1/2)Mv_cm²(1 + k²/R²)

Where k is radius of gyration (I = Mk²)

Example: Angular Momentum Conservation

A skater spins with arms outstretched at 2 rad/s. Her moment of inertia is 5 kg·m². She pulls her arms in, reducing moment of inertia to 2 kg·m². Find her new angular speed.

Solution:

Using conservation: I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂

5 × 2 = 2 × ω₂ ⇒ ω₂ = 10/2 = 5 rad/s

Problem Solving Strategies

Effective techniques for solving rotational motion problems in JEE and NEET.

Type 1: Torque & Equilibrium

Steps:

  1. Identify all forces and their points of application
  2. Choose a convenient pivot point
  3. Calculate torque due to each force (τ = rF sinθ)
  4. Apply Στ = 0 for equilibrium

Tip: Choose pivot at point where unknown force acts to eliminate it from equation

Type 2: Rotational Dynamics

Steps:

  1. Find moment of inertia about axis
  2. Calculate net torque
  3. Apply τ = Iα
  4. Use rotational kinematic equations if needed

Tip: Use parallel/perpendicular axis theorems for complex shapes

Type 3: Conservation Problems

Steps:

  1. Check if net external torque = 0
  2. Write angular momentum for initial state
  3. Write angular momentum for final state
  4. Set L_initial = L_final

Tip: Remember L = Iω for rigid bodies, L = r×p for particles

JEE ADVANCED TIP

Rolling Motion Problems

• For pure rolling: v = Rω and a = Rα

• Total KE = Translational KE + Rotational KE

• Friction may be static (no slipping) or kinetic (slipping)

• Use energy conservation: mgh = (1/2)mv² + (1/2)Iω²

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Moment of inertia depends on axis of rotation, not just mass
  • Torque is maximum when force is perpendicular to position vector
  • Angular momentum is conserved if net external torque is zero
  • For pure rolling, friction is static and does no work
  • Center of mass motion is unaffected by internal forces
  • Kinetic energy of rolling body = translational KE + rotational KE

Common Mistakes to Avoid:

  • Confusing torque (vector) with work (scalar) - both have Nm units
  • Applying conservation of angular momentum when external torque exists
  • Using wrong moment of inertia formula for given axis
  • Forgetting that ω in v = rω is angular speed, not frequency
  • Mixing up radius of gyration (k) with radius (R)
