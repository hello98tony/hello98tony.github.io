Units and Measurement

Class 11 Physics | NCERT Syllabus

A comprehensive guide to fundamental concepts of physical quantities, their measurement, and applications

Introduction to Physical Quantities

Physics is fundamentally an experimental science that deals with the study of matter, energy, and their interactions. To describe physical phenomena quantitatively, we need to measure various physical quantities.

Physical Quantity: Any measurable property of a material or system that can be quantified by measurement (e.g., length, mass, time, force, energy).

Types of Physical Quantities

Physical quantities are broadly classified into two categories:

  • Fundamental quantities: These are independent physical quantities that cannot be defined in terms of other quantities. They form the basis for deriving other quantities. The seven fundamental quantities in SI system are: length, mass, time, electric current, thermodynamic temperature, amount of substance, and luminous intensity.
  • Derived quantities: These are physical quantities that can be expressed in terms of fundamental quantities through mathematical relationships. Examples include speed (distance/time), force (mass × acceleration), and energy (force × distance).

Note: The distinction between fundamental and derived quantities is somewhat arbitrary and depends on the system of units being used. What is fundamental in one system may be derived in another.

Characteristics of Physical Quantities

Every physical quantity has two essential characteristics:

  • Magnitude: The numerical value representing how much of the quantity there is.
  • Unit: The standard against which the quantity is measured (e.g., meter for length, kilogram for mass).
Example:

When we say "the length of a rod is 5 meters", 5 represents the magnitude and 'meter' is the unit of length.

The International System of Units (SI)

The International System of Units (Système International d'Unités, abbreviated SI) is the modern form of the metric system and is the most widely used system of measurement in science and engineering worldwide.

Historical Development

The SI system was established in 1960 by the 11th General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM). It evolved from the earlier meter-kilogram-second (MKS) system and represents a refinement of the metric system that dates back to the French Revolution.

SI Base Units

The SI system is built upon seven base units which by convention are regarded as dimensionally independent:

Physical Quantity SI Unit Symbol Current Definition (Since 2019)
Length meter m Defined by fixing the speed of light in vacuum to be exactly 299,792,458 m/s
Mass kilogram kg Defined by fixing Planck's constant to be exactly 6.62607015×10⁻³⁴ J·s
Time second s Defined by fixing the unperturbed ground-state hyperfine transition frequency of the cesium-133 atom to be exactly 9,192,631,770 Hz
Electric current ampere A Defined by fixing the elementary charge to be exactly 1.602176634×10⁻¹⁹ C
Thermodynamic temperature kelvin K Defined by fixing Boltzmann constant to be exactly 1.380649×10⁻²³ J/K
Amount of substance mole mol Defined by fixing Avogadro constant to be exactly 6.02214076×10²³ mol⁻¹
Luminous intensity candela cd Defined by fixing luminous efficacy of 540 THz radiation to be exactly 683 lm/W

2019 Redefinition: In November 2018, the CGPM voted to redefine the SI base units in terms of fundamental physical constants, which took effect on May 20, 2019. This change means all SI units are now defined by constants of nature rather than physical artifacts.

SI Derived Units

Derived units are formed by combining base units according to the algebraic relations linking the corresponding quantities. Some important derived units include:

Energy joule J kg·m²·s⁻²
Power watt W kg·m²·s⁻³
Pressure pascal Pa kg·m⁻¹·s⁻²
Frequency hertz Hz s⁻¹
Electric charge coulomb C A·s
Electric potential volt V kg·m²·s⁻³·A⁻¹

Measurement Techniques

Precise measurement is fundamental to physics. Different instruments are used to measure different physical quantities with varying degrees of precision.

Measurement of Length

Length is one of the most fundamental physical quantities. The choice of measuring instrument depends on the required precision:

  • Meter Scale: Measures up to 1 mm precision (least count = 1 mm)
  • Vernier Calipers: Measures up to 0.1 mm precision (least count = 0.1 mm)
  • Screw Gauge (Micrometer): Measures up to 0.01 mm precision (least count = 0.01 mm)

Vernier Calipers

A vernier caliper consists of a main scale and a sliding vernier scale. The main scale is graduated in millimeters, while the vernier scale has 10 divisions that cover 9 mm on the main scale.

Least Count (LC) = Value of one main scale division - Value of one vernier scale division
Example Calculation:

If 1 main scale division (MSD) = 1 mm and 10 vernier scale divisions (VSD) = 9 mm, then:

1 VSD = 9/10 = 0.9 mm

Least Count = 1 MSD - 1 VSD = 1 mm - 0.9 mm = 0.1 mm

Screw Gauge

A screw gauge (or micrometer) uses a screw to amplify small distances that are too small to measure directly into large rotations of the screw that are big enough to read from a scale.

Least Count = Pitch / Number of circular scale divisions
Example Calculation:

If the pitch (distance moved per rotation) = 1 mm and there are 100 divisions on the circular scale:

Least Count = 1 mm / 100 = 0.01 mm

Measurement of Mass

Mass is a measure of the amount of matter in an object. Common instruments for measuring mass include:

  • Beam Balance: Uses comparison with known masses
  • Electronic Balance: Measures the force needed to counter the mass
  • Spring Balance: Measures weight which is proportional to mass

Measurement of Time

Time intervals can be measured using various devices:

  • Simple Pendulum: Can be used to measure time intervals based on its period
  • Stopwatch: Measures time intervals with precision up to 0.01 s
  • Atomic Clocks: Most precise timekeeping devices (uncertainty of 1 second in 100 million years)

Accuracy, Precision and Errors

Understanding errors and uncertainties is crucial in experimental physics as no measurement can be perfectly accurate.

Accuracy: Refers to how close a measurement is to the true or accepted value.

Precision: Refers to how close the measured values are to each other (reproducibility).

Types of Errors

Errors in measurement can be classified into three main categories:

Error Type Description Examples How to Minimize
Systematic Errors Consistent, reproducible errors due to faulty equipment or technique Zero error in instruments, incorrect calibration, parallax error Calibrate instruments, improve techniques, use proper measurement methods
Random Errors Unpredictable variations in measurements due to uncontrollable factors Fluctuations in temperature, observer's reaction time, electrical noise Take multiple readings and average, improve experimental conditions
Gross Errors Mistakes in observation or recording that lead to outliers Reading wrong scale, recording incorrect value, calculation errors Careful measurement and recording, double-check calculations

Absolute, Relative and Percentage Error

Errors can be expressed in different ways depending on the context:

Absolute Error (Δa) = |Measured Value (aₘ) - True Value (aₜ)|
Relative Error = Absolute Error / True Value = Δa / aₜ
Percentage Error = Relative Error × 100% = (Δa / aₜ) × 100%
Example Calculation:

If the true value of a length is 5.00 cm and the measured value is 5.02 cm:

Absolute Error = |5.02 - 5.00| = 0.02 cm

Relative Error = 0.02 / 5.00 = 0.004

Percentage Error = 0.004 × 100% = 0.4%

Combination of Errors

When measurements are used in calculations, the errors propagate according to specific rules:

For Addition/Subtraction

If Z = A ± B, then ΔZ = ΔA + ΔB (Absolute Errors Add)

For Multiplication/Division

If Z = A × B or A/B, then ΔZ/Z = ΔA/A + ΔB/B (Relative Errors Add)

For Power Relations

If Z = Aⁿ, then ΔZ/Z = n × (ΔA/A)

Note: These formulas assume that the errors are independent and random. When dealing with more complex functions, partial derivatives are used to determine error propagation.

Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional analysis is a powerful tool in physics that uses the dimensions of physical quantities to check equations, derive relationships, and convert between different units.

Dimensions: The powers to which the fundamental quantities (mass, length, time, etc.) must be raised to represent a given physical quantity.

Dimensional Formula

The dimensional formula expresses a physical quantity in terms of the fundamental quantities. It is written within square brackets [ ].

For example:

  • Speed = distance/time → [LT⁻¹]
  • Force = mass × acceleration → [MLT⁻²]
  • Energy = force × distance → [ML²T⁻²]

Applications of Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional analysis has several important applications in physics:

1. Checking the Correctness of Equations

Every valid physical equation must be dimensionally consistent (principle of homogeneity). This means the dimensions on both sides of the equation must be identical.

Example:

Check the dimensional consistency of the equation for time period of a simple pendulum: T = 2π√(l/g)

Left side: [T] = [T]

Right side: [√(l/g)] = [√(L/LT⁻²)] = [√(T²)] = [T]

Both sides have the same dimensions [T], so the equation is dimensionally correct.

2. Deriving Relationships Between Physical Quantities

Dimensional analysis can help derive relationships between physical quantities when the exact form is unknown.

Example:

Derive an expression for the time period (T) of a simple pendulum, assuming it depends on length (l) and acceleration due to gravity (g).

Assume T ∝ lˣgʸ → T = klˣgʸ

Dimensions: [T] = [L]ˣ[LT⁻²]ʸ = [Lˣ⁺ʸT⁻²ʸ]

Equating dimensions: x + y = 0 and -2y = 1 → y = -½, x = ½

Thus, T ∝ √(l/g)

3. Converting Units Between Systems

Dimensional analysis provides a systematic method for converting units from one system to another.

Example:

Convert 1 newton (SI unit of force) into dyne (CGS unit of force).

1 N = 1 kg·m·s⁻²

1 dyne = 1 g·cm·s⁻²

1 kg = 1000 g, 1 m = 100 cm

1 N = (1000 g)(100 cm)(s⁻²) = 10⁵ g·cm·s⁻² = 10⁵ dyne

Limitations of Dimensional Analysis:

  • Cannot determine dimensionless constants (like 2π in T = 2π√(l/g))
  • Cannot distinguish between quantities with same dimensions (e.g., work and torque both have dimensions [ML²T⁻²])
  • Cannot derive trigonometric, logarithmic, or exponential relations
  • Doesn't provide information about dimensionless quantities

Applications and Problem Solving

The concepts of units and measurement find wide applications in solving practical physics problems. Here are some important applications and problem-solving techniques.

Order of Magnitude Calculations

Order of magnitude refers to the scale or size of something in powers of ten. It's useful for making quick estimates and approximations.

If N = a × 10ⁿ where 1 ≤ a < 10, then order of magnitude is n
Example:

Estimate the number of heartbeats in a human lifetime:

Average heart rate ≈ 70 beats/min

Average lifespan ≈ 70 years

Total beats ≈ 70 beats/min × 60 min/h × 24 h/day × 365 days/year × 70 years

≈ 70 × 60 × 24 × 400 × 70 ≈ 3 × 10⁹ beats

Order of magnitude ≈ 10⁹ beats

Significant Figures in Calculations

Significant figures indicate the precision of a measurement. Rules for arithmetic operations with significant figures:

Addition and Subtraction

The result should have the same number of decimal places as the measurement with the least decimal places.

Example:

12.345 (3 decimal places) + 1.2 (1 decimal place) = 13.5 (rounded to 1 decimal place)

Multiplication and Division

The result should have the same number of significant figures as the measurement with the least significant figures.

Example:

3.1416 (5 sig figs) × 2.0 (2 sig figs) = 6.3 (rounded to 2 significant figures)

Practical Measurement Problems

Solving problems involving measurement requires careful consideration of instruments, precision, and error analysis.

Problem:

The length and width of a rectangular plate are measured to be 15.3 cm and 10.6 cm respectively with a vernier caliper of least count 0.1 cm. Calculate the area of the plate with proper significant figures and determine the percentage error in the area.

Solution:

Length (l) = 15.3 ± 0.1 cm

Width (w) = 10.6 ± 0.1 cm

Area (A) = l × w = 15.3 × 10.6 = 162.18 cm²

Since both measurements have 3 significant figures, area should be reported with 3 sig figs: A = 162 cm²

Relative error in area:

ΔA/A = Δl/l + Δw/w = 0.1/15.3 + 0.1/10.6 ≈ 0.0065 + 0.0094 ≈ 0.0159

Percentage error = 0.0159 × 100% ≈ 1.6%

LENGTH STANDARD
1 meter is defined as the distance traveled by light in vacuum in 1/299,792,458 seconds (exact value since 1983)
TIME STANDARD
1 second is defined as 9,192,631,770 periods of radiation corresponding to the transition between two hyperfine levels of cesium-133 atom
MASS STANDARD
1 kilogram is now defined in terms of Planck's constant (h = 6.62607015×10⁻³⁴ kg·m²·s⁻¹) since 2019 redefinition
DIMENSIONAL ANALYSIS
The dimension of force is [MLT⁻²] and energy is [ML²T⁻²]. Work and torque have same dimensions but different physical meanings
SIGNIFICANT FIGURES
0.00450 has 3 significant figures (the zeros before 4 are not significant, the zero after 5 is significant)
ERROR PROPAGATION
When adding measurements, absolute errors add. When multiplying, relative errors add. For powers, multiply relative error by the exponent
VERNIER CALIPERS
Least count = 1 MSD - 1 VSD. Typical least count is 0.1 mm for standard vernier calipers with 10 divisions on vernier scale
SCREW GAUGE
Least count = Pitch / Number of circular scale divisions. Typical least count is 0.01 mm for standard screw gauges

Chapter Summary

Key Concepts

  • Physics is a quantitative science based on measurement of physical quantities
  • Physical quantities have magnitude and unit, and can be fundamental or derived
  • The SI system has seven base units defined by fundamental constants
  • Measurement instruments have specific ranges and precisions (least count)
  • All measurements have errors that can be systematic or random
  • Dimensional analysis helps check equations, derive relations, and convert units
  • Significant figures indicate the precision of measurements

Important Formulas

Vernier Calipers: Total Reading = MSR + (VSD × LC) where LC = 1 MSD - 1 VSD
Screw Gauge: Total Reading = PSR + (CSD × LC) where LC = Pitch / No. of CSD
Absolute Error: Δa = |aₘ - aₜ|
Relative Error: Δa/aₜ
Percentage Error: (Δa/aₜ) × 100%
Error Propagation: For Z = A ± B → ΔZ = ΔA + ΔB; For Z = A × B or A/B → ΔZ/Z = ΔA/A + ΔB/B
