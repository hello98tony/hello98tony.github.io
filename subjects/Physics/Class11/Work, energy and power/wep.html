Introduction to Work, Energy & Power

Work, energy, and power are interconnected concepts that describe how forces act on objects to produce motion and transformation of energy forms. These concepts form the foundation for understanding conservation laws in physics.

Key Connection: Work is the process of transferring energy from one system to another, and power is the rate at which this work is done or energy is transferred.

Work

Work is done when a force causes displacement of an object in the direction of the force. It's a scalar quantity measured in joules (J).

W = F·s·cosθ
Energy

Energy is the capacity to do work. It exists in various forms and can be transformed from one form to another.

E = mc² (Mass-Energy)
Power

Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred. It's measured in watts (W).

P = W/t = F·v

Work in Physics

In physics, work has a specific meaning that differs from everyday usage. Work is done only when a force causes displacement in its own direction.

Work: If the force and the displacement acting on a body are in different directions then the work done is the product of the component of the force in the direction of displacement. i.e.,

W = F·s·cosθ

Where:
• W = Work done (Joules)
• F = Magnitude of force (Newtons)
• s = Displacement (meters)
• θ = Angle between force and displacement vectors

Special Cases of Work

Condition Angle (θ) Work Done Example
Force in direction of displacement 0° W = F·s (Maximum positive work) Pushing a cart forward
Force perpendicular to displacement 90° W = 0 (No work) Carrying a load horizontally
Force opposite to displacement 180° W = -F·s (Negative work) Friction opposing motion

Work done by Variable Force

A variable force is a force whose magnitude changes as it acts on an object.
This causes work done by it to change gradually. Thus the work done by this kind of force is the integral of the force with respect to the displacement of the object i.e.,

W = ∫F·dx

or the area under Force and displacement graph.
In other words, it is the sum of the work done by the force over each infinitesimal (very very very small) displacement of the object as demonstrated in the following diagram

Here W = ∫F·dx represents the sum of all the tiny rectangles formed in this graph. This provides us the required work done due to the force acting on a body.

Spring Force (Fs): Spring force is the force a spring exerts when it is either stretched or compressed. It always acts to bring the spring back to its natural length.

Fs = -kx
Conservative and Non-conservative Forces

Conservative Force: A conservative force is a force whose work depends only on the initial and final positions, not on the path taken.

Key Points:
Properties of Conservative Force:

  • Total energy is conserved
  • Work done in a loop is zero
  • Independent of path

Non-conservative Force: A Non-conservative force is a force whose work depends on the path taken.

Key Points:
Properties of Conservative Force:

  • Total energy is not conserved
  • Work done in a loop is not zero
  • Depends on path

Important Points:

  • Work is a scalar quantity but can be positive, negative, or zero(these signs only tell us about the relationship between Force and Displacement not about the direction of the work done)
  • Work done by a variable force: W = ∫F·ds (area under F-s graph)
  • Work done by spring force: W = -(1/2)kx² since W = ∫F·ds and F = -kx
Example:

When a 600g mass is hung from a vertical spring it strtches from equilibrium by 20cm. What work is required to stretch it by next 10cm?

Solution:

m = 600g = 0.6kg
x = 20cm = 0.2m
At equilibrium,

mg = kx

k = mg/x

k = 0.6 ✗ 9.8/ 0.2 = 5.88/0.2

k = 147N/m

Work done by a spring (W) = 1/2 k(x22 - x21)

W = 1/2 ✗ 147(0.32 - 0.22)

W = 73.5(0.09 - 0.04)

W = 73.5 ✗ 0.05

W = 3.675J

Energy and its Forms

Energy is a fundamental concept in physics that describes the capacity to do work. According to the law of conservation of energy, energy can neither be created nor destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.

Types of Mechanical Energy

Kinetic Energy:The energy possessed by an object due to its motion.

K = (1/2)mv²
Relation between Kinetic Energy (K) and Linear Momentum (p)

p = mv
and K = 1/2 mv2
If we multiply both numerator and denominator by m we get;
K = mv2 ✗ m/ 2m
2mK = m2v2
or,

2mK = p2

Potential Energy (U): It is the energy possessed by an object due to its position or configuration.

UGravitational = mgh
UElastic/Spring = 1/2 kx2
Potential Energy of an Ideal Spring

Fs ∝ -x
Fs = -kx
Ws = -∫kxdx = -k[x2/2] + c
On introducing limits from xi to xf we get;

Ws = Us = -1/2 kxf2

Energy Conservation

The principle of conservation of mechanical energy states that in the absence of non-conservative forces (like friction), the total mechanical energy remains constant.

Etotal = K + U = constant
K₁ + U₁ = K₂ + U₂
Example

A ball of mass 0.5 kg is dropped from a height of 20 m. Calculate its kinetic energy just before hitting the ground. (Take g = 10 m/s²)

Solution:

Using conservation of energy:

At height h: U = mgh, K = 0

Just before hitting: U = 0, K = ?

mgh = K ⇒ K = 0.5 × 10 × 20 = 100 J

Other Forms of Energy

Energy Type Description Formula/Relation

Chemical Energy Energy stored in chemical bonds Released in reactions
Electrical Energy Energy due to electric charges E = VIt
Nuclear Energy Energy stored in atomic nuclei E = Δmc²

Power and its Applications

Power measures how quickly work is done or energy is transferred. It's a crucial concept in engineering and practical applications.

Power: The time rate at which work is done or energy is transferred. It's a scalar quantity measured in watts (W = J/s).

Average Power: Pavg = W/Δt
Instantaneous Power: P = dW/dt = F·v

Power in Different Contexts

Mechanical Power

Power in mechanical systems: P = F·v, where v is velocity in direction of force.

Example: An engine exerting 500 N force on a car moving at 20 m/s produces power: P = 500 × 20 = 10,000 W = 10 kW

Electrical Power

Power in electrical circuits: P = VI = I²R = V²/R

Example: A 100W bulb operating at 220V draws current: I = P/V = 100/220 ≈ 0.45 A

Efficiency (η): The ratio of useful power output to power input, expressed as percentage.

η = (Poutput / Pinput) × 100%

Variable Power Problems

For variable power: P(t) = dW/dt. Work done = ∫P(t)dt

For vehicles with constant power: P = Fv = constant ⇒ F ∝ 1/v

Acceleration: a = (P/mv) - (F_resistance/m)

Important Theorems and Principles

MOST IMPORTANT

Work-Energy Theorem

The net work done by all forces acting on a particle equals the change in its kinetic energy.

W_net = ΔK = K_final - K_initial = (1/2)mv² - (1/2)mu²

Key Points:

  • Applies to both constant and variable forces
  • Valid for systems of particles
  • Useful for solving problems without knowing time

Conservative and Non-Conservative Forces

Type Definition Work Done Examples
Conservative Forces Work done is path-independent; depends only on endpoints Reversible; total work in closed path = 0 Gravity, Spring force, Electrostatic force
Non-Conservative Forces Work done depends on path taken Irreversible; total work in closed path ≠ 0 Friction, Air resistance, Viscous force

Conservation of Mechanical Energy

In the presence of only conservative forces, the total mechanical energy (K + U) remains constant.

ΔK + ΔU = 0 or K₁ + U₁ = K₂ + U₂
Proof of Conservation:

From work-energy theorem: W_net = ΔK

For conservative forces: W_cons = -ΔU

If only conservative forces: W_net = W_cons

Therefore: -ΔU = ΔK ⇒ ΔK + ΔU = 0 ⇒ K + U = constant

Problem Solving Strategies

Effective problem-solving techniques for JEE and NEET questions on work, energy, and power.

Type 1: Work Calculation

Steps:

  1. Identify all forces acting
  2. Find displacement vector
  3. Calculate W = F·s·cosθ for each force
  4. Sum work done by all forces

Tip: For variable force, use integration or area under F-x graph

Type 2: Energy Conservation

Steps:

  1. Check if only conservative forces act
  2. Identify initial and final states
  3. Write K₁ + U₁ = K₂ + U₂
  4. Solve for unknown

Tip: Include all forms of potential energy (gravity, spring, etc.)

Type 3: Power Problems

Steps:

  1. Determine if average or instantaneous power
  2. Use P = W/t or P = F·v
  3. For variable power, use integration
  4. Check units carefully

Tip: For vehicles, P = Fv with F = ma + F_resistance

Graphical Methods

• Work = Area under F-x graph

• Power = Area under P-t graph = Work done

• For spring: W = Area of triangle in F-x graph = (1/2)kx²

WORK DEFINITION
Work is done only when component of force is in direction of displacement. W = F·s·cosθ
KINETIC ENERGY
K = (1/2)mv². Kinetic energy is always positive and depends on frame of reference
POTENTIAL ENERGY
U = mgh (gravity), U = (1/2)kx² (spring). Potential energy is relative to reference point
WORK-ENERGY THEOREM
Net work done = Change in kinetic energy. Valid for all forces (conservative & non-conservative)
POWER FORMULAS
P_avg = W/t, P_inst = F·v. For constant power, force varies inversely with velocity
CONSERVATIVE FORCES
Work done by conservative forces is path-independent. Examples: Gravity, spring force, electrostatic force
ENERGY CONSERVATION
When only conservative forces act: K₁ + U₁ = K₂ + U₂. Mechanical energy remains constant
SPRING WORK
Work done by spring: W = -(1/2)kx² (negative as it opposes displacement). Force F = -kx

Important Points for JEE/NEET

  • Work done by friction is always negative (except pure rolling)
  • Work done by normal force is always zero
  • Kinetic energy is frame-dependent; choose the simplest frame
  • For springs, work done depends only on initial and final compression
  • Power is maximum when force and velocity are in same direction
  • Efficiency is always less than 100% due to energy losses

Common Pitfalls to Avoid:

  • Confusing work (scalar) with force (vector)
  • Forgetting that work can be negative
  • Applying energy conservation when non-conservative forces are present
  • Using wrong reference point for potential energy
  • Mixing up average and instantaneous power
